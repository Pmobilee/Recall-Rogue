## UNIT 1: Thinking Geographically (8–10%)
**7 Topics**

### 1.1 Introduction to Maps
- PSO-1.A.1: Maps are representations of the world. Different types of maps present specific information about the world.
- PSO-1.A.2: Maps reflect the perspective of the mapmaker. Maps have specific characteristics, including a title, scale, north arrow, legend, date, and projection.
- PSO-1.A.3: There are several types of map projections, each with a specific purpose and limitations. No map projection is perfect.

### 1.2 Geographic Data
- PSO-1.B.1: Geographers use quantitative (including GIS) and qualitative geospatial technologies to acquire, manage, and visualize geographic data.
- PSO-1.B.2: Spatial data is collected from primary (field observations, interviews, surveys, etc.) and secondary sources (satellite images, aerial photographs, etc.).
- PSO-1.B.3: Geographic information systems (GIS) use multiple layers of data to create comprehensive maps. Remote sensing collects data from a distance.

### 1.3 The Power of Geographic Data
- IMP-1.A.1: Spatial data presented in maps and other visual formats can be used to explain patterns and trends and make predictions.

### 1.4 Spatial Concepts
- PSO-1.C.1: Location can be identified using absolute location (latitude/longitude) and relative location (position relative to other places).
- PSO-1.C.2: Absolute and relative distance describe the space between two places. Absolute distance uses standard units of measure; relative distance uses social, cultural, or temporal measures.
- PSO-1.C.3: Spatial interaction is a product of absolute and relative distance. Time-space compression is the reduction in time it takes to diffuse something to a distant place due to advances in communications and transportation technology.
- PSO-1.C.4: Distance decay states that the interaction between two places declines as the distance between them increases.

### 1.5 Human-Environmental Interaction
- PSO-1.D.1: Environmental determinism: the view that natural environment controls human development, so that humans are products of their environment.
- PSO-1.D.2: Possibilism: the view that the physical environment may limit some human actions, but people have the ability to adjust to the environment.

### 1.6 Scales of Analysis
- IMP-1.B.1: Geographic patterns and processes can be analyzed at different scales: global, regional, national, and local. Using the same data at different scales can reveal different patterns and lead to different conclusions.

### 1.7 Regional Analysis
- PSO-1.E.1: A region is a human construct defined by one or more unique characteristics. Formal (uniform) regions have one or more shared traits. Functional (nodal) regions are organized around a focal point. Perceptual (vernacular) regions are defined by people's beliefs or feelings.
- PSO-1.E.2: Regions can overlap, and people may have different views of regional boundaries.

## UNIT 2: Population and Migration Patterns and Processes (12–17%)
**12 Topics**

### 2.1 Population Distribution
- PSO-2.A.1: Geographers use population distributions and densities to explain patterns of population.
- PSO-2.A.2: Major population clusters are found in East Asia, South Asia, Southeast Asia, and Western Europe. Sparsely populated regions include deserts, high-altitude regions, and rainforests.

### 2.2 Consequences of Population Distribution
- PSO-2.B.1: Arithmetic density (total population / total land area), physiological density (total population / arable land), and agricultural density (farmers / arable land) are used to understand the relationship between humans and the environment.

### 2.3 Population Composition
- IMP-2.A.1: Population pyramids are used to understand the age-sex composition of a population and predict future trends.
- IMP-2.A.2: Dependency ratio compares the non-working population to the working-age population.

### 2.4 Population Dynamics
- IMP-2.B.1: Crude birth rate (CBR), crude death rate (CDR), and the total fertility rate (TFR) are used to analyze population change.
- IMP-2.B.2: Natural increase rate (NIR) is calculated by subtracting CDR from CBR and represents growth (or decline) without migration.
- IMP-2.B.3: Doubling time is the number of years it takes a population to double at its current rate of growth.

### 2.5 The Demographic Transition Model (DTM)
- IMP-2.C.1: The DTM is a 5-stage model that shows how population changes through time, as crude birth rates and crude death rates change.
- IMP-2.C.2: Stage 1: high birth rate, high death rate, low growth. Stage 2: death rate declines, high birth rate, population grows rapidly. Stage 3: birth rate declines, slowing growth. Stage 4: low birth rate, low death rate, low growth. Stage 5: birth rate below death rate, population declines.

### 2.6 Malthusian Theory
- IMP-2.D.1: Thomas Malthus argued that population growth would outstrip food supply, leading to catastrophe. Neo-Malthusians apply this to modern resource limits.
- IMP-2.D.2: Critics of Malthus argue that technology and social change (e.g., improved agriculture, birth control) prevent the Malthusian catastrophe.

### 2.7 Population Policies
- SPS-2.A.1: Countries that favor increased population have pro-natalist policies. Countries that favor reduced population have anti-natalist policies.
- SPS-2.A.2: Population policies may have social, environmental, and political effects.

### 2.8 Women and Demographic Change
- SPS-2.B.1: Improvements in education and the status of women have led to declining fertility rates in many parts of the world.
- SPS-2.B.2: The epidemiological transition describes how the causes of death shift from infectious to chronic diseases as a country develops.

### 2.9 Epidemiological Transition
- IMP-2.E.1: The epidemiological transition describes changes in the causes of death in a population as it develops economically. Disease patterns shift from infectious/parasitic to chronic/degenerative diseases.

### 2.10 Causes of Migration
- SPS-2.C.1: Push-and-pull factors — economic, cultural, environmental — explain why people migrate.
- SPS-2.C.2: Forced migrations include slavery, conflict-driven displacement, and environmental disasters. Voluntary migrations are driven by economic opportunity or family connections.
- SPS-2.C.3: Ravenstein's laws of migration describe spatial patterns of migration, including that most migrants move short distances, migration occurs in steps, and streams of migrants follow corridors.
- SPS-2.C.4: Migration occurs at different scales: international (immigration/emigration), internal, rural-to-urban, transhumance, and step migration.

### 2.11 Forced and Voluntary Migration
- SPS-2.D.1: Refugees, internally displaced persons (IDPs), and asylum seekers are forced migrants. Transnational and chain migration are forms of voluntary migration.

### 2.12 Effects of Migration
- SPS-2.E.1: Migration has social, economic, and cultural effects on the origin and destination countries, including remittances, brain drain, and changing population composition.
- SPS-2.E.2: The gravity model describes patterns of migration based on population size and distance between places.

## UNIT 3: Cultural Patterns and Processes (12–17%)
**8 Topics**

### 3.1 Introduction to Culture
- PSO-3.A.1: Culture is the body of customary beliefs, social forms, and material traits that together constitute the distinct tradition of a group of people.
- PSO-3.A.2: Cultural traits include food preferences, architecture, land use, religious practices, and language.
- PSO-3.A.3: Culture is reflected in the cultural landscape (built environment). Sequent occupance describes how successive groups of people leave their mark on the landscape.

### 3.2 Cultural Landscapes
- PSO-3.B.1: Attitudes toward the environment are reflected in cultural landscapes. Architecture, land use, and place names all reflect cultural values.
- PSO-3.B.2: Cultural relativism is the practice of assessing a culture by its own standards. Ethnocentrism is the practice of judging another culture by the standards of one's own.

### 3.3 Types of Diffusion
- PSO-3.C.1: Cultural diffusion is the process by which cultures spread. Types include relocation diffusion and expansion diffusion (contagious, hierarchical, and stimulus).
- PSO-3.C.2: Contagious diffusion spreads from a focal point to adjacent areas. Hierarchical diffusion spreads from top to bottom (elite to masses or urban to rural). Stimulus diffusion occurs when the underlying idea spreads but not the specific trait.
- PSO-3.C.3: Barriers to diffusion include physical barriers (mountains, oceans) and cultural barriers (religious taboos, political isolation).

### 3.4 Historical Causes of Diffusion
- IMP-3.A.1: Colonialism, imperialism, and trade routes spread cultural practices across the world.
- IMP-3.A.2: The printing press, mass media, and digital communications have accelerated the diffusion of cultural traits.

### 3.5 Contemporary Causes of Diffusion
- IMP-3.B.1: Cultural diffusion occurs through the spread of popular culture (music, film, food, fashion) facilitated by globalization and mass communication.
- IMP-3.B.2: The expansion of global communication networks has accelerated the spread of norms and values.

### 3.6 Diffusion of Religion and Language
- PSO-3.D.1: Universalizing religions (Christianity, Islam, Buddhism, Sikhism) actively seek converts and spread through missionary work. Ethnic religions (Hinduism, Judaism, indigenous religions) are closely tied to particular groups and locations.
- PSO-3.D.2: The origin and diffusion of major world religions can be traced to specific hearths. Lingua franca is a language widely used among speakers of different native languages (e.g., English, Swahili). Creolization is the blending of languages.
- PSO-3.D.3: Language families include Indo-European (largest), Sino-Tibetan, and Afro-Asiatic. Dialects are regional variations of a language.

### 3.7 Effects of Diffusion
- IMP-3.C.1: Cultural convergence occurs when two cultures grow more alike. Cultural divergence occurs when they grow more different.
- IMP-3.C.2: Acculturation occurs when one group adopts the traits of another. Assimilation occurs when a minority group fully adopts the culture of the majority. Syncretism is the blending of two cultures to form a new one.
- IMP-3.C.3: Multiculturalism is the coexistence of multiple cultures in a single area. Nativism/nationalism can resist cultural diffusion.

### 3.8 Effects of Diffusion (continued)
- SPS-3.A.1: Globalization leads to increased cultural interaction. This can result in cultural homogenization (McDonaldization) or resistance (cultural nationalism).

## UNIT 4: Political Patterns and Processes (12–17%)
**10 Topics**

### 4.1 Introduction to Political Geography
- PSO-4.A.1: Political geography examines the relationship between political processes and geographic space.
- PSO-4.A.2: States are defined by a permanent population, a defined territory, a government, and international recognition. Nation-states have one nationality. Stateless nations lack a state (e.g., Kurds). Multinational states include multiple nationalities (e.g., India, Russia).
- PSO-4.A.3: Sovereignty refers to the ability of a state to govern itself. Self-determination is the principle that nations have the right to govern themselves.

### 4.2 Political Power and Territoriality
- PSO-4.B.1: Colonialism is the political, economic, and cultural domination of a territory and its people by a foreign power. Neocolonialism refers to economic domination without formal political control.
- PSO-4.B.2: Territoriality is the connection of people to their territory. Shatterbelts are regions of persistent conflict resulting from their position between competing powerful states. Choke points are strategic narrow passages (straits, canals) that give political power.

### 4.3 Colonialism and Imperialism
- PSO-4.C.1: Colonialism and imperialism spread European political and cultural control over much of the world. The Berlin Conference (1884–85) divided Africa among European powers.
- PSO-4.C.2: Colonialism left lasting borders that often do not match ethnic or cultural boundaries, causing ongoing conflict.

### 4.4 Shapes of States
- PSO-4.D.1: The shape of a state (compact, prorupt, elongated, fragmented, perforated) influences its ability to govern effectively.
- PSO-4.D.2: Compact states (e.g., Kenya) are easy to govern. Elongated states (e.g., Chile) are difficult. Prorupt states (e.g., Thailand) extend into neighboring areas. Fragmented states (e.g., Indonesia, Philippines) consist of islands. Perforated states (e.g., South Africa with Lesotho) surround another state.

### 4.5 Internal Boundaries
- PSO-4.E.1: Boundaries can be defined, delimited, demarcated, and administered. Relic boundaries are former political borders. Superimposed boundaries are drawn by outsiders ignoring existing cultural patterns.
- PSO-4.E.2: Antecedent boundaries existed before settlement. Subsequent boundaries evolved with cultural patterns. Consequent boundaries reflect cultural divisions. Geometric boundaries follow lines of latitude/longitude.
- PSO-4.E.3: Boundaries can cause conflict when they divide ethnic groups, create resource access issues, or are contested (e.g., maritime boundaries under UNCLOS/EEZ).

### 4.6 Internal Organization of States
- PSO-4.F.1: Unitary states give most power to the central government. Federal states distribute power between national and state/provincial governments.
- PSO-4.F.2: Gerrymandering is the manipulation of electoral district boundaries to give one political party an advantage.

### 4.7 Forms of Governance
- PSO-4.G.1: Autocratic, oligarchic, and democratic governments have different characteristics regarding who holds power.
- PSO-4.G.2: International organizations such as the UN, NATO, EU, African Union, and ASEAN promote cooperation and sometimes constrain state sovereignty.

### 4.8 Defining Devolutionary Forces
- IMP-4.A.1: Devolution is the process by which central governments give power to regional or local governments. Centrifugal forces pull a state apart (ethnic divisions, geographic isolation). Centripetal forces hold a state together (shared culture, national identity).
- IMP-4.A.2: Ethnic, economic, and physical factors can lead to separatism and devolution.

### 4.9 Challenges to Sovereignty
- IMP-4.B.1: Supranational organizations (EU, UN, WTO) can limit state sovereignty. Terrorism, ethnic conflict, and separatist movements are challenges to state sovereignty.
- IMP-4.B.2: Technology and globalization challenge traditional notions of sovereignty.

### 4.10 Consequences of Centrifugal and Centripetal Forces
- SPS-4.A.1: The degree to which centrifugal or centripetal forces dominate in a state influences the balance of power and stability.
- SPS-4.A.2: Ethnic cleansing, genocide, and civil war can result from extreme centrifugal forces. Federal systems and power-sharing arrangements are centripetal responses.

## UNIT 5: Agriculture and Rural Land-Use Patterns and Processes (12–17%)
**12 Topics**

### 5.1 Introduction to Agriculture
- PSO-5.A.1: Intensive vs. extensive agriculture: intensive farming involves greater inputs per unit of land (market gardening, plantation, mixed crop-livestock). Extensive farming involves large land areas per output (shifting cultivation, nomadic herding, ranching).
- PSO-5.A.2: Climate and physical geography influence the type of agriculture practiced in a region.

### 5.2 Settlement Patterns and Survey Methods
- PSO-5.B.1: Rural settlement patterns include clustered (nucleated), dispersed, and linear forms.
- PSO-5.B.2: Land survey methods include metes and bounds, township and range (rectangular survey), and long-lot (French Canadian) systems. These create different patterns visible on the landscape.

### 5.3 Agricultural Origins
- SPS-5.A.1: The origin of agriculture traces to multiple hearths: the Fertile Crescent (Southwest Asia), the Indus River Valley, Southeast Asia, and Central America.
- SPS-5.A.2: The Columbian Exchange — the transfer of plants, animals, diseases, and culture between the Old World and New World after 1492 — transformed agriculture globally.

### 5.4 Diffusion of Agriculture
- SPS-5.B.1: The First Agricultural Revolution is the domestication of plants and animals. The Second Agricultural Revolution introduced new technologies (crop rotation, selective breeding) to increase food production.
- SPS-5.B.2: Agricultural practices and products have diffused through trade, migration, and colonialism.

### 5.5 The Green Revolution
- SPS-5.D.1: The Green Revolution was characterized in agriculture by the use of high-yield seeds, increased use of chemicals, and mechanized farming.
- SPS-5.D.2: The Green Revolution had positive (increased food supply) and negative (environmental degradation, inequality) consequences for both human populations and the environment.

### 5.6 Agricultural Production Regions
- PSO-5.C.1: Agricultural production regions are defined by the extent to which they reflect subsistence or commercial practices (monocropping or monoculture).
- PSO-5.C.2: Intensive and extensive farming practices are determined in part by land costs (bid-rent theory).

### 5.7 Spatial Organization of Agriculture
- PSO-5.C.3: Large-scale commercial agricultural operations are replacing small family farms.
- PSO-5.C.4: Complex commodity chains link production and consumption of agricultural products.
- PSO-5.C.5: Technology has increased economies of scale in the agricultural sector and the carrying capacity of the land.

### 5.8 Von Thunen Model
- PSO-5.D.1: Von Thunen's model helps to explain rural land use by emphasizing the importance of transportation costs associated with distance from the market; however, regions of specialty farming do not always conform to von Thunen's concentric rings.

### 5.9 The Global System of Agriculture
- PSO-5.E.1: Food and other agricultural products are part of a global supply chain.
- PSO-5.E.2: Some countries have become highly dependent on one or more export commodities.
- PSO-5.E.3: The main elements of global food distribution networks are affected by political relationships, infrastructure, and patterns of world trade.

### 5.10 Consequences of Agricultural Practices
- IMP-5.A.1: Environmental effects of agricultural land use include pollution, land cover change, desertification, soil salinization, and conservation efforts.
- IMP-5.A.2: Agricultural practices — including slash and burn, terraces, irrigation, deforestation, draining wetlands, shifting cultivation, and pastoral nomadism — alter the landscape.
- IMP-5.A.3: Societal effects of agricultural practices include changing diets, role of women in agricultural production, and economic purpose.

### 5.11 Challenges of Contemporary Agriculture
- IMP-5.B.1: Agricultural innovations such as biotechnology, genetically modified organisms (GMOs), and aquaculture have been accompanied by debates over sustainability, soil and water usage, reductions in biodiversity, and extensive fertilizer and pesticide use.
- IMP-5.B.2: Patterns of food production and consumption are influenced by movements such as urban farming, community-supported agriculture (CSA), organic farming, value-added specialty crops, fair trade, local-food movements, and dietary shifts.
- IMP-5.B.3: Challenges of feeding a global population include food insecurity, food deserts, problems with distribution, adverse weather, and land use lost to suburbanization.
- IMP-5.B.4: The location of food-processing facilities and markets, economies of scale, distribution systems, and government policies all have economic effects on food-production practices.

### 5.12 Women in Agriculture
- IMP-5.C.1: The role of females in food production, distribution, and consumption varies in many places depending on the type of production involved.

## UNIT 6: Cities and Urban Land-Use Patterns and Processes (12–17%)
**11 Topics**

### 6.1 The Origin and Influences of Urbanization
- PSO-6.A.1: Site and situation influence the origin, function, and growth of cities.
- PSO-6.A.2: Changes in transportation and communication, population growth, migration, economic development, and government policies influence urbanization.

### 6.2 Cities Across the World
- PSO-6.A.3: Megacities and metacities are distinct spatial outcomes of urbanization increasingly located in countries of the periphery and semiperiphery.
- PSO-6.A.4: Processes of suburbanization, sprawl, and decentralization have created new land-use forms — including edge cities, exurbs, and boomburbs — and new challenges.

### 6.3 Cities and Globalization
- PSO-6.B.1: World cities function at the top of the world's urban hierarchy and drive globalization.
- PSO-6.B.2: Cities are connected globally by networks and linkages and mediate global processes.

### 6.4 The Size and Distribution of Cities
- PSO-6.C.1: Principles useful for explaining the distribution and size of cities include rank-size rule, the primate city, gravity model, and Christaller's central place theory.

### 6.5 The Internal Structure of Cities
- PSO-6.D.1: Models and theories useful for explaining internal structures of cities include the Burgess concentric-zone model, the Hoyt sector model, the Harris and Ullman multiple-nuclei model, the galactic city model, bid-rent theory, and urban models drawn from Latin America, Southeast Asia, and Africa.

### 6.6 Density and Land Use
- IMP-6.A.1: Residential buildings and patterns of land use reflect and shape the city's culture, technological capabilities, cycles of development, and infilling.

### 6.7 Infrastructure
- IMP-6.B.1: The location and quality of a city's infrastructure directly affects its spatial patterns of economic and social development.

### 6.8 Urban Sustainability
- IMP-6.C.1: Sustainable design initiatives and zoning practices include mixed land use, walkability, transportation-oriented development, and smart-growth policies, including New Urbanism, greenbelts, and slow-growth cities.
- IMP-6.D.1: Praise for urban design initiatives includes reduction of sprawl, improved walkability and transportation, improved and diverse housing options, improved livability and promotion of sustainable options. Criticisms include increased housing costs, possible de facto segregation, and the potential loss of historical or place character.

### 6.9 Urban Data
- IMP-6.E.1: Quantitative data from census and survey data provide information about changes in population composition and size in urban areas.
- IMP-6.E.2: Qualitative data from field studies and narratives provide information about individual attitudes toward urban change.

### 6.10 Challenges of Urban Changes
- SPS-6.A.1: As urban populations move within a city, economic and social challenges result, including: issues related to housing and housing discrimination such as redlining, blockbusting, and affordability; access to services; rising crime; environmental injustice; and the growth of disamenity zones or zones of abandonment.
- SPS-6.A.2: Squatter settlements and conflicts over land tenure within large cities have increased.
- SPS-6.A.3: Responses to economic and social challenges in urban areas can include inclusionary zoning and local food movements.
- SPS-6.A.4: Urban renewal and gentrification have both positive and negative consequences.
- SPS-6.A.5: Functional and geographic fragmentation of governments — the way government agencies and institutions are dispersed between state, county, city, and neighborhood levels — presents challenges in addressing urban issues.

### 6.11 Challenges of Urban Sustainability
- SPS-6.B.1: Challenges to urban sustainability include suburban sprawl, sanitation, climate change, air and water quality, the large ecological footprint of cities, and energy use.
- SPS-6.B.2: Responses to urban sustainability challenges can include regional planning efforts, remediation and redevelopment of brownfields, establishment of urban growth boundaries, and farmland protection policies.

## UNIT 7: Industrial and Economic Development Patterns and Processes (12–17%)
**8 Topics**

### 7.1 The Industrial Revolution
- SPS-7.A.1: Industrialization began as a result of new technologies and was facilitated by the availability of natural resources.
- SPS-7.A.2: As industrialization spread, it caused food supplies to increase and populations to grow; it allowed workers to seek new industrial jobs in the cities and changed class structures.
- SPS-7.A.3: Investors in industry sought out more raw materials and new markets, a factor that contributed to the rise of colonialism and imperialism.

### 7.2 Economic Sectors and Patterns
- SPS-7.B.1: The different economic sectors — including primary, secondary, tertiary, quaternary, and quinary — are characterized by distinct development patterns.
- SPS-7.B.2: Labor, transportation (including shipping containers), the break-of-bulk point, least cost theory, markets, and resources influence the location of manufacturing such as core, semiperiphery, and periphery locations.

### 7.3 Measures of Development
- SPS-7.C.1: Measures of social and economic development include Gross Domestic Product (GDP); Gross National Product (GNP); Gross National Income (GNI) per capita; sectoral structure of an economy, both formal and informal; income distribution; fertility rates; infant mortality rates; access to health care; use of fossil fuels and renewable energy; and literacy rates.
- SPS-7.C.2: Measures of gender inequality, such as the Gender Inequality Index (GII), include reproductive health, indices of empowerment, and labor-market participation.
- SPS-7.C.3: The Human Development Index (HDI) is a composite measure used to show spatial variation among states in levels of development.

### 7.4 Women and Economic Development
- SPS-7.D.1: The roles of women change as countries develop economically.
- SPS-7.D.2: Although there are more women in the workforce, they do not have equity in wages or employment opportunities.
- SPS-7.D.3: Microloans have provided opportunities for women to create small local businesses, which have improved standards of living.

### 7.5 Theories of Development
- SPS-7.E.1: Different theories, such as Rostow's Stages of Economic Growth, Wallerstein's World System Theory, dependency theory, and commodity dependence, help explain spatial variations in development.

### 7.6 Trade and the World Economy
- PSO-7.A.1: Complementarity and comparative advantage establish the basis for trade.
- PSO-7.A.2: Neoliberal policies, including free trade agreements, have created new organizations, spatial connections, and trade relationships, such as the EU, World Trade Organization (WTO), Mercosur, and OPEC, that foster greater globalization.
- PSO-7.A.3: Government initiatives at all scales may affect economic development, including tariffs.
- PSO-7.A.4: Global financial crises (e.g., debt crises), international lending agencies (e.g., the International Monetary Fund), and strategies of development (e.g., microlending) demonstrate how different economies have become more closely connected.

### 7.7 Changes as a Result of the World Economy
- PSO-7.A.5: Outsourcing and economic restructuring have led to a decline in jobs in core regions and an increase in jobs in newly industrialized countries.
- PSO-7.A.6: In countries outside the core, the growth of industry has resulted in the creation of new manufacturing zones — including special economic zones, free-trade zones, and export-processing zones — and the emergence of an international division of labor in which developing countries have lower-paying jobs.
- PSO-7.A.7: The contemporary economic landscape has been transformed by post-Fordist methods of production, multiplier effects, economies of scale, agglomeration, just-in-time delivery, the emergence of service sectors, high technology industries, and growth poles.

### 7.8 Sustainable Development
- IMP-7.A.1: Sustainable development policies attempt to remedy problems stemming from natural-resource depletion, mass consumption, the effects of pollution, and the impact of climate change.
- IMP-7.A.2: Ecotourism is tourism based in natural environments — often environments that are threatened by looming industrialization or development — that frequently helps to protect the environment while also providing jobs for the local population.
- IMP-7.A.3: The UN's Sustainable Development Goals help measure progress in development, such as small-scale finance and public transportation projects.
