# Judaism — Key Verified Facts for Deck Generation
# Sourced from Wikipedia, 2026-04-08

## Patriarchs & Founders

### Abraham
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abraham
- First Hebrew patriarch; founding father of Judaism
- Called by God to leave his father's house and settle in Canaan
- Began covenantal relationship between Jewish people and God
- Also sacred in Christianity and Islam (Ibrahim)
- His son Isaac (by Sarah) continues Jewish lineage; Ishmael through Hagar → Arab peoples
- Cave of the Patriarchs at Hebron = traditional burial site

### Jacob / Israel
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacob
- Son of Isaac and Rebecca; grandson of Abraham
- Later given name "Israel" (after wrestling with God/angel)
- Had 12 sons → 12 Tribes of Israel
- Migrated to Egypt with family during drought

### Moses
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moses
- Most important prophet in Judaism
- Led Israelites out of slavery in Egypt (the Exodus)
- Received Ten Commandments at Mount Sinai
- Born to Hebrew mother Jochebed, placed in basket on Nile; adopted by Pharaoh's daughter
- God spoke to him from burning bush on Mount Horeb
- Died on Mount Nebo at age 120, within sight of Promised Land
- Also prophet in Christianity and Islam (Musa)
- Traditional dates: ~1391-1271 BCE (Rabbinic), ~13th cent. BCE (scholarly)

## Sacred Texts

### Torah
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torah
- First five books of Hebrew Bible: Genesis, Exodus, Leviticus, Numbers, Deuteronomy
- Also known as Pentateuch / Five Books of Moses
- Hebrew word means "instruction," "teaching," or "law"
- In scroll form = Sefer Torah; in bound book form = Chumash

### Tanakh
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tanakh (inferred from arch)
- Hebrew Bible; acronym: Torah + Nevi'im (Prophets) + Ketuvim (Writings)
- T = Torah (5 books of Moses)
- N = Nevi'im (Prophets): Joshua, Judges, Samuel, Kings, Isaiah, Jeremiah, Ezekiel, 12 Minor Prophets
- K = Ketuvim (Writings): Psalms, Proverbs, Job, Song of Solomon, etc.

### Talmud
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Talmud
- Central text of Rabbinic Judaism, second in authority only to Tanakh
- Consists of Mishnah + Gemara (commentary on Mishnah)
- 63 tractates, written in Aramaic and Hebrew
- Compiled between ~70 CE and ~500-600 CE
- Two versions: Babylonian Talmud (more authoritative) and Jerusalem Talmud

### Mishnah
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mishnah (inferred from Talmud article)
- First major written collection of Jewish oral traditions / oral Torah
- Compiled c. 200 CE by Rabbi Judah the Prince
- Organized into 6 orders (sedarim)

## Ten Commandments
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ten_Commandments
- Hebrew: Aseret HaDibrot ("Ten Words")
- Given by God to Moses at Mount Sinai
- Appear in Exodus 20 and Deuteronomy 5
- Written on two stone tablets; Moses broke first set after seeing golden calf
- Placed in Ark of the Covenant
- Foundational core of Jewish law (Halakha)

## Temples

### First Temple (Solomon's Temple)
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solomon%27s_Temple
- Built by King Solomon on Temple Mount in Jerusalem
- Destroyed by Nebuchadnezzar II (Neo-Babylonian Empire) in 587/586 BCE
- Contained Ark of the Covenant in Holy of Holies
- Only High Priest could enter Holy of Holies, only on Yom Kippur

### Second Temple
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Temple
- Completed ~516 BCE; rebuilt/enhanced by Herod the Great ~18 BCE
- Destroyed by Romans in 70 CE (Siege of Jerusalem under Titus)
- Destruction led to Diaspora and shift to Rabbinic Judaism
- Three Pilgrimage Festivals (Passover, Shavuot, Sukkot) observed there

## Key Figures

### David
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David (standard knowledge; confirmed in arch)
- Second king of united Kingdom of Israel
- Killed Goliath; wrote many Psalms
- Jerusalem became capital under his reign
- Father of Solomon

### Solomon
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solomon
- Third king of united Israel; son of David
- Built First Temple ~957 BCE
- Known for wisdom (Judgment of Solomon)
- Tanakh credits him with Proverbs, Ecclesiastes, Song of Solomon

### Maimonides (Rambam)
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maimonides
- Full name: Moses ben Maimon (1135/1138 – 1204 CE)
- Born in Córdoba, Spain; family exiled by Almohad Caliphate 1148
- Settled in Fustat (Cairo)
- Hebrew acronym: Rambam
- 14-volume Mishneh Torah = codification of Jewish law
- 13 Principles of Faith
- Also served as personal physician to Saladin

### Rashi
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rashi
- Born: c. 22 February 1040, Troyes, France; died: 13 July 1105
- Full name: Shlomo Yitzchaki; acronym: Rashi
- Authored comprehensive commentaries on Talmud and Hebrew Bible
- Commentary on Babylonian Talmud included in every edition since first printing 1520s
- Most widely read commentary on the Torah

### Hillel the Elder
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hillel_the_Elder
- Active end of 1st century BCE / early 1st century CE
- Died ~10 CE
- Founded House of Hillel school; associated with development of Mishnah and Talmud
- Famous maxim: "What is hateful to you, do not do unto your fellow. That is the whole Torah; the rest is the explanation; go and learn."

### Theodor Herzl
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theodor_Herzl
- Born: 2 May 1860; Died: 3 July 1904
- Hungarian Jewish journalist and lawyer
- "Father of modern political Zionism"
- Published Der Judenstaat (1896): argued for a Jewish homeland
- Founded Zionist Organization (1897); chaired First Zionist Congress in Basel

## Historical Events

### Masada
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Masada
- Mountain fortress in Judaean Desert; built in 1st century BCE
- Herod the Great built palaces there
- During First Jewish-Roman War (66-73 CE): final holdout of Jewish rebels (Sicarii faction)
- Romans built siege ramp; according to Josephus ~1,000 inhabitants died by mass suicide 73/74 CE (debated by historians)

### The Holocaust / Shoah
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Holocaust
- Genocide of European Jews during WWII by Nazi Germany and collaborators
- 1941-1945: approximately six million Jews murdered (about two-thirds of Europe's Jewish population)
- Methods: mass shootings and poison gas chambers (Auschwitz-Birkenau, Treblinka, Belzec, Sobibor, Chelmno, Majdanek)
- Hebrew name: Shoah

### Modern Israel
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israel (standard reference)
- State of Israel declared independence: 14 May 1948
- Theodor Herzl is called "spiritual father" of the Jewish State

## Western Wall (Kotel)
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Wall
- Ancient retaining wall of Temple Mount in Jerusalem
- Remains from Second Temple period (Herodian construction)
- Most sacred site in Judaism accessible for prayer
- Also called Kotel (Hebrew); Western/Wailing Wall (English)

## Practices

### Shabbat
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shabbat
- Judaism's day of rest: Friday sunset to Saturday night (3 stars)
- Observance: refrain from work; festive meals; candles lit, blessings over wine and bread
- Seventh day of week; commemorates creation and redemption from Egypt

### Kashrut (Kosher)
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kashrut
- Set of dietary laws
- Only certain mammals, birds, fish permitted
- Pork and shellfish forbidden
- Meat and dairy may never be mixed
- Animals slaughtered via shechita by certified shochet

### Bar/Bat Mitzvah
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bar_and_bat_mitzvah
- Coming of age ritual in Judaism
- Boys: 13 (Orthodox); Girls: 12 (Orthodox) or 13 (Reform/Conservative)
- At this age, child is held accountable for own actions under Jewish law

### Kaddish
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaddish
- Hymn praising God recited during Jewish prayer services
- Central theme: magnification and sanctification of God's name
- Mourner's Kaddish: recited during mourning (11 months after parent's death)
- Must be said with a minyan (quorum of 10 adult Jews)

## Holidays

### Passover (Pesach)
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Passover
- Celebrates Exodus of Israelites from slavery in Egypt
- One of Three Pilgrimage Festivals
- God "passed over" homes marked with lamb's blood during 10th plague
- Seder meal: read Haggadah recounting Exodus

### Yom Kippur
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yom_Kippur
- Holiest day in Judaism; "Day of Atonement"
- 10th of Tishrei (September/October)
- Observance: full fast, extended prayer, sin confessions
- Last of Ten Days of Repentance (which begin on Rosh Hashanah)

### Rosh Hashanah
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rosh_Hashanah
- Jewish New Year; literal: "Head of the Year"
- 1st-2nd of Tishrei (late Sept/Oct)
- One of High Holy Days; begins Ten Days of Repentance
- Traditional anniversary of creation of Adam and Eve

### Hanukkah
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hanukkah
- Commemorates Maccabean Revolt against Seleucid Empire (2nd century BCE)
- Maccabees recovered Jerusalem and Second Temple
- 8 nights and days beginning 25 Kislev
- Each night: light one more candle on hanukkiah (9-branched menorah)
- Relatively minor in religious terms; gained major cultural significance in Western world

### Sukkot
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sukkot
- Feast of Tabernacles/Booths; begins 15 Tishrei
- One of Three Pilgrimage Festivals
- Autumn harvest festival; commemorates Exodus
- Observance: festive meals in sukkah (temporary hut); four species of plants

### Purim
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Purim
- Commemorates saving of Jewish people from Haman (official of Achaemenid/Persian Empire)
- Queen Esther and Mordecai foiled Haman's plot
- Recounted in Book of Esther
- Day of feasting and rejoicing

### Shavuot
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shavuot
- One of Three Pilgrimage Festivals; wheat harvest holiday
- Rabbinic tradition: marks revelation of Ten Commandments at Mount Sinai
- Hebrew: "Weeks" — marks end of 7-week Counting of the Omer after Passover
- Falls 50 days after Passover (hence Greek name Pentecost in Christian tradition)

## Religious Objects

### Temple Menorah (7-branched)
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temple_menorah
- Seven-branched candelabrum used in Tabernacle and Temple in Jerusalem
- Made of pure gold; fueled by fresh olive oil
- Became State of Israel's official emblem in 1948
- Depicted on Arch of Titus being carried away after 70 CE Temple destruction

### Mezuzah
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mezuzah
- Piece of parchment with Torah verses (Deuteronomy 6:4-9 and 11:13-21)
- Affixed in small case to doorposts of Jewish homes
- Verses include part of the Shema prayer
- Must be placed on every post-and-lintel entrance

### Tallit
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tallit
- Fringed prayer shawl worn by religious Jews
- Has tzitzit (fringes) on four corners
- Worn during morning prayer services and all Yom Kippur services

### Shofar
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shofar
- Ancient musical horn, typically a ram's horn
- Blown in synagogue on Rosh Hashanah and at end of Yom Kippur
- Also blown daily in month of Elul (leading up to Rosh Hashanah)

### Tefillin
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tefillin
- Small black leather boxes with leather straps containing Torah scroll verses
- Traditionally worn by adult male Jews during weekday morning prayers
- One placed on upper arm, one between eyes at forehead

## Star of David
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_of_David
- Hebrew: Magen David ("Shield of David"); hexagram (two overlapping triangles)
- Association with Jewish people dates to 17th century Prague
- Chosen as central symbol for Jewish national flag at First Zionist Congress (1897)
- Central symbol on national flag of State of Israel
- Used to identify fallen Jewish soldiers in WWI

## Symbols

### Tikkun Olam
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tikkun_olam
- Hebrew: "repairing the world"
- Modern usage: pursuit of social justice; Jews bear responsibility for welfare of society
- In Lurianic Kabbalah: mystical repair/return of sparks of Divine light

## Denominations

### Orthodox Judaism
- Strictly observes traditional Jewish law (Halakha)
- Believes Torah is divinely revealed and binding

### Conservative Judaism
- Middle position between Orthodox and Reform
- Maintains traditional practices while accepting some modern adaptations

### Reform Judaism
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reform_Judaism
- Origins: mid-19th century Germany (Rabbi Abraham Geiger)
- Emphasizes ethics over ceremony; views Jewish law as non-binding
- Individual Jew is autonomous in religious practice

### Reconstructionist Judaism
- Founded by Mordecai Kaplan (early 20th century)
- Sees Judaism as evolving religious civilization; focuses on community

### Hasidic Judaism
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hasidic_Judaism
- Founded by Israel Ben Eliezer "Baal Shem Tov" in 18th century Ukraine
- Spiritual revival movement emphasizing joy and direct connection to God
- Sub-group of Haredi (ultra-Orthodox) Judaism today
