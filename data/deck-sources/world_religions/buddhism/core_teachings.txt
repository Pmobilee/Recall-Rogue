## Four Noble Truths (Cattāri Ariyasaccāni)
1. Dukkha (suffering, unsatisfactoriness) — life involves suffering; even pleasant experiences are tinged with impermanence
2. Samudaya (the origin of dukkha) — suffering arises from craving (tanha) and attachment
3. Nirodha (the cessation of dukkha) — it is possible to end suffering; nirvana is the cessation of craving
4. Magga (the path) — the Noble Eightfold Path leads to the cessation of suffering

## Noble Eightfold Path (Ariyo Atthangiko Maggo)
Grouped into three sections:
- Wisdom (prajna): Right View, Right Intention
- Ethical conduct (sila): Right Speech, Right Action, Right Livelihood
- Mental cultivation (samadhi): Right Effort, Right Mindfulness, Right Concentration

1. Right View — understanding the Four Noble Truths; seeing reality clearly
2. Right Intention — intention of renunciation, non-ill will, non-cruelty
3. Right Speech — no lying, divisive speech, harsh words, idle chatter
4. Right Action — no killing, stealing, or sexual misconduct
5. Right Livelihood — avoiding occupations that harm living beings
6. Right Effort — cultivating wholesome states, abandoning unwholesome ones
7. Right Mindfulness — mindful awareness of body, feelings, mind, mental objects
8. Right Concentration — development of the four jhanas (meditative absorptions)

## Three Marks of Existence (Tilakkhana)
1. Anicca (impermanence) — all conditioned phenomena are impermanent
2. Dukkha (unsatisfactoriness/suffering) — all conditioned phenomena involve some degree of suffering
3. Anatta (non-self) — no permanent, unchanging self or soul exists

## Three Jewels / Three Refuges (Triratna / Ti-Ratana)
Taking refuge in the Three Jewels is central to Buddhist practice:
1. The Buddha — the Awakened One, the historical teacher
2. The Dharma — the teachings, the truth, the law
3. The Sangha — the community of practitioners (especially monastic community)
The formula "I take refuge in the Buddha, the Dharma, the Sangha" is a foundational refuge prayer

## Five Precepts (Panca-sila)
Lay Buddhist ethical guidelines:
1. Refrain from taking life (ahimsa)
2. Refrain from taking what is not given (no stealing)
3. Refrain from sexual misconduct
4. Refrain from false speech
5. Refrain from intoxicants that cloud the mind

## Key Concepts
- Karma: the law of intentional action and its consequences; actions shape future experiences
- Rebirth (Samsara): the cycle of death and rebirth; liberation from samsara = nirvana
- Nirvana: the cessation of craving, aversion, and delusion; the unconditioned state; liberation
- Dependent Origination (Pratitya-samutpada): all phenomena arise in dependence on conditions; nothing exists independently
- Middle Way (Majjhima Patipada): the path between extreme asceticism and sensual indulgence; what Buddha discovered after rejecting both
- Dharma: the Buddha's teachings; also the natural law/truth underlying reality
- Sangha: the community of Buddhist practitioners
- Bodhicitta: the aspiration to attain enlightenment for the benefit of all beings (Mahayana concept)
- Sunyata (Emptiness): the teaching that all phenomena are empty of inherent, independent existence (key Mahayana/Madhyamaka concept)
