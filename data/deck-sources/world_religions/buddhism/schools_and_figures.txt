# Buddhism — Schools, Figures, Texts, Practices, Holy Days
# Source: Wikipedia (multiple articles)
# Fetched: 2026-04-08

## Three Main Schools

### Theravada
- Oldest surviving Buddhist school
- Predominant in: Sri Lanka, Thailand, Cambodia, Myanmar, Laos
- Means "Teaching of the Elders"
- Canon: Pali Canon (Tipitaka) — written in Pali language
- Ideal: Arahant — one who has attained personal liberation/enlightenment
- Emphasis: individual liberation, meditation, monastic discipline
- NOT to be called "Hinayana" — that term is considered pejorative and rejected by Theravadins

### Mahayana
- "Great Vehicle" — the larger of the two major surviving schools
- Predominant in: China, Japan, Korea, Vietnam, Tibet (also Vajrayana)
- Developed approximately 1st century BCE onwards
- Ideal: Bodhisattva — one who postpones own nirvana to help all beings attain liberation
- Key texts: Heart Sutra, Lotus Sutra, Diamond Sutra
- Emphasizes Buddha's cosmic nature, multiple Buddhas and bodhisattvas
- Sources: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mahayana

### Vajrayana
- "Diamond/Thunderbolt Vehicle" — esoteric/tantric branch of Mahayana
- Predominant in: Tibet, Bhutan, Mongolia, parts of Japan (Shingon)
- Practices: mandalas, mantras, visualizations, guru-disciple transmission, ritual
- Associated with Tibetan Buddhism and the Dalai Lama lineage
- Sources: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vajrayana

## Sub-Schools

### Zen (Chan in Chinese)
- Japanese form of Chan Buddhism, brought from China
- Emphasis: direct experience of enlightenment through meditation (zazen)
- Uses koans — paradoxical riddles for meditation (e.g., "What is the sound of one hand clapping?")
- Bodhidharma traditionally credited with bringing Chan to China (5th–6th century CE)
- Sources: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zen

### Pure Land
- Emphasizes devotion to Amitabha Buddha (Amida in Japanese)
- Practitioners chant Amitabha's name (nembutsu: "Namu Amida Butsu") to be reborn in the Pure Land
- Pure Land = Sukhavati, a realm where conditions for attaining enlightenment are perfect
- Major form of Buddhism in China, Japan, Korea
- Sources: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pure_Land_Buddhism

### Nichiren
- Japanese school founded by monk Nichiren (1222–1282 CE)
- Centers on devotion to the Lotus Sutra
- Chant: "Nam-myoho-renge-kyo"
- Sources: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nichiren_Buddhism

### Tibetan Buddhism
- Combines Mahayana and Vajrayana elements with indigenous Bon practices
- Dalai Lama = spiritual leader of the Gelug school; reincarnation lineage
- 14th Dalai Lama: Tenzin Gyatso, born 1935; Nobel Peace Prize 1989
- Sources: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tibetan_Buddhism

## Bodhisattvas

### Avalokiteshvara (Guanyin)
- Bodhisattva of compassion
- In Chinese: Guanyin (also Kuan Yin) — often depicted as female in East Asian Buddhism
- In Tibetan: Chenrezig — the Dalai Lama is considered his emanation
- One of the most widely venerated bodhisattvas
- Sources: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guanyin

### Manjushri
- Bodhisattva of wisdom
- Depicted wielding a flaming sword to cut through ignorance, holding a lotus with a sutra
- Sources: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manjushri

### Maitreya
- The future Buddha — the next Buddha to appear in this world after Gautama
- Currently residing in the Tusita Heaven, awaiting his time to be born
- Sources: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maitreya

### Tara
- Female bodhisattva of compassion, activity, and protection
- Green Tara and White Tara are most commonly venerated forms
- Especially venerated in Tibetan Buddhism
- Sources: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tara_(Buddhism)

## Key Historical Figures

### Ashoka (r. ~268–232 BCE)
- Mauryan Emperor of India; one of the most powerful emperors in Indian history
- After the bloody Kalinga War, converted to Buddhism and renounced violence
- Spread Buddhism across India and sent missionaries to Sri Lanka, Central Asia, and beyond
- Built stupas throughout his empire; erected Ashoka Pillars with edicts carved on them
- Considered the first great patron of Buddhism
- Sources: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ashoka

### Nagarjuna (~2nd century CE)
- Indian Buddhist philosopher; founder of the Madhyamaka school of Mahayana philosophy
- Elaborated the concept of sunyata (emptiness) — all phenomena are empty of inherent existence
- One of the most influential thinkers in Buddhist history
- Sources: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nagarjuna

### Bodhidharma (~5th–6th century CE)
- Indian (or Central Asian) Buddhist monk traditionally credited with transmitting Chan (Zen) Buddhism to China
- Legend: meditated facing a wall for nine years at Shaolin Monastery
- Considered the first Chinese patriarch of Chan Buddhism
- Sources: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bodhidharma

### Padmasambhava (~8th century CE)
- Indian tantric master credited with introducing Vajrayana Buddhism to Tibet
- Called "Guru Rinpoche" (Precious Teacher) in Tibet
- Co-founded the first Buddhist monastery in Tibet (Samye Monastery) with Shantarakshita
- Sources: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Padmasambhava

### Thich Nhat Hanh (1926–2022)
- Vietnamese Zen Buddhist monk, peace activist, writer
- Coined the term "Engaged Buddhism" — applying Buddhist ethics to social justice and peace
- Founded the Plum Village tradition; wrote "The Heart of the Buddha's Teaching"
- Nominated for Nobel Peace Prize by Martin Luther King Jr.
- Sources: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thich_Nhat_Hanh

### Dalai Lama (14th — Tenzin Gyatso)
- Born: July 6, 1935 in Taktser, Tibet
- Recognized as 14th Dalai Lama at age 2; enthroned 1940
- "Dalai Lama" means "Ocean of Wisdom" (Mongolian dalai + Tibetan bla-ma)
- Nobel Peace Prize: 1989
- Volatile: living figure; lineage succession is contested

## Sacred Texts

### Tripitaka (Pali: Tipitaka)
- "Three Baskets" — the complete scriptural canon of Theravada Buddhism
- Three sections: Vinaya Pitaka (monastic rules), Sutta Pitaka (discourses), Abhidhamma Pitaka (philosophical analysis)
- Written in Pali language
- The Pali Canon is the most complete early Buddhist canon
- Sources: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tipitaka

### Heart Sutra (Prajna Paramita Hridaya)
- Short but central Mahayana text on sunyata (emptiness)
- Contains the famous line: "Form is emptiness, emptiness is form"
- Widely chanted in Mahayana traditions daily
- Sources: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heart_Sutra

### Lotus Sutra (Saddharma Pundarika Sutra)
- Major Mahayana text; foundational for Tiantai and Nichiren schools
- Teaches that all beings can attain Buddhahood; Buddha has infinite lifespan
- Sources: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lotus_Sutra

### Diamond Sutra (Vajracchedika Prajnaparamita Sutra)
- Key Prajnaparamita text; core to Chan/Zen tradition
- One of the oldest printed books in the world (868 CE Chinese woodblock print)
- Sources: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diamond_Sutra

### Dhammapada
- Collection of sayings of the Buddha in verse form, from the Pali Canon
- One of the most widely read Buddhist scriptures
- 423 verses organized into 26 chapters
- Sources: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dhammapada

## Holy Sites (Four Main Pilgrimage Sites)
1. Lumbini (Nepal) — birthplace of Siddhartha Gautama
2. Bodh Gaya (Bihar, India) — site of the Bodhi tree where Buddha attained enlightenment
3. Sarnath (Uttar Pradesh, India) — Deer Park where Buddha gave first sermon
4. Kushinagar (Uttar Pradesh, India) — site of Buddha's parinirvana (death)

Additional: Lhasa (Tibet) — home of the Potala Palace, seat of the Dalai Lama lineage

## Religious Objects
- Stupa: dome-shaped structure housing Buddhist relics; a shrine and pilgrimage focus; the Mahabodhi Temple at Bodh Gaya is famous
- Mandala: sacred geometric diagram representing the universe or a deity's realm; used in Vajrayana visualization practice
- Prayer wheel (Mani wheel): cylindrical wheel containing Buddhist prayers/mantras; spinning it is said to have the same effect as reciting the prayers
- Mala: string of beads used for counting recitations of mantras or prayers (108 beads traditionally)
- Vajra (thunderbolt): ritual object in Vajrayana Buddhism representing indestructibility and skillful means

## Religious Buildings
- Stupa: the archetypal Buddhist monument; houses relics
- Pagoda: East Asian multi-tiered tower derived from the stupa; found in China, Japan, Korea
- Vihara: monastery; living quarters for Buddhist monks and nuns
- Temple: general term for Buddhist worship hall

## Holy Days
- Vesak (Wesak, Buddha Day): the most sacred Buddhist holiday; celebrates Buddha's birth, enlightenment, AND parinirvana on the same day (full moon of May); UNESCO recognized; also called "Buddha Purnima" in South Asia
- Bodhi Day: December 8 in some traditions (esp. Japanese Zen); commemorates Buddha's enlightenment under the Bodhi tree
