## TAOISM

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taoism
Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laozi
Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tao_Te_Ching
Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zhuangzi_(philosopher)

- Laozi (also Lao Tzu / Lao-Tze) — traditional founder, dates ~6th-4th century BCE, historicity debated
- Wikidata: Laozi birth ~579 BCE, death ~500 BCE (Zhoukou, Chu state)
- Tao Te Ching: attributed to Laozi, ~81 short chapters, compiled ~4th century BCE (scholarly dating)
- Zhuangzi: philosopher ~369–286 BCE, second major classical Taoist text (Zhuangzi)
- Tao (道): "the Way" — the fundamental, unnameable principle underlying all reality
- Wu wei (無為): "effortless action" / "non-action" — acting in accordance with nature's flow
- Yin-yang (陰陽): complementary interdependent forces; first appears in Taoist cosmology
- Taoism has ~20 million adherents primarily in China, Taiwan, SE Asia
- Taoist temples are common in China; Chinese folk religion has heavy Taoist overlap

## CONFUCIANISM

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confucianism
Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confucius
Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analects
Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mencius

- Confucius (孔子 Kǒng Zǐ): born 551 BCE Qufu, Shandong; died 479 BCE — per Wikidata Q4604
- Analects (論語 Lúnyǔ): collection of sayings and ideas attributed to Confucius, compiled by disciples
- Ren (仁): benevolence / humaneness — the highest Confucian virtue
- Li (禮): ritual propriety, correct social conduct
- Xiao (孝): filial piety — respect for parents and ancestors, foundational social value
- The Five Relationships: ruler/subject, parent/child, husband/wife, elder/younger brother, friend/friend
- Junzi (君子): "exemplary person" / "gentleman" — Confucian ideal of moral cultivation
- Mencius (孟子 Mèngzǐ): 372–289 BCE, most important Confucian after Confucius; taught human nature is inherently good
- Confucianism not always classified as "religion" — some scholars call it an ethical-political philosophy; functions religiously in East Asian culture
- Influence: shaped Chinese, Korean, Japanese, Vietnamese governance, education for ~2000 years

## SHINTO

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shinto
Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kami
Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amaterasu
Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ise_Grand_Shrine
Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torii

- Shinto: Japan's indigenous religion, no single founder, no fixed canon of scripture
- Kami (神): spirits/divine forces in nature, objects, ancestors — central concept of Shinto
- Kami-no-michi (神の道): Japanese reading of Shinto — "Way of the Kami"
- Amaterasu (天照大神): sun goddess, one of principal kami; considered divine ancestor of the Japanese imperial family
- Torii gate (鳥居): sacred gateway marking entrance to Shinto shrine space
- Jinja (神社): Shinto shrine — place of worship, kami residence
- Ise Grand Shrine (伊勢神宮): most sacred Shinto site in Japan, dedicated to Amaterasu; rebuilt every 20 years (Shikinen Sengu) — current reconstruction 2013
- Mount Fuji considered sacred in both Shinto and Buddhist traditions
- Misogi / purification rituals: ritual cleansing (harae) central to Shinto practice; temizuya (water basin) at shrine entrance
- ~3 million practicing Shinto adherents (formal); cultural practice far more widespread in Japan

## ZOROASTRIANISM

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zoroastrianism
Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zoroaster
Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ahura_Mazda
Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Avesta
Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parsis

- Zoroaster (also Zarathustra / Zartosht): founder; Wikidata Q35811 gives ~627–550 BCE (traditional Iranian date); many scholars prefer 1500–1000 BCE based on linguistic analysis; Ray (Rhagae) per Wikidata
- Ahura Mazda (Wise Lord): the one supreme God of Zoroastrianism — uncreated, all-knowing, benevolent
- Angra Mainyu (Ahriman): the destructive spirit / evil principle opposing Ahura Mazda — cosmic dualism
- Avesta: sacred scripture of Zoroastrianism; oldest parts (Gathas) composed by Zoroaster himself
- Fire temple (Atash Behram): sacred fire maintained as symbol of Ahura Mazda's light and purity — fire is NOT worshipped; it is the object of veneration as symbol
- Parsis: Zoroastrian diaspora community in India (mainly Mumbai/Gujarat), descendants of refugees from Persia after Islamic conquest (~8th–10th cent CE); ~55,000–69,000 today
- Nowruz (Persian New Year): Zoroastrian/Persian new year, spring equinox — celebrated for ~3,000 years
- Zoroastrianism influenced later Abrahamic religions: concepts of heaven/hell, cosmic dualism, final judgment, resurrection, angels/demons
- ~100,000–200,000 Zoroastrians worldwide today

## JAINISM

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jainism
Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mahavira
Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tirthankara
Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ahimsa_in_Jainism
Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digambara
Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shvetambara

- Mahavira (Vardhamana): 24th and last Tirthankara, born c.599 BCE Vaishali (Bihar), died c.527 BCE Pawapuri — per Wikipedia
- Tirthankaras (24): spiritual teachers who have attained enlightenment and guide others to liberation; Rishabhanatha (Adinatha) is the first; Parshvanatha is the 23rd
- Ahimsa (non-violence): the supreme Jain principle — extended radically to all living beings including insects, microorganisms; Jain monks sweep path to avoid stepping on insects
- Five major vows (Mahavrata for monks): ahimsa, satya (truth), asteya (non-stealing), brahmacharya (celibacy), aparigraha (non-possessiveness)
- Digambara (sky-clad): major Jain sect; monks practice nudity as rejection of worldly attachment
- Shvetambara (white-clad): major Jain sect; monks wear white robes
- Karma in Jainism: physical substance that clings to the soul due to actions — unique Jain doctrine
- Moksha: liberation from the cycle of rebirth; attained by destroying all karma through right knowledge, faith, and conduct
- Agamas: canonical Jain scriptures (Shvetambara tradition)
- ~4–5 million Jains worldwide, primarily in India; disproportionate economic and cultural influence
- Jainism and Buddhism both emerged in 6th–5th cent BCE as reform movements opposing Vedic ritualism

## BAHÁ'Í FAITH

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27%C3%AD_Faith
Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27u%27ll%C3%A1h
Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_B%C3%A1b
Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kit%C3%A1b-i-Aqdas

- Bahá'u'lláh (born 12 Nov 1817, Tehran; died 29 May 1892, Acre/Bahjí, Ottoman Syria): prophet-founder of Bahá'í Faith
- The Báb (born 20 Oct 1819, Shiraz; executed 9 July 1850): forerunner and herald of Bahá'u'lláh; declared mission in 1844 in Shiraz, Persia — this is the formal founding date of the Bahá'í/Bábí movement
- Kitáb-i-Aqdas: "Most Holy Book" — primary Bahá'í scripture, authored by Bahá'u'lláh
- Bahá'í World Centre: located in Haifa and Acre, Israel — administrative and spiritual center; includes Shrine of the Báb on Mount Carmel
- Core teachings: unity of all religions (all major faiths are progressive revelations of one God), unity of humanity, elimination of all forms of prejudice, equality of men and women, harmony of science and religion, adoption of a world auxiliary language
- Nine-pointed star: symbol of Bahá'í Faith (nine is significant as number of perfection/completion)
- ~5–8 million Bahá'ís worldwide in nearly every country

## YORUBA / IFA

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yoruba_religion
Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/If%C3%A1
Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orisha

- Yoruba religion: indigenous West African spiritual tradition originating among the Yoruba people of Nigeria, Benin, and Togo
- Olodumare (also Olorun): supreme God/creator, transcendent, interacts with humanity through orishas
- Orishas: divine spirits/deities each governing aspects of nature and human life (e.g., Shango — thunder; Oshun — rivers/love; Yemoja — ocean/motherhood)
- Ifa divination: sacred system of divination using palm nuts or chain (opele); Ifa corpus is an oral scripture of 256 chapters (Odu) — recognized by UNESCO as Intangible Cultural Heritage
- ~100 million people of Yoruba heritage worldwide; diaspora spread to Americas via transatlantic slave trade
- Yoruba religion directly influenced Candomblé (Brazil), Santería (Cuba), Trinidad Orisha, Louisiana Voodoo

## RASTAFARI

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rastafari
Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haile_Selassie
Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bob_Marley

- Rastafari: religious and social movement that arose in Jamaica in the 1930s
- Haile Selassie I (born 23 July 1892 as Tafari Makonnen; died 27 August 1975): Emperor of Ethiopia 1930–1974; Rastafarians regard him as a divine or messianic figure, the fulfillment of Biblical prophecy, specifically Psalm 68:31 and Revelation 5:5 ("Lion of the tribe of Judah")
- Pre-coronation title Ras Tafari gave the movement its name
- Core beliefs: Africa (especially Ethiopia) as Zion/spiritual homeland; Babylon = oppressive Western civilization; repatriation to Africa (spiritual and/or physical)
- Ital diet: natural, organic, often vegan or vegetarian food — central Rastafari practice
- Dreadlocks: symbolic of the Lion of Judah, uncut hair per Nazarite vow (Numbers 6), and natural living
- The Bible: central text, read through Afrocentric interpretive lens
- Bob Marley (1945–1981): Jamaican musician, internationally most famous Rastafarian; spread Rastafari globally through reggae music
- ~700,000–1,000,000 Rastafarians worldwide

## VODOU / SANTERÍA / CANDOMBLÉ

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haitian_Vodou
Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Santeri%C3%ADa
Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Candombl%C3%A9

- Haitian Vodou: Afro-Caribbean religion of Haiti; blend of West African traditions (Fon, Ewe, Yoruba) with elements of French Catholicism
- Loa (also lwa): spiritual beings in Vodou — intermediaries between Bondye (the supreme God, remote/inaccessible) and humans; include Baron Samedi (death), Erzulie (love), Ogou (iron/war)
- Vodou is NOT sorcery or "voodoo dolls" — that stereotype comes from Hollywood horror films. Vodou is a legitimate, complex syncretic religion with millions of practitioners.
- Santería (Lucumí / Regla de Ocha): Afro-Cuban religion blending Yoruba orisha veneration with Catholic saints; practiced in Cuba, Puerto Rico, USA
- Candomblé: Afro-Brazilian religion rooted in Yoruba tradition; centers on orishas; practiced mainly in Bahia, Brazil
- These three traditions are distinct but share West African Yoruba roots and the history of the transatlantic slave trade

## INDIGENOUS / TRADITIONAL SPIRITUALITIES

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Native_American_religion
Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dreamtime

- CRITICAL: There is no single "Native American religion" — there are hundreds of distinct Indigenous nations in North America, each with their own spiritual traditions. Generalizations are inaccurate.
- Common THEMES (not universal): reverence for land and nature, ceremonial practices tied to seasons, oral tradition, ancestors as present spirits — but specifics vary enormously by nation
- Animism: the belief that all things (animals, plants, rivers, mountains) possess spiritual essence — a common but not universal feature of indigenous spiritualities worldwide
- Aboriginal Australian Dreamtime (Tjukurpa): the complex of beliefs of Australian Aboriginal peoples about the creation of the world, spiritual beings, and moral law; the "Dreaming" describes both a distant creative past and an ever-present spiritual reality
- ~370 million indigenous people worldwide maintaining traditional spiritual practices

## TENRIKYO / CAO DAI (brief)

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tenrikyo
Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caodaism

- Tenrikyo: Japanese new religion founded 1838 by Nakayama Miki (1798–1887), a woman who claimed divine possession; based in Tenri City, Nara Prefecture; ~1.5 million adherents
- Cao Dai: Vietnamese syncretic religion founded 1926 in Tây Ninh Province; blends elements of Buddhism, Taoism, Confucianism, and Western religions; ~4–6 million adherents; distinctive colorful temples
