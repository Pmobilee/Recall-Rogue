SHIELD-MAIDEN — NORSE WOMEN WARRIORS

Definition and Terminology
A shield-maiden (Old Norse: skjaldmær, plural skjaldmeyjar) was a woman who chose to fight as a warrior in Norse tradition. The term appears in medieval Norse literary sources — sagas, Eddic poetry, and the Latin chronicle Gesta Danorum — to describe women who took up weapons, trained for combat, and participated in battles alongside male warriors.

The term skjaldmær combines skjald (shield) and mær (maiden, young woman). However, scholars note that the term may not date to the Viking Age itself. While the shield-maiden concept appears in sources composed during and after the Viking Age, evidence suggests the specific terminology skjaldmær may have entered Old Norse in the 13th century, meaning the term could be a medieval literary construction applied to an older concept.

Literary Attestations

Hervor (Hervarar saga ok Heiðreks)
Hervor is one of the most detailed shield-maiden characters in Old Norse literature. Born to the legendary warrior Hervarðr, she rejected a conventional female life and instead took up arms, adopting a male name (Hervard) and sailing with Vikings. The saga describes her venturing alone to the island of Samsø to retrieve the cursed sword Tyrfing from the burial mound of her father Hervarðr — a scene of remarkable dramatic intensity in which she confronts and banishes the dead. She later served as a warrior before eventually settling, marrying, and having children. Her granddaughter also bore the name Hervor and became a warrior.

Brynhildr (Völsunga saga and Poetic Edda)
Brynhildr is simultaneously a valkyrie, a shield-maiden, and a central tragic figure of Norse heroic legend. In the Völsunga saga, she is a valkyrie whom Odin punished by putting her into a sleep surrounded by fire, to be awakened by the hero Sigurd. She is described as a warrior woman of exceptional combat skill before her punishment. She is also the "Brynhildr" of the Prose Edda and Poetic Edda poems. After Sigurd awakens her, the tragic love story between them and the political machinations of the Nibelungen cycle follow.

Brynhildr of Bósa saga ok Herrauðs
A separate character from the Völsunga Brynhildr, this Brynhildr is a shield-maiden described in the legendary saga as a warrior in her own right.

Thornbjörg (Hrólfs saga Gautrekssonar)
A Swedish princess who takes up arms and fights as a shield-maiden in this legendary saga.

Lagertha (Gesta Danorum, by Saxo Grammaticus, c. 1200)
Saxo Grammaticus, the Danish chronicler, described Lagertha as a shield-maiden who fought in the army of the legendary Danish king Ragnar Lodbrok during a conflict in Norway. Saxo wrote that she "had the courage of a man, and fought in front among the bravest with her hair loose over her shoulders." He noted she commanded a contingent during battle and distinguished herself in combat.

Saxo also describes other female warriors at the same battle, including Wisna (standard-bearer who lost a hand) and Webiorg (who killed several warriors before dying herself). However, Saxo's account is considered semi-legendary and was written approximately two centuries after the Viking Age, calling its strict historical accuracy into question.

Other Named Shield-Maidens in Gesta Danorum
Saxo Grammaticus names several other female warriors: Princess Hed, Visna, Lagertha, and Veborg. These women are described as participating in the legendary Battle of Brávellir.

The Birka Warrior Grave (Grave Bj 581)
The most significant archaeological evidence for historical women in warrior roles is a grave excavated at Birka, Sweden, in the 1870s. The site is designated Bj 581 in the archaeological record.

Original excavation (1878): The grave was excavated by archaeologist Hjalmar Stolpe. It contained a remarkably well-equipped warrior burial: a sword, an axe, a spear, armor-piercing arrows, a battle knife, two shields, and the bones of two horses (one mare, one stallion). Gaming pieces were also included, interpreted by some scholars as evidence of military strategy expertise. The burial was designated a high-ranking warrior based on its contents.

For over 130 years, scholars assumed the individual was male, as was standard for warrior burials of this type.

2014 osteological analysis: Bioarchaeologist Anna Kjellström conducted a new analysis of the skeletal remains and identified them as belonging to a female individual based on pelvic and mandibular morphology.

2017 DNA analysis: A study published in the American Journal of Physical Anthropology confirmed through ancient DNA analysis that the individual was biologically female. The study, led by Charlotte Hedenstierna-Jonson and colleagues, concluded that the person in Bj 581 was a high-ranking female professional warrior — possibly a military commander given the gaming pieces and the overall richness of the burial.

Significance of Bj 581: This is the first Viking Age burial to be confirmed through DNA analysis as belonging to a woman warrior with a full martial burial kit. It validated theories some archaeologists had proposed since the 1970s based on the skeletal morphology.

Scholarly debate: The 2017 study generated significant academic discussion. Some archaeologists argued the weapons may have been symbolic rather than functional, or that a wealthy woman may have been buried with martial equipment for reasons unrelated to personal combat. Others noted that burial goods in Norse practice typically reflected the deceased's actual role in life.

Additional Archaeological Evidence
Multiple female burials across Scandinavia have yielded weapons and military equipment:
- A Norwegian grave contained a woman buried with a sword, spear, and shield
- A Danish burial revealed a skeleton interred with arrows and a bow
- Historian Neil Price (author of The Children of Ash and Elm) argues that the prevalence of weapon-bearing female burials suggests female warriors were not exceptional outliers but represented a recognized social role

Scholarly Arguments and Debate

Arguments for historical shield-maidens:
- Neil Price argues: "If it was something so out of the ordinary, why was it so common in the text?" — pointing to the frequency of literary references as evidence for cultural acceptance
- Viking Age burial customs meant that objects placed in graves reflected the deceased's actual role; weapon burials indicate actual use
- Archaeological evidence from Birka and elsewhere confirms some women were buried as warriors
- Historical records describe "women participating in battles alongside men" during the defense of Dublin in the 9th century

Arguments for caution:
- Both the legendary sagas and Gesta Danorum were written after the Viking Age and are considered largely fictional or semi-legendary
- The specific term skjaldmær may not date to the Viking Age itself
- Some scholars argue weapon burials may reflect status, family identity, or symbolic meaning rather than personal combat roles
- No contemporary (Viking Age) written source explicitly describes women fighting in battles

Historical Context
Viking Age society's practical circumstances may have created conditions for female warriors. With men frequently absent on raids, trade expeditions, and wars, women in Norse communities took on expanded roles in defense and resource management. Some scholars suggest that "women likely took up arms to defend their homes, settlements, and communities" during such periods of male absence.

The valkyrie mythology — female figures who chose the slain on battlefields and carried warriors to Valhalla — may reflect a cultural framework that made female martial roles conceptually coherent within Norse cosmology and society.
