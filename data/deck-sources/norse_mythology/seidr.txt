SEIÐR — NORSE MAGIC AND SORCERY

Definition and Etymology
Seiðr (pronounced roughly "SAY-der") derives from the Old Norse word seiðr, which literally means "cord," "string," or "snare." It refers to a pre-Christian Norse magical and shamanic practice concerned fundamentally with understanding and manipulating fate. The core concept involved a form of symbolic weaving — practitioners wove new events into existence much as the Norns wove the fates of all beings at the roots of Yggdrasil. To accomplish this work, the practitioner entered an ecstatic trance state, typically while holding a ritual distaff, enabling communication with the spirit world.

Core Practices and Applications
Seiðr served multiple purposes within Viking Age society. Attestations across sagas and Eddic poetry indicate seiðr rituals were used for:
- Divination and clairvoyance (seeing the future or hidden things)
- Seeking out hidden knowledge, secrets of the mind, and lost physical objects
- Healing the sick
- Bringing good luck and prosperity
- Controlling weather
- Calling game animals and fish to hunters
- Destructive applications: casting curses, blighting land, inducing illness, spreading false prophecies, causing death in conflict

The practice manifested most commonly in three forms: prophecy, blessing, or curse-work. This versatility made practitioners both valued and feared by their communities.

The Völva: The Professional Practitioner
The primary human practitioner of seiðr was the völva (plural: völur), a term meaning "staff-carrier" or "wand-bearer" referring to the ritual distaff she carried. The völva was typically a traveling specialist: she moved between settlements, offering magical services in exchange for food, shelter, and payment.

The Saga of Erik the Red (Eiríks saga rauða) provides the most detailed surviving description of a völva and her work. The saga describes a woman named Þorbjörg lítilvölva ("the little völva") who visits a farmstead in Greenland during a time of famine and hardship. The description is unusually specific: she wore a blue cloak adorned with stones, a hood of black lambskin lined with white cat fur, boots of calfskin with the hair facing outward, and gloves of cat fur. She carried a staff decorated with brass fittings and a knob of stone. A special high-seat with a cushion of hen feathers was prepared for her.

When she performed the seiðr ceremony, other women formed a circle around her and sang the special songs (varðlokkur) that summoned the spirit helpers. The völva then entered a trance and answered questions about the coming year and about individuals' futures.

The Norns as Supreme Practitioners
The Norns — Urðr, Verðandi, and Skuld — occupied the highest rank among seiðr practitioners in the cosmological sense. These three beings lived at the roots of Yggdrasil at Urðarbrunnr (the Well of Urðr) and wove the fates of all gods, humans, and beings. Their weaving was permanent and irrevocable. This cosmological connection between weaving and fate-working formed the conceptual foundation of seiðr.

Human practitioners who performed seiðr were occasionally called norn (lowercase) — the Old Norse term for witch — reflecting their perceived role as fate-workers on a smaller scale.

Divine Practitioners: Freyja and Odin

Freyja
Freyja, the Vanir goddess of love, fertility, magic, and war, is identified in the Ynglinga saga (by Snorri Sturluson) as the most skilled practitioner of seiðr among the gods. Crucially, it was Freyja who introduced seiðr to the Aesir when she joined their pantheon as part of the post-war exchange of hostages between the Aesir and Vanir. The Ynglinga saga states explicitly: "Freyja was the first to teach the Aesir the art of seiðr, which had previously belonged only to the Vanir."

Freyja serves as the archetype of the völva. She presides over Fólkvangr, receives half of all warriors slain in battle (the other half going to Odin's Valhalla), and is described as deeply learned in seiðr's applications.

Odin
Despite his masculine authority as the Allfather, Odin is also described as a practitioner of seiðr. In the Ynglinga saga, it is stated that Odin learned seiðr from Freyja. However, this placed him in a socially precarious position.

In the Lokasenna flyting poem, Loki explicitly accuses Odin of practicing seiðr on the island Samsø, claiming he performed rituals "in witch's guise among men" — beating a drum (or performing chanting associated with seers). This was not merely poetic insult: it referenced a real social stigma.

Odin's embrace of seiðr despite this stigma reflects his character as the god willing to sacrifice social standing, physical integrity (he also sacrificed his eye and hung on Yggdrasil) and masculine honor for the acquisition of knowledge and power.

The Concept of Ergi — Unmanliness
At the core of seiðr's social complexity was the concept of ergi (noun: argr). In Viking Age Norse society, any man who adopted female social or sexual roles was associated with deep shame and dishonor. The word argr was one of the most severe insults that could be directed at a man — implying effeminacy, cowardice, and passive sexual behavior.

Because seiðr was fundamentally associated with women and the feminine sphere (weaving, prophecy, and the domestic world), men who practiced it invited the charge of argr. Weaving in particular was central to the female economic sphere in Norse society — the spindle and distaff were quintessentially female tools, and performing rituals using them carried the cultural weight of those gender associations.

Despite this severe social penalty, numerous men practiced seiðr professionally and their exploits were recorded in sagas. The paradox was this: while seiðr was deemed unmanly, it represented perhaps the greatest power available — the ability to alter destiny itself. Some practitioners apparently accepted the loss of masculine social standing in exchange for access to this transformative power.

Seiðmaðr — Male Practitioners
A male seiðr practitioner was called a seiðmaðr (plural seiðmenn). Sagas record their activities but always with awareness of the ergi stigma attached. The cultural tension surrounding male practitioners revealed a contradiction at the heart of Norse values: the highest power was coded feminine, and accessing it required men to transgress gender norms.

Techniques and Tools
Based on saga accounts, seiðr ceremony involved several elements:

- The staff or distaff (seiðstafr): A ritual implement carried by the völva, symbolically connecting her to the Norns' weaving
- Trance induction: Achieved through rhythmic chanting (galdr), drumming, singing of special songs (varðlokkur), and possibly the inhalation of psychoactive herbs or smoke
- Musical assistance: Egil's Saga references music as an aid to achieving altered states
- Glossolalia: Speaking in tongues or chanting runes as part of the ritual
- The raised platform (seiðhjallr): A specially constructed seat on a platform from which the völva performed
- Spirit helpers: The völva called upon helping spirits during the trance, who provided information about the future

Historical and Archaeological Context
The practice of seiðr has parallels in the shamanic traditions of many circumpolar and indigenous cultures — the trance journey, the spirit helpers, the ritual staff, and the special garments recur across Siberian, Sami, and Arctic shamanic traditions. Scholars including Neil Price have argued that Norse seiðr was influenced by Sami shamanic practices, given the geographic proximity and cultural contact between Norse and Sami peoples in Scandinavia.

Archaeological finds include unusual burials of women with staffs or wands, sometimes called "völva graves," which appear to confirm that the role of the traveling seeress was a real social institution and not purely literary invention.

Literary Attestations
Seiðr appears in multiple Old Norse sources:
- Ynglinga saga (Snorri Sturluson) — most explicit discussion of seiðr's origins and techniques
- Eiríks saga rauða (Saga of Erik the Red) — most detailed description of a völva ceremony
- Völuspá (Poetic Edda) — the "Insight of the Völva," a poem spoken by a völva
- Lokasenna (Poetic Edda) — Odin accused of seiðr practice
- Hrólfs saga kraka — features seiðr practiced in warfare
- Laxdæla saga — seiðr used in conflict between characters
- Numerous other Icelandic family sagas with passing references
