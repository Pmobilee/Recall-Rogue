GUNGNIR — ODIN'S SPEAR

Name and Etymology
Gungnir (Old Norse pronunciation: approximately "GUNG-neer") translates as "the swaying one" or "the trembling one" in Old Norse. The name may refer to the weapon's motion in flight or to its instability and unpredictability in the hands of enemies — paradoxically, the very spear that "sways" never misses its mark. An alternative reading connects the name to "the vibrating one," referring to the hum or vibration of a thrown spear in flight.

In the Prose Edda (Gylfaginning), Gungnir is listed among Odin's prized possessions and is mentioned by name. Additional attestations appear in Skáldskaparmál, Háttatal, Sigrdrífumál, and in a poem by the 9th-century skald Bragi Boddason.

Creation and Origin
Gungnir was crafted by the Sons of Ivaldi, a group of master dwarf craftsmen who lived in Nidavellir (the realm of the dwarves). The story of how Odin came to possess Gungnir is tied directly to one of Loki's characteristic acts of mischief and subsequent repair.

The Hair-Cutting Incident
While the goddess Sif slept, Loki crept to her side and cut off all her golden hair. When Thor discovered what had happened, he threatened Loki with severe violence unless he made restitution. Loki traveled to the realm of the dwarves (Svartalfheim or Nidavellir) and contracted the Sons of Ivaldi to craft replacement hair for Sif — hair made of real gold that would grow like natural hair.

The Sons of Ivaldi agreed and, while at their forge, also crafted two additional gifts unsolicited: the ship Skidbladnir for the god Freyr (a ship that could sail any sea, fold up like a cloth, and fit in a pocket when not needed) and Gungnir for Odin. All three items were masterworks of dwarf craftsmanship.

The Wager with Brokkr and Sindri
Emboldened, Loki made a wager with the dwarves Brokkr and Sindri (also called Eitri), betting his own head that they could not produce three items as magnificent as what the Sons of Ivaldi had made. Brokkr and Sindri accepted the wager and forged three additional treasures: the golden boar Gullinbursti for Freyr, the gold arm-ring Draupnir for Odin, and the hammer Mjolnir for Thor.

Loki attempted to sabotage their work by transforming into a fly and biting Brokkr on the eyelid during the hammering of Mjolnir, causing the hammer's handle to come out slightly short. Despite this flaw, the gods judged Mjolnir the most valuable of all six gifts due to its utility against giants. Brokkr sought to claim Loki's head, but was prevented because he could not touch Loki's neck (as the wager specified the head only). Instead, Loki's lips were sewn shut.

Through this narrative, Gungnir arrived in Odin's hands as part of the delivery of the six dwarf-forged gifts to the Aesir. This story is told in Skáldskaparmál in the Prose Edda.

Physical Properties
Gungnir possesses several supernatural attributes documented in Norse sources:

Never-Misses Accuracy: The defining characteristic of Gungnir is that it never fails to reach its target. Once Odin hurls the spear, it cannot be deflected or evaded — it always strikes what Odin intends it to strike. The Old Norse text states: geirrinn nam aldri staðar í lagi, which translates as "the spear never stopped in its thrust" or alternately "the spear always pierced through."

Runes: Runes — the magical alphabet with both practical and supernatural associations — were carved onto the point (tip) of Gungnir. These runes imbued the spear with additional powers beyond its physical construction, as runes in Norse tradition carried inherent magical properties depending on which runes were carved and how.

Sacred Oath Object: Oaths sworn on Gungnir were considered among the most binding commitments possible in Norse culture. Invoking the sanctity of Odin's spear gave an oath the weight of the chief god's authority. In a culture where honor and word-keeping were paramount, swearing on Gungnir represented the highest possible level of personal commitment. Breaking such an oath would be not merely dishonorable but cosmologically significant.

Ritual and Martial Uses by Odin
Odin employed Gungnir not only as a weapon but as a ritual implement. In Norse accounts:

Initiating Battle: Before a large battle, Odin would hurl Gungnir over the heads of the opposing army, declaring them his by the act of throwing his spear over them. This ritual act — dedicating the enemy as sacrificial offerings to Odin — is attested in the Prose Edda and associated with the First War between the Aesir and Vanir. The Ynglinga saga states that Odin "declared this custom — that the chief should hurl a spear over the host of the enemy" before a battle, establishing a precedent that Norse warriors would follow.

The Aesir-Vanir War: According to Völuspá (the Prophecy of the Seeress), Odin threw Gungnir into the Vanir host at the start of the first war between the gods: "Geirum hristi" ("the spear he shook") before battle was joined.

Odin's Sacrifice on Yggdrasil: In the Hávamál poem, Odin describes hanging himself on Yggdrasil for nine days and nights as a sacrificial act to gain knowledge of the runes. The text states he was "wounded with a spear." This is generally interpreted as Odin piercing himself with Gungnir as part of the sacrifice — the spear that never misses, turned against its own master as a ritual act of self-offering.

Role at Ragnarök
Gungnir plays its final role in the apocalyptic battle of Ragnarök. According to Gylfaginning in the Prose Edda (chapter 51), Odin will ride at the head of the einherjar (his chosen warriors from Valhalla) into the final battle. He will be wearing:
- A gold helmet
- A coat of mail (brynja)
- Carrying Gungnir

Odin will use Gungnir to attack Fenrir, the great wolf who has broken free from his chains. Despite having the greatest spear ever forged and all of his powers, Odin will be swallowed whole by Fenrir during the battle. His son Víðarr will then avenge him by killing Fenrir — either by tearing the wolf's jaws apart or by stabbing it through the palate with his foot.

Comparative Mythology
Magical spears of divine origin appear across many mythological traditions, suggesting a cross-cultural archetype:
- The Gáe Bulg: The magical spear of the Irish hero Cú Chulainn, with barbs that opened inside a wound
- The spear of Lugh: The magical spear of the Irish god Lugh, one of the Tuatha Dé Danann's four treasures
- The Amenonuhoko: The jeweled spear of heaven used by Izanagi and Izanami in Japanese mythology to create the islands of Japan

These parallels suggest that divine spears carrying the authority of chief sky-gods represented a widespread Indo-European mythological motif.

Cultural Significance
The spear was the primary weapon of the Norse warrior and of Odin specifically. While Thor's symbol was the hammer (associated with thunder and protection), Odin's symbol was the spear — associated with war, sacrifice, and the granting of victory. Odin was the god who gave victory to warriors, and Gungnir represented that power.

Archaeological finds from the Migration Period and Viking Age include numerous spearheads deposited in bogs, rivers, and graves as votive offerings, suggesting that spear-dedications to Odin (mirroring his act of hurling Gungnir before battle) were practiced historically.
