HÁRBARÐSLJÓÐ (THE LAY OF HÁRBARÐR)

Hárbarðsljóð (Old Norse: 'The Lay of Hárbarðr'; anglicized as Hárbardsljód, Hárbarthsljóth, and variations) is one of the poems of the Poetic Edda, found in the Codex Regius and AM 748 I 4to manuscripts. It is a flyting poem with figures from Norse Paganism. Hárbarðsljóð was first written down in the late 13th century but may have had an older history as an oral poem.

WHAT IS A FLYTING

A flyting is a ritual verbal contest or exchange of insults. Hárbarðsljóð is an extended flyting between the god Thor and a ferryman named Hárbarðr ("Greybeard").

SYNOPSIS

In this poem, the ferryman Hárbarðr is rude and obnoxious towards Thor, who is returning to Asgard after a journey in Jötunheimr, the land of the jötnar. Hárbarðr obstructs his way and refuses him passage across a swollen river.

Hárbarðr begins by saying that Thor dresses poorly (in a beggar's clothes, without pants) and that his mother is dead. In the course of the poem:
- Hárbarðr boasts of his sexual prowess, his magical and tactical abilities
- Thor responds by telling how he defeated Giants
- Ultimately, after mocking him at length, Hárbarðr curses Thor and tells him to walk around

STRUCTURE

The poem is significantly less structured than most Eddic poems, and is predominantly written in a metric form known as málaháttr or "conversational style." However, other metrical forms are also discernible, while some of the text is pure prose. Several scholars have concluded that the poem is an intentionally stylized version of the traditional flyting structure.

WHO IS HÁRBARÐR?

The identity of the ferryman Hárbarðr has been debated by scholars.

ODIN IDENTIFICATION (DOMINANT VIEW): Hárbarðr repeatedly boasts of conquests of giantesses, as Odin does in Havamal. Most scholars identify Hárbarðr with Odin based on:
- Odin's statement in Grimnismál (stanza 47) that Hárbarð is one of his by-names
- The boastful sexual conquests match Odin's character, not Loki's
- Scholars Finnur Jónsson, Fredrick Sander, and Felix Niedner all support the Odin identification

LOKI IDENTIFICATION (MINORITY VIEW): Early commentators F. W. Bergmann and Viktor Rydberg argued Hárbarð was in fact Loki, because:
- In the opening exchange of insults, Thor addresses the ferryman as one would speak to a youthful servant (inconsistent with Odin's age/dignity)
- Some of Hárbarðr's exploits seem more akin to those of Loki
- Both Loki (in Lokasenna) and Hárbarðr (in Hárbarðsljóð) accuse Thor's wife Sif of adultery — a charge never denied
- They also speak identical half-lines accusing Thor of being unmanly

This theory was rejected by later scholars.

MENTION OF ÞJÁLFI

In the poem, Thor tells Hárbarðr that he fought women in Hlesey (now the island Læsø in Denmark). Hárbarðr says that fighting women is a shameful thing. Thor responds that these females were she-wolves — hardly women at all — and details that they attacked his ship, threatened him with iron clubs, and chased after Þjálfi:

Benjamin Thorpe translation: "She-wolves they were, and scarcely women. / They crushed my ship, which with props I had secured, / with iron clubs threatened me, and drove away Thiâlfi. / What meanwhile didst thou, Harbard?"

Henry Adams Bellows translation: "She-wolves they were like, and women little; / My ship, which well I had trimmed, did they shake; / With clubs of iron they threatened, and Thjalfi they drove off. / What, Harbarth, didst thou the while?"

SIGNIFICANCE

Hárbarðsljóð is notable for presenting a rare portrayal of Thor as an object of mockery and ridicule — the normally triumphant hero unable to cross a simple river when a ferryman refuses him. The poem highlights the contrast between Odin's cunning, magic, and seductive power versus Thor's brute strength and relative simplicity.

The poem also contains Thor's boast about being "Magni's father" (Hárbarðsljóð stanza 53) — one of the kenning attestations for Magni as Thor's son.
