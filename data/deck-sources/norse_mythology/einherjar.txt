EINHERJAR — THE CHOSEN WARRIORS OF VALHALLA

Definition and Etymology
The Einherjar (Old Norse: einherjar, singular einheri) are the warriors who have been chosen by the Valkyries to dwell in Valhalla, Odin's great hall in Asgard, after dying heroic deaths in battle. They constitute Odin's army-in-training for the final apocalyptic battle of Ragnarök.

The etymology of einherjar is debated. The most straightforward interpretation is "those who fight alone" or "lone fighters" (from einn, "one/alone," and herr, "army/warrior"). However, the scholar Rudolf Simek and others have proposed an alternative interpretation: the term may derive from an older compound meaning "those who belong to an army" or "those of the one host," reflecting their collective military role rather than individual combat. A third interpretation connects it to "unique fighter" or "matchless warrior," emphasizing exceptional martial distinction.

Selection by the Valkyries
Warriors do not choose to become einherjar — they are chosen. The Valkyries (from Old Norse valkyrja, "chooser of the slain") are Odin's female warrior-servants who ride across battlefields during conflicts, selecting which warriors will die and which will survive. Those selected for death and deemed worthy are gathered by the Valkyries and escorted to Valhalla.

The Valkyries are described in the Prose Edda and Poetic Edda as beautiful warrior-maidens who ride armored horses through the air and over the seas. Their primary function is to serve as the instruments of Odin's will in battle — not merely deciding who dies, but selecting specifically which fallen warriors Odin wants for his hall.

The selection criteria emphasized exceptional martial prowess, bravery, and a glorious death in combat. Warriors who died of illness, old age, or without fighting went instead to Hel, the realm of the dead presided over by Hel (Loki's daughter). Only battlefield death of the right quality qualified a warrior for Valhalla.

Valhalla — The Hall of the Slain
Valhalla (Old Norse: Valhöll, "hall of the fallen" or "hall of the slain") is located in Asgard, the realm of the Aesir gods. The Prose Edda describes it in considerable detail:

- The hall has 540 doors, each wide enough for 800 warriors to march through side by side. At Ragnarök, these doors will open simultaneously for the einherjar to march out.
- The rafters are made of spear shafts, the roof is thatched with shields, and the benches are strewn with coats of mail.
- The hall is illuminated by swords rather than fire.
- An eagle perches at the top of the hall, and a squirrel runs between it and the roots of Yggdrasil.

Life of the Einherjar: Daily Combat and Feasting
The existence of the einherjar follows an eternal daily cycle, described in the Prose Edda (Gylfaginning):

Each morning, the einherjar arm themselves and engage in combat in the great courtyard outside Valhalla. The fighting is unrestricted — warriors fight until they kill each other or are badly wounded. There is no permanent death in this combat: all wounds heal completely each evening, and fallen warriors are restored to life.

Each evening, the einherjar ride back into Valhalla and sit down together for the feast. The food is the flesh of the great boar Sæhrímnir, who is slaughtered and cooked every day and then miraculously restored to life overnight to be slaughtered again the next day. The cooking of Sæhrímnir is performed by the cook Andhrímnir in the cauldron Eldhrímnir.

The drink is mead that flows from the udders of the goat Heiðrún, who stands on top of Valhalla and feeds on the leaves of the tree Læraðr (identified with Yggdrasil). Heiðrún produces so much mead that she fills a great tun (barrel) every day — enough to keep all the einherjar permanently supplied.

The Valkyries serve the einherjar during the feast, carrying drinking horns and attending to the warriors. They are described as serving both as cup-bearers and as companions to the warriors in the hall.

Purpose: Preparing for Ragnarök
The entire cycle of daily combat and feasting has a single purpose: to prepare the einherjar for the final battle of Ragnarök — the twilight of the gods and the end of the current age of the world.

Odin's motivation for gathering the einherjar is explicitly stated in the sources: he needs the greatest warriors who ever lived as allies when the forces of chaos and destruction break free at Ragnarök. The Poetic Edda poem Vafþrúðnismál includes a passage where Odin (in disguise) learns about Ragnarök, and the einherjar's role in the final battle is made clear.

At Ragnarök, the 540 doors of Valhalla will open simultaneously. Eight hundred warriors will march through each door — giving an implied total of 432,000 einherjar in Odin's army. Despite this massive force, the Norse sources are explicit about the outcome: Odin and most of the gods will fall in the final battle, and with them, many of the einherjar. The einherjar, along with Odin himself, will fall during this cataclysmic event.

Odin will be swallowed by the wolf Fenrir. Thor will kill the Midgard Serpent (Jörmungandr) but will die from its venom after taking nine steps. Freyr, lacking his sword (which he gave away to win Gerðr), will be killed by the fire giant Surtr. Despite the finest warriors from all of human history fighting at their side, the gods will ultimately lose.

This foreknowledge of defeat makes the einherjar concept uniquely poignant: Odin is collecting warriors not to win but because it is his nature to prepare and to fight, even knowing the outcome. The Norse mythological framework embraces this tragic determination as a virtue.

The Einherjar as Cultural Ideal
For Viking Age warriors, the prospect of being chosen as einherjar represented the highest possible honor and the most desirable afterlife. Dying in battle was therefore not to be feared but sought — it was the path to Valhalla, eternal combat, excellent feasting, and the companionship of the greatest warriors who had ever lived.

This belief system served as a powerful motivating force, especially before battles. The Viking warrior who welcomed death in combat was acting rationally within his cultural framework: ordinary death meant Hel, while battlefield death meant potentially being chosen for Valhalla.

The einherjar ideal also reinforced the warrior code: bravery was not merely socially admired but had cosmological consequences. Cowardice and dying without fighting excluded a warrior from the ranks of the einherjar and condemned them to the gray, undistinguished realm of Hel.

Textual Sources

Gylfaginning (Prose Edda, Snorri Sturluson, 13th century)
The most detailed description of the einherjar, their daily life, Valhalla's structure, Sæhrímnir, Heiðrún, and the purpose of their gathering.

Grímnismál (Poetic Edda)
Odin (in disguise as Grímnir) describes Valhalla and the einherjar's activities: "Five hundred doors and forty more, I think, are in Valhalla; eight hundred warriors will pass through each door at one time when they go to fight the wolf."

Vafþrúðnismál (Poetic Edda)
A wisdom contest between Odin and the giant Vafþrúðnir, touching on Ragnarök and the fate of the einherjar.

Hákonarmál
A skaldic poem by Eyvindr skáldaspillir (10th century) composed in honor of the Norwegian king Hákon the Good after his death in battle. The poem describes Hákon being welcomed to Valhalla by Odin's einherjar and Odin himself, representing one of the earliest attestations of the einherjar concept in praise poetry.

Eiríksmál
Another 10th-century skaldic poem describing the reception of a fallen king (Eirik Bloodaxe) into Valhalla, with Odin welcoming the new warrior to join the einherjar.
