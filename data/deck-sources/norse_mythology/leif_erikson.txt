Leif Erikson, also known as Leif the Lucky (c. 970s – c. 1018 to 1025), was a Norse explorer who is thought to have been the first European to set foot on continental America, approximately half a millennium before Christopher Columbus. According to the sagas of Icelanders, he established a Norse settlement at Vinland, which is usually interpreted as being coastal North America.

EARLY LIFE AND FAMILY

Leif was the son of Erik the Red and his wife Thjodhild (Old Norse: Þjóðhildur), and through his paternal line the grandson of Thorvald Ásvaldsson. His father Erik the Red, when young, had his father banished from Norway for manslaughter, and the family went into exile in Iceland (which had been colonized by Norsemen, mainly from Norway, in the century preceding Leif's birth). Leif was also a distant relative of Naddodd, who discovered Iceland.

Leif's year of birth is estimated in the c. 970s. He was likely born in Iceland, possibly at Haukadalur Valley, where his mother's family was based. Erik the Red was later banished from Iceland and sailed west to a place he named Greenland. He briefly returned to Iceland to bring his family and other colonists back to Greenland, establishing its first permanent settlement in 986. Leif grew up on the family estate Brattahlíð in the Eastern Settlement of Greenland. He had two brothers, Thorstein and Thorvald, and a sister, Freydís. Tyrker, one of Erik's thralls, had been specially trusted to keep charge of Erik's children, and Leif later referred to him as his "foster father."

DISCOVERING VINLAND

The Saga of Erik the Red and the Saga of the Greenlanders (both written around 1200) contain detailed but sometimes conflicting accounts of the voyages to Vinland. The only known strictly historical mentions of Vinland appear in Adam of Bremen's historical treatise (c. 1075) and in the Book of Icelanders (c. 1122) by Ari the Wise.

Account in the Saga of Erik the Red

According to this saga, Leif discovered Vinland after being blown off course on his way from Norway to Greenland. Before this voyage, Leif had spent time at the court of Norwegian King Olaf Tryggvason, where he had converted to Christianity. After arriving at an unknown shore, the crew disembarked and explored the area. They found wild grapes, self-sown wheat, and maple trees. Afterwards, they loaded their ship with samples of these goods and sailed east to Greenland, rescuing a group of shipwrecked sailors along the way. For this act, and for converting Norse Greenland to Christianity, Leif earned the nickname "Leif the Lucky."

Account in the Saga of the Greenlanders

According to this saga, Leif was not the first European to discover Vinland. Instead Bjarni Herjólfsson and his crew, on a voyage from Iceland to Greenland, were overtaken by wind and fog, missed the southern tip of Greenland, and encountered an unknown coast. They did not disembark but continued to sail and found two additional coasts. Roughly 15 years later, Leif approached Bjarni, purchased his ship, gathered a crew of thirty-five men, and mounted an expedition towards the lands Bjarni had described.

Leif's father Erik was set to join him but dropped out after falling from his horse (interpreted as a bad omen). Leif followed Bjarni's route in reverse and landed first in a rocky, desolate place he named Helluland (Flat-Rock Land; possibly Baffin Island or northern Labrador). After venturing further, he landed in a forested place he named Markland (Forest Land; possibly near Cape Porcupine, Labrador). After two more days at sea, he landed on an island to the north (possibly Belle Isle), then returned to the mainland past a cape on the north side (perhaps Cape Bauld). They sailed west and landed in a verdant area with a mild climate and plentiful salmon. As winter approached, he encamped there and sent out parties to explore. Tyrker discovered that the land was full of vines and grapes. Leif therefore named the land Vinland ('Wineland'). There he and his crew built a small settlement called Leifsbudir (Leif's Booths) by later visitors. After wintering over, Leif returned to Greenland in the spring with a cargo of grapes and timber. On the return voyage, he rescued an Icelandic castaway and his crew, earning the nickname "Leif the Lucky."

ARCHAEOLOGICAL EVIDENCE

Research in the early 1960s by Norwegian explorer Helge Ingstad and his wife, archaeologist Anne Stine Ingstad, identified a Norse site at the northern tip of Newfoundland — L'Anse aux Meadows. Carbon dating estimates 990–1050 CE; tree-ring analysis dates to the year 1021. It has been suggested this could be Leifsbudir. Later archaeological evidence suggests Vinland may have been the areas around the Gulf of St. Lawrence and that L'Anse aux Meadows was a ship repair station and waypoint. The Saga of Erik the Red mentions two other settlements in Vinland: Straumfjǫrðr (beyond Kjalarnes promontory and the Wonderstrands) and Hóp (located even farther south).

PERSONAL LIFE AND CONVERSION

Leif went to Norway likely to serve as a retainer to King Olaf Tryggvason. On the journey to Norway, Leif's ship was driven to the Hebrides where he remained for much of the summer. There he fell in love with a noblewoman, Thorgunna, who gave birth to their son Thorgils. Thorgils was later sent to Leif in Greenland but did not become popular. After arriving at the Norwegian court, Leif was converted to Christianity. King Olaf Tryggvason commissioned Leif to return to Greenland to convert the settlers. Leif's father Erik reacted coldly to the suggestion to abandon his religion, while his mother Thjóðhildr became a Christian and built a church called Thjóðhild's Church.

CHIEFTAINCY AND DEATH

The winter following Leif's return from Vinland, his father Erik died (shortly after 1000 CE), making Leif paramount chief in Greenland. Leif is last mentioned alive in 1018 in the Saga of St. Olaf. According to The Saga of the Sworn Brothers, by 1025 the chieftaincy of Eiríksfjǫrðr had passed to his son Thorkell. He probably died in Greenland sometime between these dates.

LEGACY AND COMMEMORATION

In 1925, President Calvin Coolidge stated at the Minnesota State Fair to a crowd of 100,000 people that Leif had indeed been the first European to discover America. In 1929, the Wisconsin Legislature passed a bill to make 9 October "Leif Erikson Day." The U.S. Congress authorized 9 October as the annual "Leif Erikson Day" in 1964. In 1930, a statue of Leif was erected in Reykjavík, Iceland (currently situated in front of Hallgrímskirkja) as a gift from the United States to Iceland, commemorating the 1,000 year anniversary of Alþingi.

KEY FACTS

- Born: c. 970s (likely Iceland)
- Died: c. 1018–1025 (Greenland)
- Father: Erik the Red (founded first Norse settlement in Greenland, 986)
- Mother: Thjodhild
- Brothers: Thorstein, Thorvald
- Sister: Freydís
- Foster father: Tyrker
- Sons: Thorgils (born in the Hebrides), Thorkell (succeeded as Greenland's chieftain)
- Religion: Converted to Christianity at Norwegian court of King Olaf Tryggvason
- Nickname: "Leif the Lucky" — earned for rescuing shipwrecked sailors
- Lands named: Helluland (Flat-Rock Land), Markland (Forest Land), Vinland (Wineland)
- Settlement: Leifsbudir in Vinland; L'Anse aux Meadows in Newfoundland (dated 990–1050 CE) is the likely archaeological site
- Primary sources: Saga of Erik the Red, Saga of the Greenlanders (both c. 1200)
