In Norse mythology, Andvaranaut (12th c. Old Norse: [ˈɑndˌwɑrɑˌnɔut]), meaning Andvari's Gem ("Andvari's precious possession"), is a magic ring, initially owned by Andvari, that could help with finding sources of gold. Andvaranaut is mainly a cursed treasure, but according to scholars, it forms part of a bigger tradition of objects that are divinely and supernaturally empowered in Norse mythology. Such items held cultural and religious significance and often mediated relationships between gods, dwarves, and heroic figures.

The mischievous god Loki stole Andvari's treasure and the ring. In revenge, Andvari cursed the ring to bring misfortune and destruction to whoever possessed it. Loki quickly gave the cursed Andvaranaut to Hreidmar, King of the Dwarves, as reparation for having inadvertently killed Hreidmar's son, Ótr. Ótr's brother, Fafnir, then murdered Hreidmar and took the ring, turning into a dragon to guard it. Sigurd (Siegfried) later killed Fafnir and gave Andvaranaut to Brynhildr (Brünnehilde). Queen Grimhild of the Nibelungs then manipulated Sigurd and Brynhildr into marrying her children, bringing Andvaranaut's curse into her family.

== Key Figures in the Andvaranaut Myth ==

- Andvari: A dwarf who originally owned the ring. He possessed it to help find sources of gold. When Loki stole his treasure including the ring, Andvari cursed it.
- Loki: The mischievous god who stole Andvari's treasure and ring. He passed the curse on by giving the ring to Hreidmar as weregild (reparation) for the killing of Ótr.
- Hreidmar: King of the Dwarves. He demanded the ring as payment for the accidental killing of his son Ótr. He was later murdered by his son Fafnir, who coveted the treasure.
- Ótr: Son of Hreidmar, accidentally killed by Loki. His death set in motion the chain of events surrounding the cursed ring.
- Fafnir: Another son of Hreidmar. He murdered his father to gain the cursed ring and treasure, then transformed into a dragon to guard his hoard.
- Sigurd (also known as Siegfried): The hero who slew the dragon Fafnir and claimed the treasure, including Andvaranaut. He later gave the ring to Brynhildr.
- Brynhildr (also known as Brünnehilde): A shieldmaiden/valkyrie to whom Sigurd gave the ring. She was later manipulated by Queen Grimhild into a tragic marriage arrangement.
- Grimhild: Queen of the Nibelungs. She used the ring's curse to manipulate both Sigurd and Brynhildr into marrying her children, perpetuating the ring's destructive influence.

== The Curse ==

When Andvari's treasure was stolen by Loki, Andvari cursed the ring so that it would bring misfortune and destruction to all who possessed it. The curse passed from Hreidmar (murdered by his son) to Fafnir (slain by Sigurd) to Sigurd himself and ultimately to Brynhildr and the Nibelung family. Each owner met ruin, fulfilling the curse's promise.

== Literary References ==

Richard Wagner used Andvaranaut as inspiration for the title of his musical drama Der Ring des Nibelungen. J.R.R. Tolkien may have been inspired by Andvaranaut when designing the One Ring, both by making the One Ring cursed and by making one of its aspects to allow the wearer to find the other Rings of Power, knowing the location of the wearer of each of the Rings of Power, so that the wearer of the One Ring could control these Ringbearers. Modern scholarship emphasizes how Tolkien reimagines Andvaranaut in his poem cycle Sigurd & Guðrún by drawing upon, yet reworking, the incidents of the Völsunga saga. A comparative study demonstrates how the themes attendant on the ring were revised by Tolkien to correspond with contemporary literary interest while retaining key mythological components.

== Source Texts ==

The Andvaranaut story appears in the Völsunga saga and the Poetic Edda (notably the Reginsmál, Fáfnismál, and Sigrdrífumál poems), and in the Prose Edda by Snorri Sturluson. These texts date to the 13th century but preserve earlier oral tradition. The curse on Andvaranaut is closely comparable to the curse placed on the sword Tyrfing in the Tyrfing Cycle (Hervarar saga).
