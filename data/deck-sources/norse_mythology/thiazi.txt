THIAZI (ÞJAZI) — NORSE GIANT

Identity and Nature
Thiazi (also spelled Þjazi, Thjazi, Tjasse) is a jötunn (giant) in Norse mythology. He possessed the ability to shapeshift into an eagle form. He is one of the most significant giants in the mythology due to his role in the abduction of Idunn and the consequences that followed for the gods of Asgard.

Family
Thiazi's father was the giant Ölvaldi. He had two brothers: Idi and Gangr. When Ölvaldi died, the three brothers divided their inheritance by each taking a mouthful of gold. Thiazi fathered Skadi, a giantess who later became associated with winter, skiing, hunting, and mountains. After Thiazi's death, Skadi would become a goddess and marry the sea god Njord.

Thrymheim — The Mountain Hall
Thiazi's home was called Thrymheim ("Thunder Home" or "Noise Home"), a hall located high in the mountains of Jötunheim. Skadi later inherited Thrymheim after her father's death and continued to live there, as she preferred the mountains to the seashore home of her husband Njord.

The Encounter with Loki, Odin, and Hœnir
The primary myth involving Thiazi begins when Odin, Loki, and Hœnir (also written as Honir) were traveling together. Hungry, they attempted to roast an ox over a fire, but the meat would not cook. They discovered a large eagle perched in an oak tree above them — this was Thiazi in disguise.

The eagle offered to use his power to make the meat cook properly, in exchange for receiving a portion of the food. The gods agreed. However, when the ox was ready, Thiazi took the two haunches and both shoulders, leaving very little for the gods. This infuriated Loki, who grabbed a large staff or pole and struck at the eagle.

The staff became magically stuck to Thiazi's feathers, and Loki's hands became stuck to the staff. Thiazi then flew high into the sky, dragging Loki helplessly behind him. He flew close to the ground and over rocks and trees, scraping Loki painfully. When Loki begged to be released, Thiazi agreed — but only if Loki would swear an oath to bring the goddess Idunn out of Asgard and deliver her into the giant's hands.

The Abduction of Idunn
Idunn was the goddess who guarded the magical apples that kept the Aesir gods perpetually young. Without her apples, the gods would age and eventually die. Loki, bound by his oath, deceived Idunn by claiming he had found similar fruits growing in the forest outside Asgard, and he persuaded her to bring her own apples for comparison.

When Idunn left Asgard with Loki, Thiazi swooped down in his eagle form and seized her, carrying her away to Thrymheim in Jötunheim.

Consequences for the Gods
Without Idunn's apples, the Aesir gods began to age rapidly. Their hair turned grey, their skin grew wrinkled, and their strength waned. Realizing Loki was the last one to be seen with Idunn, the gods threatened him with torture and death unless he retrieved her.

Loki borrowed the falcon-feather cloak (sometimes described as a hawk-feather cloak) belonging to the goddess Freyja and flew to Jötunheim in the form of a falcon.

The Rescue
Loki arrived at Thrymheim when Thiazi happened to be away at sea fishing. Finding Idunn alone, Loki transformed her into a nut and carried her in his talons, flying swiftly back toward Asgard.

When Thiazi returned and discovered Idunn missing, he put on his eagle shape and gave chase. The gods could see from Asgard that a falcon carrying a nut was being pursued by an eagle. They gathered wood shavings and kindling at the walls of Asgard and, the moment the falcon flew inside the walls, set the pile ablaze.

Thiazi's great eagle wings caught fire as he crossed into Asgard. He fell to the ground inside the walls and was killed by the gods. The Prose Edda, specifically Skáldskaparmál, records that the gods killed Thiazi inside Asgard after his wings caught fire.

Aftermath and Skadi's Vengeance
After Thiazi's death, his daughter Skadi put on her armor and weapons and traveled to Asgard demanding compensation for her father's death. Rather than fight, the Aesir offered to make amends. The compensation they offered included:

1. The right to choose a husband from among the gods — but she could only see their feet when making her selection. Skadi chose the pair of feet she thought most beautiful, hoping to choose Baldr. But the beautiful feet belonged to Njord, the sea god, and he became her husband.

2. The gods also made her laugh, which was part of the compensation. Loki achieved this by tying one end of a rope to a goat and the other end to his own genitals. Their mutual struggles and cries eventually made Skadi laugh.

3. Odin took Thiazi's eyes and threw them up into the sky, where they became stars. This act was a form of honor paid to the slain giant.

Kennings and Poetic References
In Old Norse skaldic poetry, kennings (poetic circumlocutions) reference Thiazi's story. Gold was sometimes called "the mouth-tale of Ölvaldi's sons" (a reference to how Thiazi and his brothers divided their inheritance by mouthfuls of gold). Idunn is referenced in kennings relating to Thiazi: she could be called "Thiazi's booty" or similar terms relating to the abduction.

The story is told in the Prose Edda (Skáldskaparmál) and referenced in the Poetic Edda poem Hárbarðsljóð, where Odin (in disguise as Harbard) boasts to Thor about how Thiazi was killed. The myth also appears in the skaldic poem Haustlöng by the 9th-century Norwegian skald Þjóðólfr of Hvinir, making it one of the earliest attested Norse myths.

Haustlöng Attribution
The Haustlöng of Þjóðólfr of Hvinir is among the oldest sources for the Thiazi myth, composed in the late 9th century. It describes scenes from the myth that were apparently depicted on a shield given as a gift, making the Thiazi narrative one of the most ancient attested mythological stories in Norse tradition. The skald describes the eagle pursuit and the fire at Asgard's walls in vivid detail.

Sources
The myth appears primarily in:
- Skáldskaparmál (Prose Edda, by Snorri Sturluson, 13th century)
- Gylfaginning (Prose Edda) — passing references
- Hárbarðsljóð (Poetic Edda) — brief reference
- Haustlöng by Þjóðólfr of Hvinir (9th century skaldic poem)
