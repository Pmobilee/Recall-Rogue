DRAUGR — NORSE UNDEAD

Definition and Names
The draugr (plural: draugar; also spelled draugur, dreygur, or draugen) is an undead being from Scandinavian mythology, sagas, and folklore. Unlike ghosts, which are typically ethereal or spiritual, the draugr is a revenant — a physical, corporeal undead creature; a reanimated corpse that continues to inhabit and move within its own grave or burial mound.

The term draugr is used broadly in medieval Norse literature, though the texts sometimes employ more specific terminology. Related terms include:
- Haugbúi ("barrow-dweller" or "mound-dweller"): A category of undead that stays within its burial mound, often described as non-violent and sometimes helpful to those who treat it respectfully
- Aptrganga ("again-walker" or "re-walker"): A term emphasizing the creature's ability to walk again after death; used interchangeably with draugr in some texts
- Draugen: The sea-variant of the draugr in later Scandinavian folklore, associated with those who drowned

The word draugr may derive from a root related to Swedish drög, meaning "a pale, ineffectual, slow-minded person that drags himself along," though scholarly opinion varies on the etymology.

Physical Characteristics
Draugar are distinguished by specific physical traits described in the sagas:

- Corporeal form: They possess their original bodies, not spiritual projections. They can wrestle, carry objects, and leave physical marks.
- Unnatural size: Many draugar are described as having grown to monstrous proportions after death — sometimes as large as an ox or a great bull. The saga account of Glámr in Grettis saga describes him as "livid and swollen to the size of a bullock."
- Disturbing features: Glámr is described with "staring glassy eyes," "purple lips," and a tongue bitten through and protruding. The skin of draugar often takes on a darkened or bluish-black coloration (corpse-blue), sometimes described as the "hue of death."
- Odor: Draugar are consistently associated with a terrible stench, the smell of decomposition.
- Weight: They are described as supernaturally heavy, pressing down on rooftops and crushing people with their bulk.

Abilities and Powers
Draugar possess a range of supernatural abilities that make them dangerous:

- Superhuman strength: Strong enough to kill horses, cattle, and men with their bare hands
- Shapeshifting: Some draugar can transform into other forms, including animals, to deceive or attack enemies
- Weather magic: Certain draugar can cause snowstorms, blizzards, and other meteorological events
- Cursing: A draugr who is defeated in combat may still curse its victor with misfortune. This is central to the Glámr episode in Grettis saga.
- Disease and madness: Prolonged proximity to or conflict with a draugr can bring illness, nightmares, and psychological damage to survivors
- Propagation: Those killed by a draugr may themselves rise as draugar, creating a chain of undead
- Riding rooftops: Some accounts describe draugar riding atop farm buildings ("riding the roof"), causing structural damage and frightening inhabitants

Creation and Origin
Draugar arise from the dead for several reasons identified in the sagas:

1. Improper burial or lack of burial rites
2. Dying in a state of spiritual unrest — including vengeance unfulfilled, oaths broken, or grievances unaddressed
3. Extreme greed or attachment to possessions in life, leading the dead to guard their treasures from within the grave
4. Dying with evil character — those who were wicked, malicious, or violated sacred customs
5. Being slain by a draugr oneself

The case of Glámr in Grettis saga illustrates one specific origin: Glámr was a Swedish farmhand who refused to observe proper Christian fasting customs on Christmas Eve. He was subsequently found dead under mysterious circumstances (having apparently been killed by a draugr or malevolent entity) and returned as a violent undead, killing livestock and farmhands.

Famous Saga Examples

Glámr (Grettis saga / Grettir's Saga)
The most famous draugr in Norse literature. Glámr was a large, disagreeable Swedish farmhand hired to tend sheep on a haunted farm in Iceland. He violated Christmas fasting customs, disappeared one night, and was found dead the next day. His burial was difficult — the earth would not hold him properly. He returned as a draugr of enormous size, riding rooftops and crushing livestock and workers to death.

The hero Grettir Ásmundarson eventually grappled with Glámr in a prolonged wrestling match inside a farmhouse that ended outside in the snow. Grettir managed to pin Glámr and cut off his head. However, in the moment before death, Glámr's eyes caught the moonlight and Glámr spoke a curse: Grettir would always see those terrible eyes in the dark and would be haunted by darkness and fear for the rest of his life. Grettir won the fight but was diminished by it, eventually becoming the longest-proscribed outlaw in Icelandic history, his strength undermined by perpetual fear.

Kárr inn gamli / Kárr the Old (Grettis saga)
An elderly chieftain's father who continues to guard his burial mound and its treasures after death. Grettir breaks into the mound (a practice called haugbrott, "mound-breaking"), confronts the draugr in hand-to-hand combat, and defeats him, claiming an heirloom sword from the treasure. This episode demonstrates the draugr as primarily a guardian of grave goods, less malevolent than Glámr but still lethal to those who disturb the mound.

Eyrbyggja Saga
Contains an extended episode known as the "Froda haunting" involving multiple draugar who return to plague a household, sitting by the fire and interacting with the living. The haunting is eventually resolved through a formal legal hearing held against the dead — a remarkable detail showing the Norse could bring legal proceedings against their undead.

Egils Saga
References draugr encounters and mound-breaking episodes, with Egill Skallagrímsson interacting with burial mounds in ways that have draugr implications.

The Haugbúi Distinction
The haugbúi ("barrow-dweller") is often distinguished from the more aggressive draugr. The haugbúi typically remains within its burial mound and guards the treasures buried with it. Unlike the wandering draugr, the haugbúi generally will not leave its mound to attack the living unprovoked. Haugbúar are often described as looking much as they did in life and are sometimes portrayed as non-violent or even helpful to descendants who show proper respect.

The distinction was not always consistently maintained in the sagas — the terms overlap, and context determines whether a given encounter involves a wandering terror or a stationary guardian.

Haugbrott — Mound-Breaking
The act of breaking into burial mounds (haugbrott) was a recognized if dangerous activity in Norse society. Motivations included: treasure-seeking, recovering heirlooms belonging to one's family, and in some accounts, ritual purposes. The draugr and haugbúi narratives functioned partly as cautionary tales discouraging grave robbery — disturbing the dead had consequences. The "Reimleik motive" identified by scholars refers to the practice of burying the dead with heavy stones or wooden stakes to physically prevent resurrection.

Destruction Methods
Various methods appear in the sagas for permanently ending a draugr's threat:

- Decapitation: Cutting off the head, often combined with placing it at the feet or buttocks of the corpse to prevent reanimation
- Burning: Cremating the body eliminates the physical vessel for the draugr
- Nídstöng (Nithing-pole): A curse-pole erected against the draugr or its patron
- Physical combat and wrestling the corpse back into the grave
- Calling the draugr by name in certain ritualistic ways
- Religious rites: In later, Christianized accounts, priests could dispel draugar through religious means

Modern Cultural Influence
The draugr concept has had significant influence on modern fantasy gaming and fiction. They appear in the Elder Scrolls video game series (particularly Skyrim, 2011), which portrays them as undead Norse warriors guarding ancient tombs. The concept also influenced the creation of similar undead archetypes in fantasy literature and games.
