LOKASENNA — LOKI'S FLYTING

Title and Basic Information
Lokasenna (Old Norse: "Loki's Quarrel" or "Loki's Truth-Telling," sometimes translated as "The Flyting of Loki") is an Eddic poem found in the Codex Regius, a medieval Icelandic manuscript compiled around 1270 CE. The poem belongs to the Poetic (Elder) Edda. While the linguistic traditions underlying Norse mythology extend back as far as the 9th century, the Lokasenna is considered a relatively late composition, dated by scholars to approximately 1200 CE. It may have been modeled after or influenced by Lucian's Assembly of the Gods from classical antiquity.

Literary Genre: Flyting
Lokasenna exemplifies the flyting tradition — a Scandinavian and broader Germanic literary practice of ritualized verbal combat and exchange of insults between characters. The poem comprises Loki's extended exchange of accusations, taunts, and insults with twelve gods and two servants at a divine feast. The Codex Regius text includes distinctive markings featuring the first letters of the gods' names, and prose additions that scholars believe may indicate the piece was "dramatically performed" in its original context.

The Setting: Aegir's Feast
The poem opens with a prose introduction describing a grand gathering of gods, goddesses, and elves assembled in the hall of Aegir, the sea personification. According to the Prose Edda, Aegir's hall was located on the island Hlesey (modern Danish Læsø). The setting is described as majestic and peaceful, with glittering gold illuminating the hall and ale serving itself to the guests. Thor is notably absent, having traveled eastward to deal with giants.

Two servants attend the feast: Fimafeng and Eldir. Loki's entrance proves immediately disruptive — jealous of the praise directed at Fimafeng for his excellent service, Loki kills him. The assembled gods respond by shaking their shields and driving Loki away.

Loki's Return and Entry
After being expelled, Loki encounters Eldir outside and interrogates him about the conversation inside. Eldir warns that the gods are speaking of their martial deeds and that Loki would find no friends there. Loki declares his intent to bring "slander and hatred" to the gathering and forces his way back in.

Inside, he demands either a seat or that someone tell him to leave. Bragi (god of poetry and eloquence) refuses him directly, stating the gods know exactly who they want at their feast. Loki then invokes his ancient blood-brotherhood with Odin — the two had mixed their blood and sworn to share hospitality. Odin is compelled by this oath to honor it. He instructs his son Vidar to move aside so that Loki may take a seat.

The Structure of the Insults
From stanza 17 onward, a refrain of "Silence!" structures the poem as Loki works through the assembled gods. The insults fall into several recurring categories: accusations of sexual impropriety (primarily directed at goddesses), charges of cowardice (directed at male gods), accusations of practicing seiðr magic (an unmanliness charge against Odin), and references to specific mythological incidents known from other sources.

Loki's Accusations, God by God

Against Bragi: Loki accuses the poetry god of cowardice, calling him an "adorner of benches" — implying he prefers sitting to fighting. Bragi offers a sword, rings, and an ox as compensation to leave, but Loki dismisses him.

Against Idunn (wife of Bragi): The goddess who guards the apples of immortality is accused by Loki of being "most lustful for men," making her one of many women at the feast whom Loki accuses of sexual misconduct. She attempts to calm Bragi's anger and does not directly respond to the insult.

Against Gefjun: Loki accuses this goddess of sexual impropriety, claiming a "fair boy" seduced her with a necklace and gifts.

Against Odin: Loki accuses Odin of practicing dark magic (seiðr) on the island Samsø, claiming he performed rituals "in witch's guise among men." This is among the gravest insults possible — the practice of seiðr was considered unmanning for men. Odin counter-insults Loki by pointing out that Loki once lived as a milkmaid beneath the earth for eight years and bore children — referencing his transformation into a mare to father Sleipnir.

Against Frigg (Odin's wife): Loki accuses Frigg of having slept with Odin's brothers Vili and Vé during a period when Odin was away. Notably, this same incident is corroborated in Snorri Sturluson's Ynglinga saga, suggesting it was a recognized mythological tradition.

Against Freyja: Loki accuses Freyja of having slept with every god and elf in the hall, including her own brother Freyr. He also references the myth in which she wept tears of gold when Oðr (her husband) departed.

Against Njord: Loki insults Njord for his origin as a Vanir god (considered inferior by the Aesir in Loki's framing) and makes crude references to Njord's past humiliation by the daughters of the frost-giant Hymir. When Njord points to his son Freyr as a defense, Loki responds that Freyr was born of Njord's relationship with his own sister — incest being a Vanir tradition.

Against Tyr: Loki mocks the one-handed battle god regarding his sacrifice of his hand to bind the wolf Fenrir. Tyr acknowledges that both have paid losses, but Loki counters by claiming he fathered a son with Tyr's wife.

Against Freyr: This is a strategically significant insult. Loki points out that Freyr gave away his magical sword to the giant Skírnir as payment for wooing the giantess Gerðr on his behalf. Without his sword, Freyr will have no weapon at Ragnarök when Surtr comes. This prophecy — that Freyr's disarmament leads to his death — is confirmed in other Eddic sources. Freyr's servant Byggvir receives mockery about cowardice and hiding beneath a millstone.

Against Heimdall: Loki mocks the divine watchman for his perpetual vigilance, saying he endures "an ugly fate" standing wet-backed as guardian of the gods. Heimdall offers wise advice about the dangers of drinking too much, but Loki dismisses him.

Against Skadi: The giantess-goddess of winter warns Loki that his loose tongue will be stilled when the gods bind him. Loki taunts her by boasting that he was the one who guided the mistletoe into the blind god Höðr's hand, causing the death of Baldr — an act Skadi's father Thiazi was indirectly connected to through consequences. Skadi responds coldly: "from my holy places, only cold counsel shall come for you."

Against Sif (Thor's wife): Sif attempts to placate Loki by pouring mead for him and asking to be spared from insults. Loki responds by claiming he seduced her in secret.

The Language of Gender and Honor
The Lokasenna poem reveals important aspects of Norse social values. The gravest male insult was argr or ragr — meaning effeminate, unmanly, cowardly, or sexually passive in a feminine role. This insult was "completely out of the question in an extremely masculine society like the Norse." Women, by contrast, are primarily accused of lustful behavior and sexual promiscuity. The poem demonstrates stricter gender roles than popular culture often imagines for the Norse.

Thor's Arrival and Resolution
The servant Beyla foretells Thor's return before he arrives. Thor enters dramatically, brandishing Mjolnir and calling Loki "an unmanly creature." He threatens to hurl Loki to the east, into the realm of giants where none of the gods could see him. Loki makes one final taunt about an incident in which Thor hid inside the thumb of the giant Skrymir's glove, but when Thor raises his hammer again, Loki concedes. He declares he has spoken his mind and turns a final bitter remark at Aegir, predicting fire will consume his hall.

The Binding
The prose epilogue describes Loki's punishment following the feast. The gods pursue and capture him. They bind him with the entrails of his own son Váli, who was transformed into a wolf and killed his brother Narfi. The entrails transformed into iron fetters once placed on Loki. Skadi placed a serpent above Loki that drips venom onto his face. His wife Sigyn holds a bowl to catch the poison, but when she goes to empty it, drops fall on Loki's face, causing his writhing — which produces earthquakes.

Snorri Sturluson's Prose Edda offers an alternative account: Loki's binding resulted primarily from his role in Baldr's death rather than the feast's insults alone.

Cultural Significance
The poem mirrors essential aspects of Norse society: the obligation of hospitality (Odin cannot refuse Loki despite wanting to), the culture of manliness and fear of effeminacy, the practice of seiðr and its gender associations, and the high importance placed on verbal skill and the composition of verses. Many accusations Loki makes are corroborated by other Eddic and saga sources, suggesting the poem drew on a shared mythological tradition rather than inventing its content wholesale.

Sources

- Poetic Edda (collection containing Lokasenna)
- Prose Edda by Snorri Sturluson — corroborates several accusations (Frigg and Vili/Vé, Freyr's sword, Loki's binding)
- Ynglinga saga — confirms the Frigg/Vili/Vé tradition
