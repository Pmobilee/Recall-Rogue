HEIMDALL (HEIMDALLR)

In Norse mythology, Heimdall (from Old Norse Heimdallr; modern Icelandic Heimdallur) is a god. He is the son of Odin and nine sisters. Heimdall keeps watch for invaders and the onset of Ragnarok from his dwelling Himinbjorg, where the burning rainbow bridge Bifrost meets the sky. He is attested as possessing foreknowledge and keen senses, particularly eyesight and hearing.

The god and his possessions are described in enigmatic manners. Heimdall is golden-toothed, "the head is called his sword", and he is "the whitest of the gods."

Heimdall possesses the resounding horn Gjallarhorn and the golden-maned horse Gulltoppr, along with a store of mead at his dwelling. He is said to be the originator of social classes among humanity.

Heimdallr is also known as Rig, Hallinskidi, Gullintanni, and Vindler or Vindhler.

The antagonistic relationship between Heimdall and Loki is notable — they are foretold to kill one another during Ragnarok. Heimdall once recovered Freyja's treasured possession Brisingamen while battling Loki in the shape of a seal.

Two lines of an otherwise lost poem about the god, Heimdalargaldr, survive.

NAMES AND ETYMOLOGY

The etymology of the name Heimdall is obscure, but 'the one who illuminates the world' has been proposed. Heimdallr may be connected to Mardoll, one of Freyja's names.

His three other attested names:
- Hallinskidi: obscure meaning, subject of much scholarly debate
- Gullintanni: literally 'the one with the golden teeth'
- Vindler (or Vindhler): translates as either 'the one protecting against the wind' or 'wind-sea'

SALTFLEETBY SPINDLE-WHORL INSCRIPTION

Heimdallr is likely mentioned on the Saltfleetby spindle-whorl, a lead spindle whorl bearing an Old Norse Younger Futhark inscription discovered in Saltfleetby, Lincolnshire, England in 2010. Dated from the 10th to 12th centuries CE, it likely records a charm asking for help from Odin, Heimdallr, and a figure whose name resembles Tjalfi (one of Thor's servants).

POETIC EDDA ATTESTATIONS

Heimdall is attested in six Poetic Edda poems: Voluspa, Grimnismal, Lokasenna, Thrymskvidha, Rigspula, and Hrafnagaldr Odhins.

VOLUSPA

Heimdall is mentioned three times in Voluspa. In the opening stanza, the undead volva calls for silence and refers to all humans as "Heimdall's sons":

Benjamin Thorpe translation: "For silence I pray all sacred children, / great and small, sons of Heimdall. / they will that I Valfather's deeds recount, / men's ancient saws, those that I best remember."

Henry Adams Bellows translation: "Hearing I ask from the holy races, / From Heimdall's sons, both high and low; / Thou wilt, Valfather, that well I relate. / Old tales I remember of men long ago."

This stanza has led to various scholarly interpretations. The notion of humanity as "Heimdall's sons" connects to the prose introduction of Rigspula, where Heimdall is said to have once gone about among people, slept between couples, and so doled out classes among them.

Later in Voluspa, Heimdall blows Gjallarhorn at Ragnarok:
- Benjamin Thorpe: "Loud blows Heimdall, his horn is raised; Odin speaks with Mim's head."
- Henry Adams Bellows: "Loud blows Heimdall, the horn is aloft, In fear quake all who on Hel-roads are."

Another Voluspa stanza refers to Heimdall's "hearing" or "horn" (Old Norse hljod) hidden under the world tree. Scholar Carolyne Larrington suggests that if "hearing" is the correct reading, Heimdall — like Odin — sacrificed a body part (his ear) for wisdom at the cosmic well. "Odin exchanged one of his eyes for wisdom from Mimir, guardian of the well, while Heimdall seems to have forfeited his ear."

GRIMNISMAL

Odin, disguised as Grimnir, mentions Himinbjorg (Heimdall's dwelling) as the eighth cosmic location:

Benjamin Thorpe translation: "Himinbiorg is the eighth, where Heimdall, / it is said, rules o'er the holy fanes: / there the gods' watchman, in his tranquil home, / drinks joyful the good mead."

Henry Adams Bellows: "Himingbjorg is the eighth, and Heimdall there / O'er men holds sway, it is said; / In his well-built house does the warder of heaven / The good mead gladly drink."

THRYMSKVIDHA

When the gods gather to discuss how to recover Thor's stolen hammer from the giants, Heimdall advises dressing Thor in bridal garments to disguise him as Freyja. He is here described as hvitastr asa ('whitest/brightest/most glittering of the gods') and said to have foresight like the Vanir:

Henry Adams Bellows: "Then Heimdall spake, whitest of the gods, / Like the Wanes he knew the future well: / 'Bind we on Thor the bridal veil, / Let him bear the mighty Brisings' necklace.'"

Jeramy Dodds: "The most glittering of gods, Heimdall, who, like the Vanir, is gifted with the gift of foresight, said: 'Let's strap a bridal veil over Thor's face and let him don the Brising necklace.'"

Scholar John Lindow notes there are no other indications of Heimdall being considered among the Vanir.

RIGSPULA — HEIMDALL AS FATHER OF HUMAN CLASSES

The introductory prose to Rigspula says that Heimdall, described as a god among the Aesir, once fared on a journey and referred to himself as Rigr. Walking along a seashore, he visited the homes of various couples, dined with them, gave advice, and spent three nights in each couple's bed. The wives became pregnant, and from them came the various classes of humanity:
- Thralls (enslaved class) from the lowest couple
- Farmers/freemen from the middle couple
- Warriors/nobles (jarls) from the highest couple

One warrior-class son becomes so skilled he earns the title of Rigr himself, sharing runic knowledge with Heimdall.

LOKASENNA

In Lokasenna (Loki's flyting with the gods), Loki taunts Heimdall by saying he was assigned the "wretched job" of standing as the gods' watchman. Loki also references a past battle with Heimdall in the form of seals at Singasteinn over the Brisingamen necklace.

PROSE EDDA ATTESTATIONS (GYLFAGINNING)

In Gylfaginning chapter 27, High describes Heimdall:
- He is the son of nine mothers (nine sisters, all daughters of Aegir)
- Needs less sleep than a bird
- Can hear grass growing on the ground and wool growing on sheep
- Can see equally well night and day for hundreds of miles
- Stands at the edge of heaven to guard Bifrost against mountain-giants
- Possesses Gjallarhorn — when blown, it can be heard in all the worlds

In chapter 49, Heimdall rides his horse Gulltoppr to attend Baldr's funeral.

In chapter 51 (Ragnarok), Heimdall stands and blows mightily into Gjallarhorn to alert all the gods. At the end of Ragnarok, Loki and Heimdall slay one another.

SKÁLDSKAPARMÁL

In Skáldskaparmál, Heimdall is referred to in kennings as: "son of nine mothers," "whitest of the Aesir," "possessor of Gjallarhorn," "Loki's enemy," "recoverer of Brisingamen," and "father of mankind."

Two surviving lines from the lost Heimdalargaldr (Heimdall's magic song): Snorri quotes Heimdall saying "I am the offspring of nine sisters."

SCHOLARLY THEORIES

Scholars have proposed connections between Heimdall and: sheep/wool (due to his keen hearing of wool growing), borders and boundary-marking (as guardian of Bifrost), waves (the nine mothers interpreted as waves of the sea), and the concept of whiteness/purity.

KEY ATTRIBUTES SUMMARY
- Dwelling: Himinbjorg (at the junction of Bifrost and the sky)
- Possessions: Gjallarhorn (warning horn), Gulltoppr (golden-maned horse), mead
- Senses: Can see 100+ miles by day and night; hears grass and wool growing; needs less sleep than a bird
- Father: Odin
- Mothers: Nine sisters (daughters of Aegir)
- Role: Guardian of Bifrost and Asgard against giants and enemies
- Fate at Ragnarok: Slays Loki and is slain by Loki
