MÓÐI AND MAGNI

In Norse mythology, Móði (Old Norse: IPA ˈmoːðe; anglicized Módi or Mothi) and Magni (IPA ˈmɑɣne) are the sons of Thor. Their names translate as "Wrath" (Móði) and "Strength" (Magni), respectively.

Scholar Rudolf Simek states that, along with Thor's daughter Þrúðr ("Power"), they embody their father's features.

PARENTAGE

Móði and Magni's descent from Thor is attested by the kennings:
- "Móði's father" (faðir Móða) — in Hymiskviða, stanza 34
- "Magni's father" (faðir Magna) — in Þórsdrápa and Hárbarðsljóð, stanza 53

Snorri Sturluson confirms this in Gylfaginning (stanza 53) and Skáldskaparmál (stanza 4).

According to Skáldskaparmál (stanza 17):
- Magni is the son of Thor and the jötunn Járnsaxa
- Móði is the son of Thor and an unnamed female jötunn (possibly also Járnsaxa)

POETIC EDDA — SURVIVORS OF RAGNARÖK

The two brothers are mentioned among the survivors of Ragnarök in the Poetic Edda poem Vafþrúðnismál (stanza 51):

Old Norse:
"Móði ok Magni / skulu Mjöllni hafa / Vingnis at vígþroti."

Bellows' translation:
"Modi and Magni / shall Mjollnir have / When Vingnir falls in fight."

This establishes that after Ragnarök — after Thor's death in battle with Jörmungandr — his sons Móði and Magni will inherit his hammer Mjölnir.

PROSE EDDA — MAGNI AND THE HRUNGNIR BATTLE

In the Prose Edda book Skáldskaparmál, Magni plays a role in the myth of Thor's battle with the giant Hrungnir:

From Skáldskaparmál (stanza 17, Brodeur's translation):
"But the hammer Mjöllnir struck Hrungnir in the middle of the head, and smashed his skull into small crumbs, and he fell forward upon Thor, so that his foot lay over Thor's neck. Thjálfi struck at Mökkurkálfi, and he fell with little glory. Thereupon Thjálfi went over to Thor and would have lifted Hrungnir's foot off him, but could not find sufficient strength. Straightway all the Æsir came up, when they learned that Thor was fallen, and would have lifted the foot from off him, and could do nothing.

Then Magni came up, son of Thor and Járnsaxa: he was then three nights old; he cast the foot of Hrungnir off Thor, and spake: 'See how ill it is, father, that I came so late: I had struck this giant dead with my fist, methinks, if I had met with him.' Thor arose and welcomed his son, saying that he should surely become great; 'And I will give thee,' he said, 'the horse Gold-Mane, which Hrungnir possessed.' Then Odin spake and said that Thor did wrong to give the good horse to the son of a giantess, and not to his father."

KEY FACTS
- Magni, at only three nights old, was the only being — god or otherwise — strong enough to lift Hrungnir's dead leg off Thor after the battle
- Thor rewarded Magni with the slain giant's horse Gullfaxi (Gold-Mane)
- Odin objected that the horse should have gone to him, not to Magni
- Magni's mother is the jötunn Járnsaxa (not Thor's wife Sif)

PARALLEL WITH VÁLI

Scholar John Lindow draws a parallel between Magni and Odin's son Váli:
- Both have a giantess mother (Járnsaxa for Magni; Rindr for Váli)
- Both achieve a feat at a very young age (Váli is only one day old when he kills Höðr, avenging Baldr's death; Magni is three nights old when he lifts Hrungnir's leg)
