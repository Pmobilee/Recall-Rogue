NIDHOGG (NIDHÖGGR / NIDHÖGGR)

Nidhogg (Old Norse: Nidhöggr, IPA niddhOGGzh; older Icelandic Nidhöggr; Modern Icelandic Nidhöggur) is a Germanic dragon in Norse mythology who gnaws at the roots of the world tree, Yggdrasil, and is likewise associated with the dead in Hel and Niflheim.

The English cognate of the name would be "Nithhewer."

ETYMOLOGY

The suffix -höggr clearly means "biter, striker, hewer." The prefix is disputed:
- nidr ("down, downwards"): thus "Biter Below (the roots)"
- nid (social stigma in Viking society implying loss of honor and villain status): thus "Malice Biter" — referring to its role gnawing corpses of those guilty of murder, adultery, and oath-breaking in Niflheim
- nida- (as in Nidafjoll, "nida-mountains", speculated to relate to Swedish nedan, the waning moon): thus "Striker in the Dark" or "mountains of waning"

In all interpretations, the name refers to gnawing on the roots of Yggdrasil.

ORTHOGRAPHY

The standardized Old Norse spelling is Nidhöggr. The letter ö is frequently used in place of the rarer character. English texts use various anglicizations: Nidhogg, Nidhoggur, Nidhög, Nidhug, Nidhögg, Nidhugg. The Danish forms Nidhug and Nidhög can also be encountered.

PROSE EDDA ATTESTATIONS

GYLFAGINNING

According to the Gylfaginning part of Snorri Sturluson's Prose Edda, Nidhogg is a being which gnaws one of the three roots of Yggdrasill. This root is placed over Niflheimr, and Nidhogg gnaws it from beneath.

Gylfaginning also states: "The squirrel called Ratatoskr runs up and down the length of the Ash, bearing envious words between the eagle [at the top] and Nidhöggr [the snake]." (Gylfaginning XVI, Brodeur's translation)

SKÁLDSKAPARMÁL

Snorri Sturluson specifies Nidhogg as a serpent in a list of names for such creatures:
"These are names for serpents: dragon, Fafnir, Jormungand, adder, Nidhogg, snake, viper, Goinn, Moinn, Grafvitnir, Grabak, Ofnir, Svafnir, masked one." (Faulkes translation, p. 137)

Later in Skáldskaparmál, Snorri includes Nidhogg in a list of terms and names for swords.

POETIC EDDA ATTESTATIONS

GRIMNISMAL

The poem Grimnismal identifies a number of beings living in Yggdrasill. The tree suffers great hardship from all the creatures on it. The poem identifies Nidhogg as tearing at the tree from beneath, and mentions Ratatoskr as carrying messages between Nidhogg and the eagle at the top of the tree. Snorri Sturluson often quotes Grimnismal and clearly used it as his source.

VOLUSPA — TWO APPEARANCES

First appearance — Naströnd description:

Nidhogg is described at the shore of the dead (Naströnd, "Dead Body Shore"), gnawing the corpses of the worst of the dead. The hall at Naströnd is woven of serpents' spines, its door facing north. Drops of venom fall through the roof.

Ursula Dronke's translation:
"A hall she saw standing / remote from the sun / on Dead Body Shore. / Its door looks north. / There fell drops of venom / in through the roof vent. / That hall is woven / of serpents' spines. / She saw there wading / onerous streams / men perjured / and wolfish murderers / and the one who seduces / another's close-trusted wife. / There Malice Striker sucked / corpses of the dead, / the wolf tore men. / Do you still seek to know? And what?"

The hall at Naströnd is where those guilty of the worst crimes go after death: perjurers, murderers, and adulterers. Nidhogg sucks their corpses.

Second appearance — after Ragnarok:

Nidhogg is mentioned at the very end of Voluspa, identified as a dragon and serpent flying from Nidafjoll ("Dark/Waning Mountains"):

Ursula Dronke's translation:
"There comes the shadowy / dragon flying, / glittering serpent, up / from Dark of the Moon Hills. / He carries in his pinions / —he flies over the field— / Malice Striker, corpses. / Now will she sink."

The meaning of this final appearance is disputed. The most prevalent interpretation is that Nidhogg's arrival heralds or accompanies Ragnarok as an omen of doom. An alternate interpretation holds that, given Norse mythology's themes of renewal, Nidhogg may be "shedding" the corpses and beginning life anew — or lifting the bodies of righteous rulers to dwell in Gimle. Depending on translation, either Nidhogg or the volva herself sinks.

UNIQUE FEATURES

Nidhogg is uniquely described as feathered and flying in Voluspa. Winged dragons are rare in Germanic art and myth prior to the 13th century. A runestone (Uppland Runic Inscription U 887, dated 1070-1100, from Skillsta, Sweden) shows a runic dragon and a bipedal winged dragon, considered related to the Voluspa tradition.

Nidhogg is not mentioned in any ancient source other than Grimnismal, Voluspa, and the Prose Edda.

KEY FACTS SUMMARY
- Location: Gnaws at the root of Yggdrasil over Niflheim
- Also associated with: Naströnd (shore of the dead in Hel)
- Nature: Serpent and dragon — described as both in sources
- Activity: Gnaws Yggdrasil's roots; sucks corpses of the evil dead at Naströnd
- Communication link: Ratatoskr the squirrel carries messages between Nidhogg (below) and the eagle (above) in Yggdrasil
- Post-Ragnarok: Described flying and carrying corpses after the world's end

COMPARISONS TO OTHER CULTURES

At least one Irish story (Conneda) tells of a man journeying into the otherworld past several giant snakes with names of different sins and transgressions, paralleling Nidhogg's role consuming sinners.

Nidhogg is seen as parent of all the snakes of the Norse underworld realm of Naströnd, separated from the rest of Hel by the river Gjallar (made of acidic poison). This is where souls denied crossing on the Gjallarbru wind up — also where Loki is eventually imprisoned.
