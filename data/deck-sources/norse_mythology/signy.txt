Signy or Signe (Old Norse: Signý, sometimes known as German: Sieglinde) is the name of two heroines in two connected legends from Norse mythology which were very popular in medieval Scandinavia. Both appear in the Völsunga saga, which was adapted into other works such as Wagner's Ring cycle, including the opera Die Walküre.

SIGNY IN VÖLSUNGA SAGA (PRIMARY)

Signy was the only daughter of King Völsung of Hunaland and Hljod the giantess. She and her twin brother Sigmund were the oldest of Völsung's eleven children. Signy reluctantly married King Siggeir of Gautland after he asked King Völsung for her hand. It was at their wedding feast that Sigmund drew the sword Gram from Barnstokkr.

The Betrayal

Three months after her wedding, Signy's father and brothers visited her at her new home in Gautland. Signy warned them of her husband's plan to betray them. Despite her warning, Völsung was killed and all of Signy's brothers were captured. At Signy's request, her brothers were put in the stocks rather than executed immediately, but each night one was killed by a she-wolf (Siggeir's shapeshifting mother). When only Sigmund was left alive, Signy sent a man to smear honey on his face and mouth, which the wolf licked instead of biting, allowing Sigmund to escape alive. Signy then helped her brother hide in the woods.

Testing Her Sons

Signy wanted nothing but to see her father's death avenged. She sent her elder son to Sigmund for testing — when Sigmund revealed to her that the son was unworthy, Signy told Sigmund to kill him, which he did. The same happened with her younger son.

The Incest

Signy met a sorceress with whom she exchanged shapes. Signy, looking like the sorceress, went to her brother Sigmund in the woods and slept with him for three nights. She then returned to the castle and regained her appearance. After a time she gave birth to a son Sinfjötli. He was also sent to Sigmund in the forest when he was nine years old. Sinfjötli, born of their incest, passed all tests and proved worthy.

Vengeance and Death

Sigmund and Sinfjötli killed Siggeir to avenge the death of their father/grandfather together. It was only after this vengeance was complete that Signy informed Sigmund of the incest that had led to Sinfjötli's birth. Signy then walked into the fire that was killing her husband, announcing: "In everything I have worked toward the killing of King Siggeir. I have worked so hard to bring about vengeance that I am by no means fit to live. Willingly I will now die with King Siggeir, although I married him reluctantly."

SIGNY AND HAGBARD (SECOND LEGEND)

The second Signy is the daughter of King Siggeir's nephew Sigar. She fell in love with the Sea-King Hagbard, and promised him that she would not live if he died. They were discovered and Hagbard was sentenced to be hanged. Hagbard managed to signal this to Signy who set her house on fire and died in the flames, whereupon Hagbard hanged himself in the gallows.

OTHER CHARACTERS NAMED SIGNY

- A third Signy is the daughter of a witch named Grid in Illuga saga Gríðarfóstra, delivered from a curse by a young man named Illugi.
- A fourth Signy was Hroðgar's sister in Skjöldunga saga and Hrólfs saga kraka (unnamed in Beowulf).

KEY FACTS

- Father: King Völsung of Hunaland
- Mother: Hljod the giantess
- Twin brother: Sigmund
- Eight other brothers (all killed by Siggeir's shapeshifting mother)
- Husband: King Siggeir of Gautland (despised, married reluctantly)
- Sons with Siggeir: two (both killed by Sigmund as unworthy)
- Son with Sigmund (incest): Sinfjötli
- Primary sources: Völsunga saga; also referenced in Beowulf (as Fitela/Sinfjötli's parentage)
- Wagner adaptation: Sieglinde in Die Walküre (Ring cycle)
- Signy's defining characteristic: absolute devotion to vengeance for her father's murder, willing to sacrifice her children, commit incest, and ultimately die to achieve it
