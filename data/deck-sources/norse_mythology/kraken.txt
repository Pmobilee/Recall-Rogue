KRAKEN — NORSE AND SCANDINAVIAN SEA MONSTER

Definition and Origin
The Kraken is a legendary sea monster from Scandinavian and Norwegian lore, described as a colossal creature of the deep capable of pulling ships under the sea. The name is related to the Norwegian and Swedish word krake, meaning an unhealthy animal or something twisted and crooked. The creature is thought to have originated in Scandinavian maritime tradition, with sailors in the waters between Norway and Iceland reporting sightings of "giant tentacles in the water that were powerful enough to crush or capsize their ships" from at least the 13th century onward.

The Konungs Skuggsjá (c. 1250 CE)
One of the earliest substantial written references to the Kraken appears in the Konungs Skuggsjá (The King's Mirror), a Norwegian didactic text written around 1250 CE. This work, structured as a dialogue between a father and son on matters of knowledge, describes two sea monsters found in the seas around Greenland:

The first is the Hafgufa, described as the largest of sea monsters, which could be as large as an island. The text describes fishermen rowing towards what appeared to be land or a large shoal of fish, only to find it was the back of the creature. When the Hafgufa dove, it created a massive whirlpool. Some modern scholars believe the Hafgufa descriptions may have been based on encounters with large whales, whose surfaces could be mistaken for islands or shoals.

The second creature mentioned is the Lyngbakr, a creature similar to a great island-sized beast.

The Konungs Skuggsjá establishes the core characteristics that would define Kraken legends: enormous size comparable to a small island, the capacity to create dangerous whirlpools when diving, and the confusion it caused for sailors who mistook its surface for land.

Francesco Negri's Account (c. 1700)
Italian priest and traveler Francesco Negri provided one of the earliest formal descriptions in his work Viaggio settentrionale, published around the turn of the 18th century. Negri described the creature as a multi-horned "fish" possessing many arms. Importantly, he distinguished the Kraken from the sea serpent, another legendary beast associated with northern waters — suggesting the two traditions were already understood as separate by this period.

Hans Egede's Account (1741)
Dano-Norwegian missionary Hans Egede, who worked in Greenland, described a sea monster that resembled the Hafgufa of medieval texts. Egede suggested the creature was possibly a large whale.

Erik Pontoppidan's Description (1752)
The most comprehensive and influential historical account of the Kraken was provided by Danish bishop and historian Erik Pontoppidan in his Norges Naturlige Historie (Natural History of Norway), first published in Danish in 1752-53. Pontoppidan explicitly stated his account was based on testimony from Norwegian fishermen who had witnessed the creature.

Pontoppidan's physical description: He characterized the Kraken as "round, flat, and full of arms" and declared it "the largest and most surprising of all the animal creation." He described it as resembling an enormous octopus (polypus).

Pontoppidan's size claim: He asserted the creature measured a mile and a half in circumference — effectively making it a small island. Early accounts consistently described the Kraken as "the size of a floating island."

Pontoppidan's behavioral account: According to his description, the Kraken would rise from the depths during summer months. The first visible sign was the appearance of shoals of fish gathering to feed on what the creature churned up. The creature itself would then begin to surface:

"Here and there a larger rising is observed like sand-banks, on which various kinds of small Fishes are seen continuously leaping about till they role off into the water from the sides of it; at last several bright points or horns appear, which grow thicker and thicker the higher they rise above the surface of the water."

These "horns" or arms could rise "as high and as large as the masts of middle-siz'd vessels." The creature could then seize and swallow fish and sailors who ventured too close. When it dove, it created "such a powerful suction vortex that any men unable to swim away from it would be sucked into the deep and drown." Pontoppidan noted the creature emitted a "strong and peculiar scent."

Pontoppidan characterized the Kraken as more dangerous through its diving behavior than through active attack — the whirlpool created by its rapid descent posed the primary threat to ships.

Physical Appearance in Various Accounts
Descriptions of the Kraken varied across different sources:
- Pontoppidan's account emphasized the flat, round body covered with arms, resembling a giant octopus or squid
- Some early accounts described "multiple heads instead of just one — and a number of claws capable of capturing any prey"
- The creature was consistently described as lying flat when surfaced, with its bulk barely visible
- The surface supposedly resembled a shallow bank or island, with small fish swimming over it

Curious Folk Beliefs
Curiously, some fishermen claimed that they could send the Kraken back into the depths simply by pronouncing its name — suggesting the creature had a ritual or magical dimension in popular belief, beyond being merely a physical danger.

The most common danger associated with the Kraken in folk tradition was not the creature seizing ships directly, but the whirlpool created when it submerged. Sailors were warned to flee immediately when they recognized the signs of a Kraken at the surface.

Connection to Real Sea Creatures
Modern scholars and marine biologists believe the Kraken legend was inspired primarily by encounters with giant squid (Architeuthis dux) and possibly large octopuses. Giant squids can reach staggering sizes — their mantle alone can exceed 2 meters, with tentacles extending the total length to over 13 meters. Decomposed or partially-digested squid remains washing ashore would have presented an extremely unusual and frightening appearance to coastal communities.

Jon Ablett, Senior Curator of Mollusca at London's Natural History Museum, proposed that the Kraken legend resulted largely from "partially digested or rotted remains of giant squid and large octopus" washing ashore. He described this as "a case of convergent evolution in myth form" — multiple northern maritime cultures independently developed similar legends from similar encounters with these unusual animals.

The Kraken in Literature
Alfred, Lord Tennyson wrote the poem "The Kraken" (1830), depicting the creature as an ancient, sleeping behemoth dwelling in abyssal depths, surrounded by enormous polypi, who would one day rise to the surface as the world ends:
- "Below the thunders of the upper deep; / Far, far beneath in the abysmal sea, / His ancient, dreamless, uninvaded sleep / The Kraken sleepeth."

Herman Melville's Moby-Dick (1851) references "the great Kraken of Bishop Pontoppidan," including it among legendary sea monsters.

Jules Verne featured a giant squid (related to the Kraken tradition) as an antagonist in Twenty Thousand Leagues Under the Sea (1870).

Victor Hugo's novel The Toilers of the Sea (1866) features a giant octopus episode drawing on Kraken tradition.

Scientific Classification Attempts
Pierre Denys de Montfort, a French malacologist, attempted in 1802 to classify the Kraken as a real species of giant octopus (Poulpe colossal), based on accounts from sailors and a ship's crew who claimed their vessel was attacked by a colossal octopus. His claims were largely ridiculed by the scientific community of the time, though the later discovery of giant squid species vindicated the general principle that enormous cephalopods existed.

The first scientifically documented giant squid specimens date to the late 19th century, confirming that animals of extraordinary size did inhabit deep waters — lending some biological credibility to the earliest Kraken reports, if not to the mile-and-a-half size claims.
