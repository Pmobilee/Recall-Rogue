NORSE COSMOLOGY AND CREATION MYTH

Norse cosmology is the account of the universe and its laws by the ancient North Germanic peoples. The topic encompasses concepts from Norse mythology and Old Norse religion such as notations of time and space, cosmogony, personifications, anthropogeny, and eschatology.

These concepts are primarily recorded from earlier oral sources in the Poetic Edda (a collection of poems compiled in the 13th century) and the Prose Edda (attributed to the Icelander Snorri Sturluson in the 13th century). Together these sources depict an image of Nine Worlds around a cosmic tree, Yggdrasil.

TIME AND CYCLIC NATURE

Scholar John Lindow notes that while events in Norse mythology describe a somewhat linear progression, various scholars note that Old Norse texts may imply or directly describe a fundamental belief in cyclic time. "The cosmos might be formed and reformed on multiple occasions by the rising sea."

CREATION OF THE COSMOS (GYLFAGINNING, PROSE EDDA)

PRE-CREATION STATE

Before the Earth came to be, there existed:
- Muspell: a bright and flaming place, so hot that foreigners may not enter it
- Niflheim: a foggy land, containing the spring Hvergelmir, from which flow numerous rivers

The rivers known as Elivagar flowed further and further from their source. Eventually the poisonous substance within the flow hardened and turned to ice. When the flow became entirely solid, a poisonous vapor rose from the ice and solidified into rime. These thick ice layers grew, spreading across the void of Ginnungagap.

FORMATION OF YMIR

The northern region of Ginnungagap continued to fill with ice and vapor. The southern portion remained clear due to proximity to Muspell's sparks and flames. Between Niflheim and Muspell — between ice and fire — was a placid location, "as mild as a windless sky."

When the rime and the blowing heat met, the liquid melted and dropped. This mixture formed the primordial being Ymir, the ancestor of all jotnar. Ymir sweated while sleeping:
- From his left arm grew a male and female jotunn
- "One of his legs begot a son with another"
- These limbs too produced children

AUDUMBLA AND BURI

Ymir fed from rivers of milk that flowed from the teats of the primordial cow, Audumbla. Audumbla fed from salt she licked from rime stones. Over the course of three days, she licked free a beautiful and strong man named Buri.

Buri's son Borr married a jotunn named Bestla. The two had three sons: the gods Odin, Vili, and Ve.

THE KILLING OF YMIR AND CREATION OF THE WORLD

The sons — Odin, Vili, and Ve — killed Ymir. Ymir's blood poured across the land, producing great floods that killed all of the jotnar but two: Bergelmir and his unnamed wife, who sailed across the flooded landscape.

Odin, Vili, and Ve took Ymir's corpse to the center of Ginnungagap and carved the world from it:
- Earth — made from Ymir's flesh
- Rocks — made from his bones
- Sea, lakes, and oceans — from his blood
- Scree and stone — from his molars, teeth, and remaining bone fragments
- The sky — made from Ymir's skull, placed above the earth in four points, each held by a dwarf (Nordri, Sudri, Austri, and Vestri — Old Norse for north, south, east, and west)
- Clouds — formed from Ymir's brains

After forming the dome of the Earth, the brothers took sparks of light from Muspell and placed them around the Earth, both above and below. Some remained fixed (stars); others moved through the sky in predetermined courses (planets and celestial bodies).

THE CREATION OF HUMANS

From Ymir's eyebrows they crafted a stronghold named Midgard to protect humanity from the jotnar. When walking along the seashore, Odin, Vili, and Ve found two trees and shaped humans from them:
- Odin gave them spirit and life
- Vili gave them wit and feeling
- Ve gave them form, speech, hearing, and sight
- They gave them clothing and names

The man was called Askr (ash tree), and the woman Embla. They were the ancestors of mankind who lived in Midgard.

Note: In the Poetic Edda poem Voluspa, the gods who create Ask and Embla are named differently as Hoenir, Lodurr, and Odin.

The brothers made for themselves in the middle of the world a city called Asgard, where the gods lived.

PERSONIFICATIONS

The Sun is personified as a goddess, Sol (Old Norse 'Sun'). The moon is personified as a male entity, Mani (Old Norse 'moon'). The Earth is also personified (Jord, Old Norse 'earth').

Night appears personified as the female jotunn Nott (Old Norse 'night'). Day is personified as Dagr (Old Norse 'day'). Dagr's father, the god Dellingr (Old Norse 'shining'), may personify the dawn.

Bodies of water are personified, such as the goddess Ran, her jotunn husband Aegir, and their wave-maiden children, the Nine Daughters of Aegir and Ran.

YGGDRASIL — THE WORLD TREE

Yggdrasil is a tree central to the Norse concept of the cosmos. The Aesir go to Yggdrasil daily to assemble at their things (traditional governing assemblies). The branches extend far into the heavens. The tree is supported by three roots:
1. One root to the well Urdarbrunnr in the heavens
2. One root to the spring Hvergelmir
3. One root to the well Mimisbrunnr

Creatures living within Yggdrasil include the dragon Nidhogg, an unnamed eagle, and the stags Dainn, Dvalinn, Duneyrr, and Durathor. The squirrel Ratatoskr runs up and down carrying messages between the eagle and Nidhogg.

THE NINE WORLDS

Old Norse texts mention the existence of Niu Heimar (Nine Worlds). According to Voluspa stanza 2, the Nine Worlds surround the tree Yggdrasil. Henry Adams Bellows (1923) proposes they consist of: Asgardr (gods), Vanaheimr (Vanir gods), Alfheimr (elves), Midgardr (humans), Jotunheimr (giants), Muspellsheimr (fire), Svartalfaheimr (dark elves), Niflheimr/Hel (dead), and possibly Nidavellir (dwarfs).

Odin threw Loki's daughter Hel into Niflheim and granted her authority over all Nine Worlds: "she has to administer board and lodging to those sent to her, and that is those who die of sickness or old age."

ASK AND EMBLA (ANTHROPOGENY)

Askr and Embla — male and female respectively — were the first two humans, created by the gods from driftwood (or trees) they encountered on a shore. The gods who form these first humans vary by source: in Voluspa, they are Hoenir, Lodurr, and Odin; in the Prose Edda, they are Odin, Vili, and Ve.

ESCHATOLOGY — RAGNAROK

Ragnarok is a series of future events including a great battle, foretold to result in the death of major deities, various natural disasters, and the submersion of the world in water. Afterward, the world will resurface anew and fertile, surviving gods will meet, and mankind will be repopulated by Lif and Lifthrasir, who will emerge from Yggdrasil.
