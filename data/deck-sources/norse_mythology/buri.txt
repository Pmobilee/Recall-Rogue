In Norse mythology, Búri (Old Norse: [ˈbuːre]) is a divinity god 'producer, father' of all other gods, and an early ancestor of the Æsir gods of the principal pantheon in Old Norse religion. Búri was licked free from salty rime stones by the primeval cow Auðumbla over the course of three days. Búri's background beyond this point is unattested, and he had a son, Borr, by way of an unknown process. Búri is attested in the Prose Edda, composed in the 13th century by Icelander Snorri Sturluson. The Prose Edda includes a quote from a 12th-century poem by skald Þórvaldr Blönduskáld that mentions the figure. Búri's mysterious origins are the subject of scholarly commentary and interpretation.

== Name ==

The name Búri, like the name of his son Burr (also spelled Borr), is derived from the Proto-Germanic *buriz "son, born". Thus, both names basically mean the same thing. In research, Buri's name is translated as "begotten, father" and Burr as "begotten, son" — probably because of the generational sequence. However, how he fathered his son is not explained; either by himself or through sexual reproduction.

== Attestations ==

Búri receives mention twice in the Prose Edda — once in Gylfaginning and again in a skaldic poem quoted in Skáldskaparmál.

The Gylfaginning section describes how the primeval cow Auðumbla licked salty rime stones over three days, progressively revealing Búri:
- First day: His hair was licked free
- Second day: His head was licked free
- Third day: His entire body was licked free — Búri emerged as a full being

Búri is mentioned nowhere in the Poetic Edda and only once in the skaldic corpus. In Skáldskaparmál, Snorri quotes the following verse by the 12th century skald Þórvaldr Blönduskáld, which references Búri.

== Position in the Norse Genealogy of the Gods ==

Búri is the first god to exist in Norse mythology — the ultimate ancestor of the Æsir. His family line:

- Búri (emerged from the primordial ice, licked free by Auðumbla)
  - Borr / Burr (son of Búri; mother unknown)
    - Odin (son of Borr and Bestla, daughter of the jötunn Bölþorn)
    - Vili (son of Borr and Bestla)
    - Vé (son of Borr and Bestla)

Odin, Vili, and Vé together slew the primordial frost giant Ymir and used his body to create the world. Odin went on to become the chief of the Æsir gods.

== Context: The Creation Myth ==

Búri's emergence is part of the Norse creation narrative. Before the worlds existed, there was Niflheim (realm of ice and mist) in the north and Muspelheim (realm of fire) in the south. Between them lay the void called Ginnungagap. When the heat of Muspelheim met the ice of Niflheim in the void, the primordial frost giant Ymir was formed from the melting ice. The primeval cow Auðumbla also emerged from the melting rime. She fed Ymir with her milk and herself fed by licking the salty rime stones, in the process revealing Búri.

Búri thus represents the first divine being, emerging through the natural process of a cow licking salt — a striking contrast to Ymir, who arose from the chaotic mixing of fire and ice. While Ymir would become the progenitor of the giants, Búri became the progenitor of the gods.

== Scholarly Notes ==

Búri's origins are considered mysterious — his emergence from the ice through Auðumbla's licking is attested only in the Prose Edda, making Snorri Sturluson the sole source for this specific creation detail. Whether this account preserves older oral tradition or reflects Snorri's own synthesis is a matter of scholarly debate. The Poetic Edda does not name Búri at all.
