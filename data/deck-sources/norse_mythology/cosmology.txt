NORSE COSMOLOGY — THE NINE REALMS AND THE WORLD TREE

Overview
Norse cosmology is the cosmological and mythological worldview of the ancient North Germanic peoples. It encompasses concepts from Norse mythology and Old Norse religion including notions of time and space, cosmogony (creation of the world), personifications of natural forces, anthropogeny (creation of humanity), and eschatology (the end of the world). These concepts are primarily preserved in the Poetic Edda (compiled in Iceland in the 13th century from older oral traditions) and the Prose Edda (written by Snorri Sturluson in Iceland around 1220 CE), as well as in skaldic poetry and sagas.

The central image of Norse cosmology is the world-tree Yggdrasil, a great ash tree around which nine realms are arranged. Norse religion lacked written scripture and was characterized by síður (custom and habitual practice), with worship occurring in homes, forest clearings, and sacred outdoor spaces rather than formal temples.

The Creation: Ginnungagap and the First Beings
Before creation, there existed only Ginnungagap — a vast, mist-filled void. Bordering this void on one side was Muspelheim (the realm of primordial fire) and on the other Niflheim (the realm of primordial ice and mist).

As the fires of Muspelheim heated the ice of Niflheim, the melting ice produced two primordial beings:

Ymir: The first frost giant (jötunn), who emerged from the thawing ice. Ymir was the ancestor of all giants. He fed from the milk of the cosmic cow Auðhumla. While Ymir slept, other beings grew from his body: a male and female emerged from his armpit, and a son grew from the coupling of his legs — these became the ancestors of all subsequent giants.

Auðhumla: A great cow who sustained Ymir with her milk. She herself fed by licking the salty ice of the primordial void. As she licked, she uncovered a being trapped in the ice: Búri, the first of the gods.

Búri had a son named Borr, who mated with the giantess Bestla (daughter of the giant Bölthorn). Borr and Bestla produced three sons: Odin, Vili, and Vé — the first of the Aesir gods.

The Creation of the World from Ymir's Body
Odin and his brothers killed Ymir. So much blood poured from the slain giant that it drowned all the other frost giants except Bergelmir and his wife, who escaped on a raft. All subsequent giants descended from Bergelmir.

The three brothers then took Ymir's body to the center of Ginnungagap and fashioned the world from his remains:
- His flesh became the earth (Midgard)
- His blood became the seas and lakes
- His bones became mountains and cliffs
- His teeth and broken bones became rocks and pebbles
- His skull became the dome of the sky, held up at four corners by four dwarves: Norðri, Suðri, Austri, and Vestri (North, South, East, and West)
- His brain was scattered to become the clouds
- His eyebrows were fashioned into a wall or fence called Midgard, creating a protected zone for humans
- Maggots formed in his flesh and were given human understanding by the gods, becoming the dwarves

Odin threw sparks from Muspelheim into the sky to become the stars and planets, establishing the patterns of day and night by setting the sun and moon on their courses.

Creation of Humanity: Ask and Embla
The first humans were Ask (an ash tree) and Embla (an elm tree, or possibly a vine). The three brothers found these trees on land and gave them:
- Odin gave them life (önd, breath/spirit)
- Vili (or Hœnir in some versions) gave them wits and the ability to move
- Vé (or Lóðurr in some versions) gave them blood, color, and the ability to speak

Ask and Embla were placed in Midgard, the realm of humans, which was protected from the hostile giants by the wall made from Ymir's eyelashes.

Yggdrasil — The World Tree
Yggdrasil (Old Norse: "Ygg's horse" — Ygg being one of Odin's names, and the name referring to the tree as the "gallows" on which Odin hanged himself) is the great ash tree at the center of Norse cosmology. All nine realms exist within and around Yggdrasil's roots, trunk, and branches.

The tree is supported by three great roots extending to three locations:
1. One root extends to Urðarbrunnr (the Well of Urðr / the Well of Fate), located in Asgard. The Norns live here.
2. One root extends to Mímisbrunnr (Mímir's Well / the Well of Wisdom), located in Jötunheim. Odin sacrificed his eye here in exchange for a drink from its waters.
3. One root extends to Hvergelmir (the Roaring Kettle), located in Niflheim. The dragon Níðhöggr gnaws at this root.

Yggdrasil is inhabited by numerous beings:
- Ratatoskr: A squirrel that runs up and down the trunk carrying messages (and insults) between the eagle at the top and Níðhöggr at the roots
- An unnamed eagle perches at the top of the tree
- The hawk Veðrfölnir sits between the eagle's eyes
- Níðhöggr: A great dragon/serpent who gnaws perpetually at the root in Niflheim
- Four stags (Dáinn, Dvalinn, Duneyrr, and Duraþrór) graze among the tree's branches, consuming its foliage
- Countless serpents gnaw at the roots alongside Níðhöggr

The Norns (Urðr, Verðandi, and Skuld) live at the base of the tree near Urðarbrunnr. Each day they draw water from the well and pour it mixed with white clay over the tree's branches to preserve it from rot. They also weave the fates of all beings — gods, humans, and giants alike.

The Nine Realms
Norse cosmological sources describe nine worlds (níu heimar) arranged around Yggdrasil. The exact list varies between sources, and Snorri Sturluson's 13th-century Prose Edda provides one systematic account that may not perfectly reflect earlier traditions.

1. Asgard (Ásgarðr) — "Enclosure of the Aesir"
The realm of the Aesir gods: Odin, Thor, Frigg, Tyr, Baldr, Heimdall, and others. Connected to Midgard by Bifröst, the rainbow bridge guarded by Heimdall. Contains multiple distinct halls: Odin's Valhalla, Thor's Bilskirnir (the largest hall in the cosmos), Frigg's Fensalir, and others. Odin's viewing throne Hliðskjálf is here, from which he can survey all nine realms.

2. Midgard (Miðgarðr) — "Middle Enclosure"
The realm of humans, created from Ymir's flesh and protected by a wall made from his eyelashes. The world of everyday human experience. Surrounded by the great sea, in which the Midgard Serpent (Jörmungandr) lies coiled.

3. Jötunheim (Jǫtunheimr) — "Home of the Giants"
Also called Útgarðr ("Outer Enclosure"). The realm of the frost giants and mountain giants, enemies of the gods. Separated from Asgard by the river Ífingr, which never freezes. Contains Utgarda-Loki's stronghold, where Thor was deceived by illusions. Also contains Þrymheim, Thiazi's mountain hall, later inherited by Skadi.

4. Niflheim (Niflheimr) — "Mist-Home"
The primordial realm of ice, mist, and cold, existing since before creation. The spring Hvergelmir in Niflheim feeds twelve rivers (the Élivágar) that flowed into Ginnungagap at the beginning. No beings inhabit Niflheim itself — it is a realm of formless cold.

5. Muspelheim (Múspellsheim) — "Home of Muspell"
The primordial realm of fire, existing since before creation. Ruled by the fire giant Surtr, who stands at its border with a flaming sword. At Ragnarök, Surtr will emerge and set fire to the world, burning everything including the gods' realm.

6. Vanaheim (Vanaheimr) — "Home of the Vanir"
The realm of the Vanir gods, a separate pantheon associated with fertility, prosperity, magic, and the sea. After the Aesir-Vanir War ended in a truce, hostages were exchanged: Njord, Freyr, and Freyja came to Asgard as Vanir hostages, while Hœnir and Mímir went to Vanaheim. Little is described about Vanaheim itself.

7. Alfheim (Álfheimr) — "Home of the Elves"
The realm of the light elves (ljósálfar), radiant beings more beautiful than the sun. In one account, Alfheim was given to the god Freyr as a tooth-gift when he was a child. The light elves were believed to inspire creativity and the arts in humans. Scholar John Lindow noted that Alfheim may have been inspired by a geographic region between the rivers Göta and Glomma at the Sweden-Norway border.

8. Svartalfheim / Nidavellir — "Home of the Dark Elves / Narrow Fields of the Dwarves"
These two names may refer to the same underground realm beneath Midgard, or to related realms. It is the home of the dwarves, who were formed from maggots in Ymir's flesh and given human understanding by Odin, Vili, and Vé. The dwarves are master craftsmen: they created Thor's hammer Mjolnir, Odin's spear Gungnir, Freyr's ship Skidbladnir, Sif's golden hair, Odin's ring Draupnir, and the chain Gleipnir that bound Fenrir. The realm is dark, underground, and filled with the smoke of dwarven forges.

9. Helheim / Hel (Helheimr)
The realm of the dead, ruled by Hel (Loki's daughter by the giantess Angrboða). Most of the dead end here — those who die of illness, old age, or any death that is not glorious battle. It lies far below the roots of Yggdrasil. The road to Hel is called Helvegr, and the traveler must go downward. A great gate guards the entrance. Hel herself is described as half-living colored and half corpse-colored — pale above the waist, dark and decaying below. Her realm is described as cold, gray, and joyless compared to Valhalla.

The Three Wells

Urðarbrunnr (The Well of Urðr / Well of Fate)
Located in Asgard beneath the root of Yggdrasil that extends toward the heavens. The Norns live here and draw water from it daily to pour over the tree. The gods themselves ride across Bifröst each day and hold their þing (council) at the well. The waters are said to be so sacred that anything dipped in them becomes white as the membrane inside an eggshell.

Mímisbrunnr (Mímir's Well / Well of Wisdom)
Located in Jötunheim beneath the second root. Its waters contain all wisdom and knowledge. The giant Mímir guards it and drinks from it daily with the horn Gjallarhorn, becoming the wisest being in existence. Odin desired to drink from it and offered one of his eyes as payment. Mímir accepted; Odin sacrificed his eye, which lies in the well. This is why Odin is one-eyed.

Hvergelmir (The Roaring Kettle)
Located in Niflheim beneath the third root. The most ancient of all springs — the source of the great rivers. The dragon Níðhöggr gnaws at the root near this well, accompanied by countless serpents. The waters of Hvergelmir feed all the rivers of the worlds.

Ragnarök — The Twilight of the Gods
Norse cosmology is eschatological — it includes a detailed account of the end of the current world order. Ragnarök (Old Norse: "Fate of the Gods" or "Twilight of the Gods") begins with Fimbulwinter: three consecutive winters with no summer between them, during which the world descends into chaos and warfare.

The events of Ragnarök:
- Fenrir breaks free from his chains
- Jörmungandr (the Midgard Serpent) rises from the ocean
- Loki breaks free from his imprisonment
- Surtr leads the sons of Muspelheim against the gods
- Heimdall blows Gjallarhorn to summon the gods
- The einherjar march out of Valhalla's 540 doors
- Odin fights Fenrir and is swallowed. Víðarr avenges him.
- Thor kills Jörmungandr but dies from its venom after nine steps
- Freyr fights Surtr but is killed for lack of his sword
- Tyr and Garm (Hel's hound) kill each other
- Heimdall and Loki slay each other
- Surtr flings fire across all the worlds, burning everything

After the destruction, a new world rises from the sea — green and fertile. Surviving gods (including Baldr, returned from Hel) repopulate the renewed cosmos. A new human pair, Líf and Lífþrasir, who survived by hiding in Hoddmímis holt (a cosmic forest), emerge to repopulate Midgard.

Primary Sources
- Völuspá (Poetic Edda) — the Seeress's account of creation, history, and Ragnarök
- Vafþrúðnismál (Poetic Edda) — cosmological wisdom contest
- Grímnismál (Poetic Edda) — detailed descriptions of divine halls and cosmic geography
- Gylfaginning (Prose Edda, Snorri Sturluson) — systematic account of Norse cosmology
- Ynglinga saga (Snorri Sturluson) — early mythological history
