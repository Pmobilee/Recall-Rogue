## Topic 1: Japan Invades Manchuria / Mukden Incident

**Wikipedia URL:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mukden_incident
**Secondary URL:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_invasion_of_Manchuria

### Exact Dates
- **September 18, 1931** — Mukden Incident (explosion at railway near Mukden/Shenyang)
- **September 19, 1931** — Japanese artillery attacks on Chinese garrison begin
- **September 21, 1931** — Reinforcements arrive from Korea
- **February 1932** — Key towns and provinces fully under Japanese control
- **March 1, 1932** — Manchukuo formally established (puppet state proclaimed)
- **March 1, 1934** — Manchukuo declared a constitutional monarchy with Puyi as emperor

### Key People
- **Colonel Seishiro Itagaki** (Kwantung Army) — masterminded and planned the false flag
- **Lieutenant Colonel Kanji Ishiwara** (Kwantung Army) — co-planner of the incident
- **Lieutenant Suemori Kawamoto** (29th Infantry Regiment, Independent Garrison Unit) — physically detonated the explosive
- **Puyi** — last Qing emperor, installed as ruler of Manchukuo (first as chief executive, then as emperor in 1934)
- **Earl of Lytton** (British politician) — led the Lytton Commission investigation
- **Yosuke Matsuoka** — Japanese ambassador, led delegation walkout at League of Nations

### Key Numbers
- Dynamite explosion was **so small the track was not destroyed** — a train passed over the damaged section minutes later
- Manchukuo population: approximately **30 million** (1931), rising to 43 million under Japanese administration
- Kwantung Army grew to **700,000 troops by 1941** (from a much smaller initial force in 1931)
- Lytton Report vote to accept: **42 votes to 1** (Japan the sole dissenter, Siam abstaining)

### Verified Quiz Facts
1. The Mukden Incident was a **false flag** operation — Japan staged the explosion and blamed Chinese dissidents to justify invasion.
2. Lieutenant **Suemori Kawamoto** detonated the explosive, but it was too weak to destroy the South Manchurian Railway track — a train passed over minutes later.
3. Manchukuo was officially proclaimed on **March 1, 1932**, with Puyi (the last Qing emperor) installed as its ruler.

### Commonly Confused / Surprising
- The civilian Japanese government did **not** authorize the invasion — rogue Kwantung Army officers acted unilaterally. The Japanese cabinet was presented with a fait accompli.
- The explosion was widely known at the time to be staged — it failed to even damage the track it was supposedly targeting.
- Manchukuo was initially proclaimed a "republic," only becoming a "constitutional monarchy" in 1934.

## Topic 2: Japan Leaves the League of Nations

**Wikipedia URL:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Y%C5%8Dsuke_Matsuoka
**Secondary URL:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_invasion_of_Manchuria

### Exact Dates
- **February 24, 1933** — Yosuke Matsuoka leads Japanese delegation walkout from League Assembly after vote on Lytton Report
- **March 27, 1933** — Japan formally notifies League of its intent to withdraw
- **March 1935** — Withdrawal legally takes effect (two-year notice period required by League rules)

### Key People
- **Yosuke Matsuoka** — Japanese ambassador/foreign minister, delivered walkout speech and led delegation out of the assembly chamber
- **Earl of Lytton** (Victor Bulwer-Lytton, 2nd Earl of Lytton) — chaired the five-member Lytton Commission

### Lytton Commission Key Findings (October 1932)
- Japan must **withdraw from Manchuria**
- Recognized **Chinese sovereignty** over Manchuria
- Refused to recognize the puppet state of **Manchukuo**
- Explicitly stated the Kwantung Army's actions were **not legitimate self-defense**

### Vote That Triggered Walkout
- **42 in favor** of Lytton Report, **1 against** (Japan), **1 abstention** (Siam)
- This was the overwhelming vote on February 24, 1933

### Verified Quiz Facts
1. Japan formally notified the League of Nations of its withdrawal on **March 27, 1933**.
2. The walkout on **February 24, 1933** was triggered by the League's 42-1 vote to adopt the Lytton Report recommending Japan withdraw from Manchuria.
3. Under League rules, withdrawal required two years' notice, so it was legally effective in **March 1935** — yet Japan had already stopped participating.

### Commonly Confused / Surprising
- Many sources cite the **walkout date (February 24)** and the **formal notice date (March 27)** as "Japan leaving the League" — they are two separate events.
- Japanese newspapers **celebrated** the withdrawal as national pride; it was not seen as a humiliation domestically.
- The Stimson Doctrine (US refusal to recognize territories gained by conquest) was announced as a response but had no enforcement mechanism.

## Topic 3: Second Sino-Japanese War Begins — Marco Polo Bridge Incident

**Wikipedia URL:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marco_Polo_Bridge_incident

### Exact Dates
- **July 7, 1937, approximately 23:00** — Japanese and Chinese forces exchange fire near the bridge
- **July 8, 1937, 04:50** — Full-scale fighting begins (Battle of Beiping-Tianjin commences)
- **July 29–30, 1937** — Japan captures Beiping (Beijing) and Taku Forts at Tianjin, concluding the battle
- This is generally considered the **start of the Second Sino-Japanese War**

### Key People / Organizations
- **Private Shimura Kikujiro** — the Japanese soldier alleged to be "missing" (he had already returned to his unit before fighting began)
- **China's 29th Army** (National Revolutionary Army) — Chinese force at the bridge
- **Imperial Japanese Army** garrison troops at Lugouqiao

### Location
- **Marco Polo Bridge (Lugou Bridge / Lugouqiao)** — 16.4 km (10.2 mi) southwest of Beijing, near the walled town of **Wanping**

### Key Numbers
- **24 days** of combat before Chinese 29th Army forced to withdraw
- Japanese demand: entry into **Wanping** to search for the missing soldier

### Verified Quiz Facts
1. The Marco Polo Bridge Incident began on **July 7, 1937**, when Japanese troops demanded entry into the walled city of Wanping to search for a "missing" soldier.
2. The supposed missing soldier — **Private Shimura Kikujiro** — had already returned to his unit before the fighting started; senior officers were never informed.
3. The incident triggered the **Second Sino-Japanese War**, with full-scale fighting beginning at 04:50 on July 8, 1937.

### Commonly Confused / Surprising
- The incident is also called the **July 7 Incident** (in Chinese historiography) and the **Lugou Bridge Incident**.
- It remains unclear who fired the first shot — both sides have blamed the other.
- The "missing soldier" pretense was already resolved before fighting broke out, making the escalation harder to explain as purely accidental.

## Topic 4: Nanjing Massacre / Rape of Nanking

**Wikipedia URL:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanjing_Massacre
**Secondary URL:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Death_toll_of_the_Nanjing_Massacre

### Exact Dates
- **December 13, 1937** — Japanese forces enter Nanjing (the capital of the Republic of China)
- **December 13, 1937 – early February 1938** — Massacre period (approximately 6 weeks)
- **November 22, 1937** — International Committee for the Nanking Safety Zone established
- **December 13, 1937** — Safety Zone sheltering approximately 250,000 people

### Key People
- **Prince Yasuhiko Asaka** — Imperial Japanese Army general; uncle by marriage of Emperor Hirohito; issued the order to "**kill all captives**" as temporary commander of the final assault (December 2–6, 1937)
- **General Iwane Matsui** — commanding general, Japanese Central China Front Army; tried and executed at Tokyo Trials
- **John Rabe** — German businessman (Nazi Party member); elected head of the International Committee for the Nanking Safety Zone; credited with protecting ~250,000 civilians; his Nazi Party membership gave him leverage with Japanese officers due to the Anti-Comintern Pact
- **Division commanders Kesago Nakajima and Heisuke Yanagawa** — informed Asaka that ~300,000 Chinese troops were surrounded

### Casualty Estimates — THE CORE DEBATE

| Source | Estimate | Notes |
|--------|----------|-------|
| Chinese government (official) | **~300,000** | Inscribed on the Memorial Hall in Nanjing |
| International Military Tribunal for the Far East (IMTFE) | **at least 200,000** | Also estimated at least 20,000 cases of rape |
| Japanese historian Hiroshi Yoshida | **more than 200,000** | |
| Japanese historian Tokushi Kasahara | **more than 100,000, close to 200,000** | |
| Canadian historian Bob Wakabayashi | **300,000 total in Yangtze delta** (not just Nanjing city) | Disputes 200,000+ figure for Nanjing city alone |
| Japanese nationalist / minimizer estimates | **40,000–50,000** | Disputed by mainstream historians |
| Academic range (city walls only) | **~50,000** (first 5 days) | Mostly massacred December 13–18 |
| Absolute minimum (nationalist Japanese) | **10,000** | Widely rejected by mainstream scholarship |
| Overall scholarly consensus range | **100,000–300,000+** | |

**Sexual violence:** Up to **80,000** women and children victimized according to recent research; the IMTFE documented at least **20,000 cases of rape**.

### Verified Quiz Facts
1. Japanese forces entered Nanjing on **December 13, 1937**, beginning a six-week period of mass killing and sexual violence.
2. **Prince Yasuhiko Asaka** — Emperor Hirohito's uncle by marriage — issued the "kill all captives" order and was **never prosecuted** because General Douglas MacArthur granted immunity to the Imperial Family.
3. **John Rabe**, a German Nazi Party member, led the International Committee for the Nanking Safety Zone and is credited with protecting approximately **250,000 Chinese civilians**.

### Commonly Confused / Surprising
- The IMTFE figure of **200,000** is often cited as the "official" number, but the Chinese government officially maintains **300,000**.
- The debate is not just about numbers but about **who counts** — whether POWs, soldiers killed in surrounding counties, etc. are included.
- The man most responsible for the massacre, **Prince Asaka**, lived freely until **1981** — he was never tried because of imperial family immunity granted by MacArthur.
- John Rabe's Nazi Party membership was actually what made him effective — Japan respected German allies.

## Topic 5: Anti-Comintern Pact

**Wikipedia URL:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anti-Comintern_Pact
**Primary source:** https://avalon.law.yale.edu/wwii/tri1.asp (Avalon Project — full text)

### Exact Date
- **November 25, 1936** — Signed in Berlin

### Signatories
- **Joachim von Ribbentrop** — German Ambassador-at-Large (later Foreign Minister)
- **Kintomo Mushanokoji** — Japanese Ambassador to Germany

### What "Comintern" Means
- **Communist International** (Comintern) — Soviet-controlled organization founded in 1919 to promote world communist revolution

### Public Treaty Content
- Germany and Japan agree to inform each other of Comintern activities
- Agree to cooperate to counter Comintern influence
- Invite other nations to join

### Secret Protocol Content
- Specified a **joint German-Japanese policy specifically against the Soviet Union**
- If either party was attacked by the USSR without provocation, the other party was **not** to do anything to assist the Soviet Union
- Void any existing bilateral treaties with the Soviets
- The secret part: neither country would help the Soviets if Stalin attacked the other

### Countries That Joined
- **Italy** — November 6, 1937 (designated as an "original signatory" retroactively)
- **Spain** — March 27, 1939
- **Hungary** — February 24, 1939
- Additional countries joined during WWII

### Verified Quiz Facts
1. The Anti-Comintern Pact was signed on **November 25, 1936**, between Nazi Germany and Japan, targeting the Soviet-led **Communist International (Comintern)**.
2. The pact had a **secret protocol** explicitly targeting the Soviet Union — neither signatory would assist the USSR if it attacked the other.
3. **Italy** joined the Anti-Comintern Pact on **November 6, 1937**, completing the original Axis triangle ahead of the Tripartite Pact.

### Commonly Confused / Surprising
- The pact was specifically **not** a military alliance — it was about intelligence sharing and diplomatic coordination against communism; the Tripartite Pact (1940) was the actual military alliance.
- The Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact (1939) effectively **voided** the Anti-Comintern Pact's secret protocol — Germany allied with the very power the pact was directed against, shocking Japan.
- Italy had previously signed the Italo-Soviet Pact partly directed against Hitler's Germany — its switch to the Anti-Comintern Pact was a major realignment.

## Topic 6: Rise of Japanese Militarism

**Wikipedia URL:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_militarism
**Secondary URL:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/February_26_incident
**Tertiary URL:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hideki_Tojo

### Key Timeline
- **Late 1920s onward** — Kodoha (Imperial Way Faction) becomes active within the Army, seeking violent overthrow of civilian government
- **May 15, 1932** — "May 15 Incident": Young naval officers assassinate Prime Minister Inukai Tsuyoshi; military officers given light sentences
- **February 26, 1936** — "2.26 Incident" (February 26 Incident): coup attempt by ~1,400 Army troops
- **February 26–29, 1936** — Rebels occupy Tokyo government buildings; rebels surrender February 29
- **1940** — All political parties dissolved into the **Imperial Rule Assistance Association** (one-party state)
- **October 18, 1941** — General Hideki Tojo becomes Prime Minister

### The Two Army Factions
| Faction | Name | Goal | Key Figures |
|---------|------|------|-------------|
| **Kodoha** | Imperial Way Faction | Direct imperial rule, purge "corrupt" politicians, strike north vs USSR | Generals Araki Sadao, Mazaki Jinzaburo |
| **Toseiha** | Control Faction | Modernize military, planned state-led expansion, strike south/Pacific | General Tojo Hideki |

### February 26 Incident (1936) — Key Facts
- **Date:** February 26, 1936
- **Forces:** ~1,400 troops from the First and Guards Divisions
- **Assassinated:** Home Minister Saito Makoto, Finance Minister Takahashi Korekiyo, Army Inspector General Watanabe Jotaro
- **Failed to kill:** Prime Minister Keisuke Okada (who hid successfully)
- **Outcome:** Rebels surrendered by February 29; **19 leaders executed**, ~40 imprisoned; Kodoha faction destroyed; Toseiha took control
- **Lasting consequence:** Civilian government lost power to intimidate the military; military gained effective veto over cabinet formation

### Hideki Tojo — Key Facts
- Born: **December 30, 1884**, Tokyo
- Military family of samurai descent
- Rose through Kwantung Army; **Chief of Staff, Kwantung Army 1937**
- **Army Minister** from July 30, 1940 (in Konoe's second cabinet)
- **Prime Minister: October 18, 1941** (presided over Pearl Harbor and Pacific War)
- Resigned: **July 18, 1944** (after fall of Saipan)
- Executed: **December 23, 1948** (Tokyo Trials)

### Verified Quiz Facts
1. The **February 26 Incident (1936)** was a failed coup by ~1,400 Kodoha (Imperial Way Faction) troops who assassinated three senior government officials but failed to kill the Prime Minister.
2. After the failed coup, the **Toseiha (Control Faction)** — Tojo's faction — took over the military; 19 coup leaders were executed and the Kodoha was destroyed.
3. General Hideki Tojo became Prime Minister on **October 18, 1941**, less than 2 months before the attack on Pearl Harbor.

### Commonly Confused / Surprising
- Tojo was actually the **moderate** option compared to the Kodoha — he was chosen because he could control the Army while pursuing expansion through planning rather than chaos.
- All political parties in Japan were **voluntarily dissolved** into the Imperial Rule Assistance Association in 1940 — there was no single dramatic "seizure of power."
- The **May 15 Incident (1932)** is less famous but equally important — when the military assassins received light sentences, it signaled the civilian government could not control the military.

## Topic 7: Soviet-Japanese Border Conflicts — Battles of Khalkhin Gol

**Wikipedia URL:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battles_of_Khalkhin_Gol
**Secondary URL:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet%E2%80%93Japanese_border_conflicts

### Exact Dates
- **May 11, 1939** — Battles begin (Mongolian cavalry enter disputed area; Manchukuo cavalry drive them back)
- **June 2, 1939** — Georgy Zhukov given command of Soviet/Mongolian forces
- **July 23, 1939** — Japanese launch large-scale assault (fails)
- **August 20, 1939, 05:45** — Soviet major offensive begins (artillery + 557 aircraft — first fighter-bomber offensive in Soviet Air Force history)
- **September 15, 1939** — Ceasefire signed in Moscow
- Also known in Japan as: **Nomonhan Incident**

### Location
- Border between **Manchukuo** (Japanese puppet state) and the **Mongolian People's Republic** (Soviet ally)
- Dispute: Japan claimed border was the **Khalkhin Gol river**; Mongolia/USSR claimed border was **16 km east** of the river

### Key People
- **Georgy Zhukov** — Soviet general; commanded the decisive offensive; this battle launched his career (he later commanded at Stalingrad, Kursk, and Berlin)
- **Kliment Voroshilov** — Soviet Commissar of Defence who appointed Zhukov
- Japanese **23rd Division** — primary Japanese unit; suffered catastrophic losses

### Casualty Figures (contested)

| Side | Official Claim | Historians' Estimate |
|------|---------------|----------------------|
| Japan | 8,440 killed, 8,766 wounded | ~19,714 total (7,696 killed, 8,647 wounded, 1,021 missing, 2,350 sick) per Kwantung Army medical records |
| Soviet Union | 9,284 casualties (for final offensive only) | Likely 21,000–24,000 total campaign |

### Strategic Impact — Hokushin-ron vs. Nanshin-ron
- **Hokushin-ron** ("Strike North") — Army doctrine: expand into Siberia, fight USSR
- **Nanshin-ron** ("Strike South") — Navy doctrine: seize Southeast Asia/Pacific
- The **decisive Soviet victory** at Khalkhin Gol destroyed the Hokushin-ron doctrine
- Japan **shifted permanently to Nanshin-ron** after this defeat — directly leading to Pearl Harbor and conquest of Southeast Asia

### Verified Quiz Facts
1. The Battles of Khalkhin Gol ended with a ceasefire on **September 15, 1939** — just **two weeks after** Germany invaded Poland, meaning the Soviet Union was fighting Japan in Asia while also partitioning Poland in the west.
2. The Soviet offensive on **August 20, 1939** was led by **Georgy Zhukov** and featured the first coordinated fighter-bomber offensive in Soviet Air Force history.
3. Japan's defeat at Khalkhin Gol killed the "**Strike North**" (Hokushin-ron) doctrine against the USSR and pivoted Japan permanently toward the "**Strike South**" (Nanshin-ron) strategy — directly leading to Pearl Harbor.

### Commonly Confused / Surprising
- The ceasefire was signed on **September 15, 1939** — the same week as the German-Soviet invasion of Poland. The USSR was at war on two fronts (de facto) simultaneously.
- Zhukov was essentially **unknown** before this battle; it was his career-making command.
- Japan's official casualty figures were **dramatically understated** — Kwantung Army medical records show nearly **20,000 casualties**.
- The battle occurred over **4 months** but is relatively obscure in Western WWII history.

## Topic 8: Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact

**Wikipedia URL:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molotov%E2%80%93Ribbentrop_Pact
**Primary source:** https://avalon.law.yale.edu/20th_century/addsepro.asp (Avalon Project — full text of secret protocol)
**Secondary:** https://encyclopedia.ushmm.org/content/en/article/german-soviet-pact

### Exact Date
- **August 23, 1939** — Signed (technically signed in the early hours of August 24, backdated to August 23)
- **August 19, 1939** — German-Soviet Credit Agreement signed (economic precursor)
- **September 1, 1939** — Germany invades Poland (one week after pact)
- **September 17, 1939** — Soviet Union invades Poland from the east

### Signatories
- **Vyacheslav Molotov** — Soviet Foreign Minister (signed for USSR)
- **Joachim von Ribbentrop** — German Foreign Minister (signed for Germany)
- **Joseph Stalin** — present at signing, watching

### Public Treaty
- **Non-aggression pact**: Germany and USSR pledged not to attack each other
- Duration: 10 years
- Neither would assist a third party attacking the other

### Secret Additional Protocol — Territorial Divisions

| Region | Assigned To |
|--------|------------|
| Eastern Poland (east of Narew-Vistula-San rivers) | Soviet sphere |
| Western Poland | German sphere |
| Finland | Soviet sphere |
| Estonia | Soviet sphere |
| Latvia | Soviet sphere |
| Lithuania | German sphere (with "interest" in Vilnius region going to USSR) |
| Bessarabia (Romania) | Soviet sphere |

### Acknowledgment of Secret Protocol
- **1939–1989**: Soviet Union **denied** the secret protocol existed
- **November 1989**: Revealed during Nuremberg trials (Western copies existed since 1945)
- **December 24, 1989**: Congress of People's Deputies of the Soviet Union officially declared the secret protocols **null and void** from the moment of signing
- **September 1, 1989**: Federal Republic of Germany had already declared the protocols invalid

### Verified Quiz Facts
1. The Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact was signed on **August 23, 1939**, by Soviet Foreign Minister **Vyacheslav Molotov** and German Foreign Minister **Joachim von Ribbentrop**.
2. The secret protocol assigned **Finland, Estonia, Latvia, and Bessarabia** to the Soviet sphere and **Lithuania** (except Vilnius) to the German sphere — Stalin was buying territory, not just peace.
3. The Soviet Union **denied the secret protocol's existence until December 24, 1989**, when the Congress of People's Deputies finally declared it null and void.

### Commonly Confused / Surprising
- The pact **shocked the Communist world** — the USSR and Nazi Germany were supposed to be ideological enemies; foreign Communists had been told fascism was the ultimate evil.
- Stalin reportedly saw the **economic agreement (August 19)** as more immediately important than the political pact.
- Lithuania was initially in the **German** sphere — this was later revised in September 1939 when Germany traded Lithuania to the USSR in exchange for a larger share of Poland.
- The pact did **not** just cover Poland — it partitioned the entire eastern European buffer zone.

## Topic 9: Tripartite Pact

**Wikipedia URL:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tripartite_Pact

### Exact Date
- **September 27, 1940** — Signed in Berlin

### Signatories
- **Joachim von Ribbentrop** — German Foreign Minister (for Germany)
- **Galeazzo Ciano** — Italian Foreign Minister (for Italy)
- **Saburo Kurusu** — Japanese Ambassador to Germany (for Japan)
- **Adolf Hitler** — present at the signing ceremony

### What the Pact Committed Each Nation To
- Signatories would "assist one another with all **political, economic and military means**" if attacked by a power "**not currently involved**" in the European War or the Sino-Japanese Conflict
- This was explicitly designed to **warn the United States** against entering the war
- Explicitly **excluded the Soviet Union** from the mutual defense clause

### Countries That Joined Later
| Country | Date Joined |
|---------|-------------|
| Hungary | November 20, 1940 |
| Romania | November 23, 1940 |
| Slovakia | November 24, 1940 |
| Bulgaria | March 1, 1941 |
| Yugoslavia | March 25, 1941 (reversed by coup two days later) |

### Why It Failed
- The three main signatories were **geographically separated** — Japan in Asia, Germany and Italy in Europe
- The defensive alliance was **never invoked** despite all three being at war
- Historian Paul W. Schroeder: the pact went from "importance in late 1940 to merely nominal existence in late 1941" and was "**virtually inoperative**" by December 1941

### Verified Quiz Facts
1. The Tripartite Pact was signed in Berlin on **September 27, 1940**, by Germany's Ribbentrop, Italy's Ciano, and Japan's Ambassador **Saburo Kurusu**.
2. The pact explicitly **excluded the Soviet Union** from its mutual defense clause — it was primarily aimed at deterring the **United States** from entering the war.
3. The Tripartite Pact's defensive guarantee was **never actually invoked** — despite all three signatories fighting major wars, they provided virtually no military coordination with each other.

### Commonly Confused / Surprising
- Saburo Kurusu signed the Tripartite Pact for Japan, then was sent to Washington in **November 1941** as the diplomat conducting final negotiations when Pearl Harbor was attacked — a remarkable historical irony.
- The pact was supposed to **deter the US** but arguably had the opposite effect — it made the US more determined to resist Axis expansion.
- Yugoslavia **joined on March 25, 1941** then was overthrown by a pro-Allied coup just two days later on March 27 — Germany invaded in response.

## Topic 10: Nazi-Soviet Economic Agreements

**Wikipedia URL:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German%E2%80%93Soviet_economic_relations_(1934%E2%80%931941)
**Secondary URL:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German%E2%80%93Soviet_Credit_Agreement_(1939)
**Primary source text:** https://avalon.law.yale.edu/20th_century/ns059.asp

### Key Agreements

#### German-Soviet Credit Agreement (1939)
- **Signed: August 19, 1939** (in Berlin) — four days before the Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact
- Also called the "German-Soviet Commercial Agreement" of August 1939
- Germany provided USSR: **200 million Reichsmarks credit** over 7 years at **4.5% interest** (extremely favorable terms)
- USSR to use credit to buy: factory equipment, machinery, machine tools, ships, vehicles, military technology
- USSR agreed to deliver **180 million Reichsmarks in raw materials** over two years

#### Soviet Raw Materials Committed Under the Agreement
| Commodity | Quantity |
|-----------|----------|
| Feed grains and legumes | 1,000,000 tons (valued at 120M RM) |
| Mineral oil (petroleum) | 900,000 tons (115M RM) |
| Phosphates | 500,000 tons |
| Cotton | 100,000 tons |
| Manganese, chromium | Additional quantities |

#### German-Soviet Commercial Agreement (1940)
- A follow-on expanded agreement in early 1940
- By **June 1940**: Soviet imports comprised **over 50%** of Germany's total overseas imports
- Often exceeded **70%** of total German overseas imports

### Strategic Significance
- Stalin reportedly considered the **economic agreement more important** than the political non-aggression pact itself
- The Soviets fulfilled deliveries **right up until June 22, 1941** (German invasion of the USSR) — not wanting to give Germany a pretext for war
- Soviet-provided oil, grain, and metals helped fuel the **Fall of France (May-June 1940)** and the Blitz
- Deeply ironic: Soviet raw materials helped enable Germany's early conquests

### Verified Quiz Facts
1. The German-Soviet Credit Agreement was signed on **August 19, 1939** — four days before the Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact — demonstrating that the economic deal preceded and enabled the political one.
2. Under the agreement, the USSR committed to delivering **1 million tons of grain** and **900,000 tons of oil** to Nazi Germany in exchange for industrial machinery and military technology.
3. By mid-1940, Soviet imports accounted for **over 50% of Germany's total overseas imports** — the USSR was materially fueling the Nazi war machine that would eventually invade it.

### Commonly Confused / Surprising
- The economic agreement is often forgotten in the shadow of the Molotov-Ribbentrop political pact, but Stalin himself may have valued it **more highly**.
- The USSR kept delivering supplies to Germany **until the day of the invasion** — Stalin refused to believe war was imminent even as intelligence warned him.
- Germany's exchange — advanced industrial machinery and military technology (including warship blueprints) — gave the Soviet military-industrial base significant upgrades it later used against Germany.

## Topic 11: Hossbach Memorandum

**Wikipedia URL:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hossbach_Memorandum
**Primary source:** https://avalon.law.yale.edu/imt/hossbach.asp (Avalon Project — full text)
**Secondary:** https://nuremberg.law.harvard.edu/documents/450604 (Harvard Nuremberg Archive)

### Exact Date
- **November 5, 1937** — The meeting in Berlin (Reich Chancellery)
- **November 10, 1937** — Hossbach wrote up the memorandum (from memory, 5 days after the meeting)

### Attendees

| Person | Role |
|--------|------|
| **Adolf Hitler** | Chancellor/Führer |
| **Friedrich Hossbach** | Hitler's Wehrmacht adjutant; took notes; document named after him |
| **Werner von Blomberg** | Reich War Minister (Feldmarschall) |
| **Werner von Fritsch** | Army Commander-in-Chief (General) |
| **Erich Raeder** | Navy Commander-in-Chief (Admiral) |
| **Hermann Göring** | Luftwaffe Commander (Generaloberst) |
| **Konstantin von Neurath** | Reich Foreign Minister (Baron) |

### Hitler's Key Statements
- Germany's future was entirely dependent on **acquiring "Lebensraum"** (living space) in Eastern Europe
- Economic problems required action **"sooner rather than later"** — falling behind in the arms race
- Primary targets: **Austria** and **Czechoslovakia** (to be absorbed)
- Timeline: **as early as 1938, no later than 1943**
- Hitler did not want war with Britain and France in 1937 — he favored **"small wars of plunder"**
- The seizure of Austria and Czechoslovakia was needed to **plunder their economies** and give Germany the lead in the arms race

### Historical Significance
- Used by US prosecution at Nuremberg as key evidence of **premeditated Nazi conspiracy** to launch WWII
- Both conquests (Austria March 1938, Czechoslovakia March 1939) occurred roughly as described — suggesting it was a genuine plan

### The Intentionalist vs. Functionalist Debate
| School | Historians | Argument |
|--------|-----------|----------|
| **Intentionalist** | Gerhard Weinberg, Andreas Hillgruber, Richard Overy | Memorandum proves Hitler had a **long-term master plan** for world war |
| **Functionalist** | Timothy Mason, Hans Mommsen, Ian Kershaw | Memorandum shows **improvised response** to economic crisis, not a grand plan |

### Reliability Issues
- Written **5 days after the meeting, from memory** — exact wording cannot be verified
- Original document was captured by the Allies after WWII

### Verified Quiz Facts
1. The Hossbach Memorandum records a meeting on **November 5, 1937**, at which Hitler told his military chiefs that Germany must acquire **Lebensraum** by seizing Austria and Czechoslovakia **as early as 1938**.
2. The document was written by **Colonel Friedrich Hossbach** from memory **five days after** the meeting, raising questions about its exact accuracy.
3. American prosecutors at the **Nuremberg Trials** used the Hossbach Memorandum as their key evidence that the Nazi leadership had conspired to launch a war of aggression from at least November 1937.

### Commonly Confused / Surprising
- Hossbach was **not** a senior decision-maker — he was Hitler's adjutant (assistant). The document is named after a note-taker, not a participant.
- The document was **not secret** in the way the Molotov-Ribbentrop protocol was — it was captured after the war, not leaked.
- The debate between intentionalists and functionalists over this document remains **one of the most contested issues** in WWII historiography.

## Topic 12: Final Diplomatic Failures — August 1939

**Wikipedia URL:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/August_1939
**Secondary URL:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Birger_Dahlerus
**Tertiary URL:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1939_German_ultimatum_to_Poland

### Key Timeline — August 1939

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| August 19, 1939 | German-Soviet Credit Agreement signed |
| August 23, 1939 | Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact signed; world stunned |
| August 25, 1939 | Britain formally signs mutual assistance treaty with Poland (had been pledged since March 1939) |
| August 29, 1939 | Hitler demands Poland send plenipotentiary to Berlin within **24 hours** to negotiate; UK considers this an ultimatum with unrealistic deadline |
| August 31, 1939 | Hitler issues **Directive No. 1** — attack on Poland to begin September 1 at 04:45; German radio announces Poland "rejected" German proposals (proposals were never officially presented to Poland) |
| September 1, 1939 | Germany invades Poland; **World War II begins** |
| September 3, 1939 | Britain and France declare war on Germany |

### The Dahlerus Mediation
- **Birger Dahlerus** — Swedish businessman; personal friend of Hermann Göring
- August 1939: Arranged meeting between Göring and 7 British businessmen at his house in Schleswig-Holstein
- Shuttled between London and Berlin carrying unofficial messages; convinced Lord Halifax to write a letter expressing UK's desire for peace
- Brought letter to Göring, who declared "Hitler must be informed immediately"
- Assessment by Nuremberg judges: negotiations were conducted "**not in good faith or with any desire to maintain peace, but solely in the attempt to prevent Britain and France from honouring their obligations to Poland**"

### Hitler's 16-Point Proposal — Bad Faith Diplomacy
- Hitler presented Germany's "16 Points" to Poland as reasonable demands
- Germany did **not** give Poland or the UK a copy of the 16 Points until **after** the deadline to respond had expired
- German radio announced Poland had "rejected" proposals Poland had never seen
- This was legal cover for aggression, not genuine diplomacy

### Pope and Roosevelt Appeals
- **Pope Pius XII** made a radio address to the world pleading for peace: "The danger is imminent, but there is yet time"
- **President Roosevelt** appealed to King Victor Emmanuel III of Italy to broker a peace conference

### Verified Quiz Facts
1. On **August 31, 1939**, Hitler issued Directive No. 1 ordering the invasion of Poland to begin September 1 at 04:45 — even as diplomatic channels were still nominally open.
2. Germany never officially presented its "16-point proposal" to Poland — the Poles and British only received the text **after** Germany announced Poland had "rejected" it, a deliberate deception.
3. **Birger Dahlerus**, a Swedish businessman with no official role, conducted the last-ditch back-channel diplomacy between Göring and London in August 1939 — a sign of how thoroughly the official diplomatic system had broken down.

### Commonly Confused / Surprising
- Britain's formal treaty with Poland was signed on **August 25, 1939** — the same day Hitler initially planned to invade (he postponed for 6 days after learning of the treaty).
- The Nuremberg tribunal explicitly found that Germany's late-August negotiations were conducted in **bad faith** — designed to give a pretext while keeping Britain and France out.
- **Pope Pius XII's** radio plea was one of the last public calls for peace — often overlooked in standard WWII history.
- The Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact signing on **August 23** was the true death knell of peace — it eliminated Germany's fear of a two-front war.

### Causation Chain
1. Mukden Incident (Sept 18, 1931) → Japan invades Manchuria
2. Manchukuo established (March 1, 1932) → League of Nations investigates
3. Lytton Report adopted (Feb 24, 1933) → Japan leaves League (March 27, 1933)
4. Anti-Comintern Pact (Nov 25, 1936) → Japan/Germany align against USSR
5. Hossbach Memorandum (Nov 5, 1937) → Hitler plans Austria + Czechoslovakia seizure
6. Marco Polo Bridge (July 7, 1937) → Full Sino-Japanese War begins
7. Nanjing Massacre (Dec 13, 1937) → Escalation of atrocities in China
8. Khalkhin Gol (May-Sept 1939) → Japan abandons "Strike North," pivots to Pacific
9. German-Soviet Credit Agreement (Aug 19, 1939) → Economic foundation for Pact
10. Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact (Aug 23, 1939) → Germany's eastern flank secured
11. Diplomatic failures (Aug 25-31, 1939) → War declared inevitable
12. Tripartite Pact (Sept 27, 1940) → Axis formally consolidated post-France fall

### Dates Easily Confused
- **February 24, 1933** (Japan's League walkout) vs. **March 27, 1933** (formal withdrawal notice)
- **July 7, 1937** (Marco Polo Bridge shots fired) vs. **July 8, 04:50** (full war begins)
- **August 19, 1939** (economic agreement) vs. **August 23, 1939** (political pact)
- **September 15, 1939** (Khalkhin Gol ceasefire) vs. **September 17, 1939** (Soviet invasion of Poland)

### Numbers That Are Contested
- Nanjing Massacre dead: 40,000 (nationalist Japanese minimum) to 430,000 (high estimates); mainstream consensus 100,000–300,000
- Khalkhin Gol Japanese casualties: 8,440 (official) vs ~20,000 (historians' estimate)
- Khalkhin Gol Soviet casualties: 9,284 (official) vs 21,000–24,000 (historians' estimate)
