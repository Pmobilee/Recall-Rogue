# Overarching Framework

## 4 Big Ideas
1. **EVO — Evolution**: The process of evolution drives the diversity and unity of life.
2. **ENE — Energetics**: Biological systems use energy and molecular building blocks to grow, reproduce, and maintain dynamic homeostasis.
3. **IST — Information Storage and Transmission**: Living systems store, retrieve, transmit, and respond to information essential to life processes.
4. **SYI — Systems Interactions**: Biological systems interact, and these systems and their interactions exhibit complex properties.

## 6 Science Practices
1. **Concept Explanation** — Explain biological concepts and processes presented in written format.
   - 1.A: Describe biological concepts and processes.
   - 1.B: Explain biological concepts and processes.
   - 1.C: Explain biological concepts and processes in applied contexts.
2. **Visual Representations** — Analyze visual representations of biological concepts and processes.
   - 2.A: Describe characteristics of visual representations of biological concepts and processes.
   - 2.B: Explain relationships between characteristics of biological models in both theoretical and applied contexts.
   - 2.C: Explain how biological models relate to larger principles, concepts, processes, systems, or theories.
   - 2.D: Represent relationships within biological models, including mathematical models, diagrams, flowcharts, and systems.
3. **Questions and Methods** — Determine scientific questions and methods.
   - 3.A: Identify or pose a testable question based on an observation, data, or a model.
   - 3.B: State the null hypothesis or predict the results of an experiment.
   - 3.C: Identify experimental procedures that align with the question (identifying dependent/independent variables, identifying/justifying appropriate controls).
   - 3.D: Propose a new investigation based on an evaluation of the experimental design or evidence.
4. **Representing and Describing Data** — Represent and describe data.
   - 4.A: Construct a graph to represent the data (bar, histogram, line, log scale, dual y, scatter plot, box and whisker, pie chart) with appropriate type, axis labeling, scaling, accurately plotted data with error bars, trend line.
   - 4.B: Describe data from a table or graph (identifying specific data points, describing trends/patterns, describing relationships between variables).
5. **Statistical Tests and Data Analysis** — Perform statistical tests and mathematical calculations to analyze and interpret data.
   - 5.A: Perform mathematical calculations (equations in curriculum, means, rates, ratios, percentages and percent changes).
   - 5.B: Use confidence intervals and error bars to estimate whether sample means are statistically different.
   - 5.C: Perform chi-square hypothesis testing.
   - 5.D: Use data to evaluate a hypothesis or prediction, including rejecting or failing to reject the null hypothesis.
6. **Argumentation** — Develop and justify scientific arguments using evidence.
   - 6.A: Make a scientific claim.
   - 6.B: Support a claim with evidence from biological principles, concepts, processes, and data.
   - 6.C: Provide reasoning to justify a claim by connecting evidence to biological theories.
   - 6.D: Explain the relationship between experimental results and larger biological concepts, processes, or theories.
   - 6.E: Predict the causes or effects of a change in, or disruption to, one or more components in a biological system.

# UNIT 1: Chemistry of Life (8-11% exam weight, ~9-11 class periods)

Big Ideas: ENE, IST, SYI

### Topic 1.1: Structure of Water and Hydrogen Bonding
- **LO 1.1.A** — Explain how the properties of water that result from its polarity and hydrogen bonding affect its biological function.
  - **EK 1.1.A.1**: Living systems depend on the properties of water to sustain life.
    - (i) Water has polarity because of the formation of polar covalent bonds between hydrogen and oxygen. This polarity contributes to hydrogen bonding between and within biological molecules.
    - (ii) Water has a high specific heat capacity, which allows for the maintenance of homeostatic body temperature.
    - (iii) Water has a high heat of vaporization, which allows for evaporative cooling. In living organisms, this property allows for body temperature to be maintained.
  - **EK 1.1.A.2**: The hydrogen bonds between adjacent polar water molecules result in cohesion, adhesion, and surface tension.
  - **Key terms**: polarity, polar covalent bonds, hydrogen bonding, specific heat capacity, heat of vaporization, evaporative cooling, cohesion, adhesion, surface tension, homeostasis
  - **OpenStax reference**: Biology 2e, Chapter 2.2 (Water)

### Topic 1.2: Elements of Life
- **LO 1.2.A** — Describe the composition of macromolecules required by living organisms.
  - **EK 1.2.A.1**: Atoms and molecules from the environment are necessary to build new molecules. Carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen are the most prevalent elements used to build biological molecules such as carbohydrates, proteins, lipids, and nucleic acids.
    - (i) Sulfur is used in the building of proteins.
    - (ii) Phosphorus is used in the building of phospholipids and nucleic acids.
    - (iii) Nitrogen is used in the building of nucleic acids.
  - **Key terms**: carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen, sulfur, phosphorus, macromolecules, carbohydrates, proteins, lipids, nucleic acids
  - **OpenStax reference**: Biology 2e, Chapter 2.1 (Atoms, Isotopes, Ions, Molecules)

### Topic 1.3: Introduction to Macromolecules
- **LO 1.3.A** — Describe the chemical reactions that build and break biological macromolecules.
  - **EK 1.3.A.1**: Hydrolysis is a chemical reaction involving the cleaving of covalent bonds. This breaks down molecules into smaller molecules. When water is added to the bond between monomers in a polymer, the bond is broken.
  - **EK 1.3.A.2**: Dehydration synthesis occurs when two smaller molecules are joined together through covalent bonding. A hydrogen ion is removed from one monomer and a hydroxyl group from the other, causing loss of the equivalent of a water molecule. Connection of many monomers is known as polymerization.
  - **Key terms**: hydrolysis, dehydration synthesis, monomer, polymer, polymerization, covalent bond, hydroxyl group
  - **OpenStax reference**: Biology 2e, Chapter 3.1 (Synthesis of Biological Macromolecules)

### Topic 1.4: Carbohydrates
- **LO 1.4.A** — Describe the structure and function of carbohydrates.
  - **EK 1.4.A.1**: Monosaccharides (simple sugars) are the monomers for polysaccharides (complex carbohydrates). These monomers are connected by covalent bonds to form polymers such as complex carbohydrates, which may be linear or branched.
  - **Illustrative examples**: Cellulose, Starch, Glycogen
  - **Exclusion**: The molecular structure of specific carbohydrate polymers is beyond the scope of the AP Exam.
  - **Key terms**: monosaccharide, polysaccharide, cellulose, starch, glycogen, simple sugar, complex carbohydrate
  - **OpenStax reference**: Biology 2e, Chapter 3.2 (Carbohydrates)

### Topic 1.5: Lipids
- **LO 1.5.A** — Describe the structure and function of lipids.
  - **EK 1.5.A.1**: Lipids are typically nonpolar, hydrophobic molecules. Fatty acids can be saturated or unsaturated.
    - (i) Saturated fatty acids contain only single bonds between carbon atoms.
    - (ii) Unsaturated fatty acids contain at least one double bond between carbon atoms, which causes the carbon chain to kink.
    - (iii) The more double bonds, the more unsaturated the lipid becomes.
    - (iv) The more unsaturated a lipid is, the more liquid it is at room temperature.
  - **EK 1.5.A.2**: Lipids provide a variety of functions. Some examples: fats, steroids including cholesterol, and phospholipids.
    - (i) Fats provide energy storage and support cell function. Can provide insulation for mammals.
    - (ii) Steroids are hormones that support physiological functions including growth, development, energy metabolism, and homeostasis.
    - (iii) Cholesterol provides essential structural stability to animal cell membranes.
    - (iv) Phospholipids group together to form the lipid bilayers found in plasma and cell membranes.
  - **Key terms**: lipid, hydrophobic, nonpolar, fatty acid, saturated, unsaturated, fat, steroid, cholesterol, phospholipid, lipid bilayer
  - **OpenStax reference**: Biology 2e, Chapter 3.3 (Lipids)

### Topic 1.6: Nucleic Acids
- **LO 1.6.A** — Describe the structure and function of DNA and RNA.
  - **EK 1.6.A.1**: In nucleic acids (DNA and RNA), biological information is encoded in sequences of nucleotide monomers. Each nucleotide has: a five-carbon sugar (deoxyribose or ribose), a phosphate, and a nitrogenous base (adenine, thymine, guanine, cytosine, or uracil).
  - **EK 1.6.A.2**: Nucleic acids have a linear sequence of nucleotides with 3' and 5' ends. During synthesis, nucleotides are added to the 3' end.
  - **EK 1.6.A.3**: DNA is structured as an antiparallel double helix. A-T via hydrogen bonds, C-G via hydrogen bonds. In RNA, A pairs with U.
  - **EK 1.6.A.4**: Structural differences between DNA and RNA:
    - (i) DNA contains deoxyribose; RNA contains ribose.
    - (ii) DNA contains thymine; RNA contains uracil.
    - (iii) DNA is typically double stranded; RNA is typically single stranded.
  - **Exclusion**: The molecular structure of specific nucleotides is beyond the scope of the AP Exam.
  - **Key terms**: nucleic acid, DNA, RNA, nucleotide, deoxyribose, ribose, phosphate, nitrogenous base, adenine, thymine, guanine, cytosine, uracil, antiparallel, double helix, 3' end, 5' end, hydrogen bonds, complementary base pairing
  - **OpenStax reference**: Biology 2e, Chapter 3.5 (Nucleic Acids)

### Topic 1.7: Proteins
- **LO 1.7.A** — Describe the structure and function of proteins.
  - **EK 1.7.A.1**: Proteins comprise linear chains of amino acids connected by peptide bonds formed between a carboxyl group (-COOH) and an amine group (-NH2).
  - **EK 1.7.A.2**: Amino acids have a central carbon atom with a hydrogen, a carboxyl group, an amine group, and a variable R group. R groups can be: hydrophobic/nonpolar, hydrophilic/polar, or ionic. R group interactions determine protein structure and function.
  - **EK 1.7.A.3**: The specific sequence of amino acids determines the primary structure and overall shape.
  - **EK 1.7.A.4**: Secondary structures form from interactions between atoms of the polypeptide backbone. Hydrogen bonding forms alpha-helices and beta-pleated sheets.
  - **EK 1.7.A.5**: Tertiary structure results from hydrogen bonds, hydrophobic interactions, ionic interactions, or disulfide bridges.
  - **EK 1.7.A.6**: Quaternary structure arises from interactions between multiple polypeptides. All four levels determine protein function.
  - **Exclusion**: The molecular structure of amino acids is beyond the scope of the AP Exam.
  - **Key terms**: amino acid, peptide bond, carboxyl group, amine group, R group, polypeptide, primary structure, secondary structure, tertiary structure, quaternary structure, alpha-helix, beta-pleated sheet, disulfide bridge, hydrophobic interaction, ionic interaction, protein folding
  - **OpenStax reference**: Biology 2e, Chapter 3.4 (Proteins)

# UNIT 2: Cells (10-13% exam weight, ~14-16 class periods)

Big Ideas: EVO, ENE, SYI

### Topic 2.1: Cell Structure and Function
- **LO 2.1.A** — Explain how the structure and function of subcellular components and organelles contribute to the function of cells.
  - **EK 2.1.A.1**: Ribosomes are comprised of rRNA and protein. They are non-membrane subcellular structures found in all forms of life (reflecting common ancestry). Ribosomes synthesize proteins according to mRNA sequences.
  - **EK 2.1.A.2**: The endomembrane system consists of membrane-bound organelles (ER, Golgi complex, lysosomes, vacuoles, transport vesicles, nuclear envelope, plasma membrane) that work together to modify, package, and transport polysaccharides, lipids, and proteins.
  - **EK 2.1.A.3**: Endoplasmic reticulum provides mechanical support and plays a role in intracellular transport.
    - (i) Rough ER is associated with membrane-bound ribosomes, allows compartmentalization, helps carry out protein synthesis.
    - (ii) Smooth ER functions include detoxification and lipid synthesis.
  - **EK 2.1.A.4**: The Golgi complex is a membrane-bound structure of flattened sacs. Functions: correctly folding and chemically modifying proteins; packaging proteins for trafficking.
  - **EK 2.1.A.5**: Mitochondria have a double membrane. Outer membrane is smooth; inner membrane is highly convoluted (folds = cristae), enabling efficient ATP synthesis.
  - **EK 2.1.A.6**: Lysosomes are membrane-enclosed sacs containing hydrolytic enzymes. They also play a role in apoptosis.
  - **EK 2.1.A.7**: Vacuoles are membrane-bound sacs.
    - (i) In plant cells, a large central vacuole maintains turgor pressure through nutrient and water storage.
    - (ii) In animal cells, vacuoles are smaller and store cellular materials.
  - **EK 2.1.A.8**: Chloroplasts are found in plants and photosynthetic algae. They contain a double membrane and are the location for photosynthesis.
  - **Key terms**: ribosome, rRNA, mRNA, endomembrane system, endoplasmic reticulum, rough ER, smooth ER, Golgi complex, lysosome, vacuole, vesicle, mitochondria, chloroplast, nuclear envelope, plasma membrane, protein synthesis, apoptosis, turgor pressure
  - **OpenStax reference**: Biology 2e, Chapter 4.3-4.5 (Eukaryotic Cells, Endomembrane System, Cytoskeleton)

### Topic 2.2: Cell Size
- **LO 2.2.A** — Explain the effect of surface area-to-volume ratios on the exchange of materials between cells or organisms and the environment.
  - **EK 2.2.A.1**: SA/V ratios affect the ability of a biological system to obtain nutrients, eliminate waste, acquire or dissipate thermal energy, and exchange chemicals and energy.
  - **EK 2.2.A.2**: The surface area of the plasma membrane must be large enough to adequately exchange materials.
    - (i) SA/V ratio can restrict cell size and shape. Smaller cells have higher SA/V and more efficient exchange.
    - (ii) As cells increase in volume, SA/V decreases and demand for internal resources increases.
    - (iii) More complex structures (membrane folds) are necessary for larger cells.
    - (iv) As organisms increase in size, their SA/V decreases, affecting rate of heat exchange.
    - (v) Smaller organisms typically have higher metabolic rate per unit body mass.
  - **Illustrative examples**: Root hairs, guard cells, gut epithelial cells, cilia, stomata
  - **Relevant equations**: Volume of sphere, cube, rectangular solid, cylinder; Surface area of sphere, cube, rectangular solid, cylinder
  - **Key terms**: surface area-to-volume ratio, metabolic rate, heat exchange, membrane folds
  - **OpenStax reference**: Biology 2e, Chapter 4.2 (Prokaryotic Cells — cell size discussion)

### Topic 2.3: Plasma Membrane
- **LO 2.3.A** — Describe the roles of each of the components of the cell membrane in maintaining the internal environment.
  - **EK 2.3.A.1**: Phospholipids have hydrophilic and hydrophobic regions. Polar heads face aqueous environments; nonpolar tails face each other in the interior.
  - **EK 2.3.A.2**: Embedded proteins can be hydrophilic, hydrophobic, or both.
- **LO 2.3.B** — Describe the fluid mosaic model of cell membranes.
  - **EK 2.3.B.1**: Plasma membranes consist of phospholipids embedded with proteins, steroids (cholesterol), glycoproteins, and glycolipids. All can move within the membrane (fluid mosaic model).
  - **Key terms**: phospholipid bilayer, hydrophilic, hydrophobic, fluid mosaic model, integral protein, peripheral protein, glycoprotein, glycolipid, cholesterol
  - **OpenStax reference**: Biology 2e, Chapter 5.1 (Components and Structure of Cell Membranes)

### Topic 2.4: Membrane Permeability
- **LO 2.4.A** — Explain how the structure of biological membranes influences selective permeability.
  - **EK 2.4.A.1**: Plasma membranes separate internal from external environment. Selective permeability results from the hydrophobic interior.
  - **EK 2.4.A.2**: Small nonpolar molecules (N2, O2, CO2) freely cross the membrane. Hydrophilic substances need channels and transport proteins.
  - **EK 2.4.A.3**: Nonpolar hydrocarbon tails prevent movement of ions and polar molecules. Small polar uncharged molecules (H2O, NH3) pass in small amounts.
- **LO 2.4.B** — Describe the role of the cell wall in maintaining cell structure and function.
  - **EK 2.4.B.1**: Cell walls of Bacteria, Archaea, Fungi, and plants provide structural boundary, permeability barrier, and protection from osmotic lysis.
  - **Key terms**: selective permeability, nonpolar, channel protein, transport protein, cell wall, osmotic lysis
  - **OpenStax reference**: Biology 2e, Chapter 5.2 (Passive Transport)

### Topic 2.5: Membrane Transport
- **LO 2.5.A** — Describe the mechanisms that organisms use to maintain solute and water balance.
  - **EK 2.5.A.1**: Selective permeability allows formation of concentration gradients across the membrane.
  - **EK 2.5.A.2**: Passive transport is net movement from high to low concentration without direct metabolic energy input.
  - **EK 2.5.A.3**: Active transport requires direct input of energy, sometimes moving molecules from low to high concentration.
- **LO 2.5.B** — Describe the mechanisms that organisms use to transport large molecules across the plasma membrane.
  - **EK 2.5.B.1**: Endocytosis and exocytosis require energy.
    - (i) Endocytosis: cell takes in large molecules by folding plasma membrane to form vesicles.
    - (ii) Exocytosis: vesicles fuse with membrane to release contents.
  - **Key terms**: concentration gradient, passive transport, active transport, endocytosis, exocytosis, vesicle
  - **OpenStax reference**: Biology 2e, Chapter 5.2-5.4 (Passive Transport, Active Transport, Bulk Transport)

### Topic 2.6: Facilitated Diffusion
- **LO 2.6.A** — Explain how the structure of a molecule affects its ability to pass through the plasma membrane.
  - **EK 2.6.A.1**: Facilitated diffusion requires transport or channel proteins for charged ions.
    - (i) Membranes may become polarized by movement of ions.
    - (ii) Na+ and K+ require channel proteins.
  - **EK 2.6.A.2**: Facilitated diffusion enables movement of large polar molecules with no energy input, down the concentration gradient.
  - **EK 2.6.A.3**: Aquaporins transport large quantities of water across membranes.
  - **Key terms**: facilitated diffusion, channel protein, transport protein, aquaporin, membrane potential, Na+, K+
  - **OpenStax reference**: Biology 2e, Chapter 5.2 (Passive Transport)

### Topic 2.7: Tonicity and Osmoregulation
- **LO 2.7.A** — Explain how concentration gradients affect the movement of molecules across membranes.
  - **EK 2.7.A.1**: External environments can be hypotonic, hypertonic, or isotonic. Water moves by osmosis from high water potential to low water potential.
- **LO 2.7.B** — Explain how osmoregulatory mechanisms contribute to the health and survival of organisms.
  - **EK 2.7.B.1**: Growth and homeostasis are maintained by constant movement of molecules across membranes.
  - **EK 2.7.B.2**: Osmoregulation maintains water balance and controls internal solute composition.
  - **Relevant equations**:
    - Water Potential: Psi = Psi_p + Psi_s
    - Solute Potential: Psi_s = -iCRT (i = ionization constant, C = molar concentration, R = 0.0831 L bars/mol K, T = temperature in Kelvin)
  - **Illustrative examples**: Contractile vacuole in protists, central vacuole in plant cells
  - **Key terms**: osmosis, water potential, solute potential, pressure potential, hypotonic, hypertonic, isotonic, osmoregulation, osmolarity
  - **OpenStax reference**: Biology 2e, Chapter 5.2 (Passive Transport — Osmosis)

### Topic 2.8: Mechanisms of Transport
- **LO 2.8.A** — Describe the processes that allow ions and other molecules to move across membranes.
  - **EK 2.8.A.1**: Metabolic energy (ATP) is required for active transport to establish and maintain electrochemical gradients.
    - (i) Membrane proteins are necessary for active transport.
    - (ii) The Na+/K+ pump and ATPase contribute to maintaining membrane potential.
  - **Key terms**: ATP, Na+/K+ pump, ATPase, electrochemical gradient, membrane potential, active transport
  - **OpenStax reference**: Biology 2e, Chapter 5.3 (Active Transport)

### Topic 2.9: Cell Compartmentalization
- **LO 2.9.A** — Describe the membrane-bound structures of the eukaryotic cell.
  - **EK 2.9.A.1**: Membranes and membrane-bound organelles compartmentalize intracellular metabolic processes and specific enzymatic reactions.
- **LO 2.9.B** — Explain how internal membranes and membrane-bound organelles contribute to compartmentalization.
  - **EK 2.9.B.1**: Internal membranes facilitate cellular processes by minimizing competing interactions and increasing surface area for reactions.
  - **Key terms**: compartmentalization, eukaryotic cell, organelle, metabolic processes
  - **OpenStax reference**: Biology 2e, Chapter 4.3 (Eukaryotic Cells)

### Topic 2.10: Origins of Cell Compartmentalization
- **LO 2.10.A** — Describe similarities and/or differences in compartmentalization between prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells.
  - **EK 2.10.A.1**: Membrane-bound organelles such as mitochondria and chloroplasts evolved from once free-living prokaryotic cells via endosymbiosis.
  - **EK 2.10.A.2**: Prokaryotes typically lack internal membrane-bound organelles but have internal regions with specialized structures and functions.
  - **EK 2.10.A.3**: Eukaryotic cells maintain internal membranes that partition the cell into specialized regions.
  - **Key terms**: endosymbiosis, prokaryote, eukaryote, mitochondria, chloroplast, endosymbiotic theory
  - **OpenStax reference**: Biology 2e, Chapter 4.2 (Prokaryotic Cells), Chapter 4.6 (Connections between Cells)

# UNIT 3: Cellular Energetics (12-16% exam weight, ~12-14 class periods)

Big Ideas: ENE

### Topic 3.1: Enzymes
- **LO 3.1.A** — Explain how enzymes affect the rate of biological reactions.
  - **EK 3.1.A.1**: Enzymes are proteins that are biological catalysts. They facilitate chemical reactions by lowering the activation energy.
  - **EK 3.1.A.2**: For an enzyme-mediated reaction to occur, the shape and charge of the substrate must be compatible with the active site (enzyme-substrate complex model).
  - **Key terms**: enzyme, catalyst, activation energy, substrate, active site, enzyme-substrate complex
  - **OpenStax reference**: Biology 2e, Chapter 6.2 (Potential, Kinetic, Free, and Activation Energy), Chapter 6.5 (Enzymes)

### Topic 3.2: Environmental Impacts on Enzyme Function
- **LO 3.2.A** — Explain how changes to the structure of an enzyme may affect its function.
  - **EK 3.2.A.1**: Change to molecular structure may result in change to function or efficiency.
    - (i) Denaturation occurs when protein structure is disrupted by change in temperature, pH, or chemical environment.
    - (ii) Environmental temperatures and pH outside the optimal range cause structural changes.
  - **EK 3.2.A.2**: In some cases, enzyme denaturation is reversible.
- **LO 3.2.B** — Explain how the cellular environment affects enzyme activity.
  - **EK 3.2.B.1**: Relative concentrations of substrates and products determine efficiency.
  - **EK 3.2.B.2**: Higher temperatures increase molecular speed, increasing collision frequency and reaction rate until optimal temperature.
  - **EK 3.2.B.3**: Competitive inhibitors bind reversibly to the active site. Noncompetitive inhibitors bind to allosteric sites.
  - **Key terms**: denaturation, optimal temperature, optimal pH, competitive inhibitor, noncompetitive inhibitor, allosteric site, enzyme kinetics
  - **OpenStax reference**: Biology 2e, Chapter 6.5 (Enzymes)

### Topic 3.3: Cellular Energy
- **LO 3.3.A** — Describe the role of energy in living organisms.
  - **EK 3.3.A.1**: All living systems require an input of energy.
  - **EK 3.3.A.2**: Life requires a highly ordered system and does not violate the first and second laws of thermodynamics.
    - (i) Energy input must exceed energy loss to maintain order.
    - (ii) Cellular processes that release energy may be coupled with processes that require energy.
    - (iii) Significant loss of order or energy flow results in death.
  - **EK 3.3.A.3**: Energy-related pathways are sequential, allowing controlled transfer of energy. A product of one reaction is typically the reactant for the next.
  - **Exclusion**: Gibbs free energy equation is beyond the scope of the AP Exam.
- **LO 3.3.B** — Explain how shared, conserved, and fundamental processes support the concept of common ancestry.
  - **EK 3.3.B.1**: Core metabolic pathways (e.g., glycolysis, oxidative phosphorylation) are conserved across all domains (Archaea, Bacteria, Eukarya).
  - **Key terms**: thermodynamics (first and second laws), energy coupling, metabolic pathway, ATP, glycolysis, oxidative phosphorylation, common ancestry
  - **OpenStax reference**: Biology 2e, Chapter 6.1-6.3 (Energy and Metabolism)

### Topic 3.4: Photosynthesis
- **LO 3.4.A** — Describe the photosynthetic processes and structural features of the chloroplast.
  - **EK 3.4.A.1**: Photosynthesis uses CO2, H2O, and light energy to make carbohydrates and O2.
    - (i) Photosynthetic organisms capture energy from the sun and produce sugars.
    - (ii) Photosynthesis first evolved in prokaryotes.
    - (iii) Cyanobacterial photosynthesis produced oxygenated atmosphere.
    - (iv) Prokaryotic pathways were the foundation of eukaryotic photosynthesis.
  - **EK 3.4.A.2**: Stroma and thylakoids are found within the chloroplast.
    - (i) Stroma is the fluid within the inner membrane; Calvin cycle occurs there.
    - (ii) Thylakoid membranes contain chlorophyll pigments organized into two photosystems and electron transport proteins.
    - (iii) Thylakoids are organized in stacks called grana. Light reactions occur in grana.
  - **EK 3.4.A.3**: Light reactions yield ATP and NADPH, which power the Calvin cycle.
- **LO 3.4.B** — Explain how cells capture energy from light and transfer it to biological molecules.
  - **EK 3.4.B.1**: ETC reactions occur in chloroplasts, mitochondria, and across prokaryotic membranes. In photosynthesis, electrons pass through thylakoid membrane and are transferred to NADP+ reducing it to NADPH in photosystem I.
  - **EK 3.4.B.2**: Chlorophylls absorb light energy, boosting electrons to higher energy levels in photosystems I and II. Water splits to replace electrons lost from photosystem II.
  - **EK 3.4.B.3**: Photosystems I and II are connected by transfer of electrons through an ETC.
  - **EK 3.4.B.4**: When electrons transfer through ETC, an electrochemical gradient of protons is established across the thylakoid membrane (high proton concentration inside thylakoid).
  - **EK 3.4.B.5**: Proton gradient drives ATP synthesis via ATP synthase (chemiosmosis = photophosphorylation).
  - **EK 3.4.B.6**: Energy captured in light reactions (ATP and NADPH) powers carbohydrate production from CO2 in the Calvin cycle (in the stroma).
  - **Exclusions**: Memorization of Calvin cycle steps, molecule structures, enzyme names (except ATP synthase); specific electron carrier names.
  - **Key terms**: photosynthesis, chloroplast, stroma, thylakoid, grana, chlorophyll, photosystem I, photosystem II, electron transport chain, NADPH, ATP synthase, chemiosmosis, photophosphorylation, Calvin cycle, light reactions, carbon fixation
  - **OpenStax reference**: Biology 2e, Chapter 8.1-8.3 (Overview of Photosynthesis, Light-Dependent Reactions, Calvin Cycle)

### Topic 3.5: Cellular Respiration
- **LO 3.5.A** — Describe the processes and structural features of mitochondria that allow organisms to use energy stored in biological macromolecules.
  - **EK 3.5.A.1**: Cellular respiration uses energy from macromolecules to synthesize ATP. Respiration and fermentation are characteristic of all forms of life.
  - **EK 3.5.A.2**: Aerobic cellular respiration in eukaryotes involves coordinated enzyme-catalyzed reactions that capture energy from macromolecules.
  - **EK 3.5.A.3**: The ETC transfers electrons in oxidation-reduction reactions, establishing an electrochemical gradient.
    - (i) In cellular respiration, electrons from NADH and FADH2 pass to electron acceptors toward oxygen (terminal electron acceptor). Anaerobic prokaryotes use other molecules.
    - (ii) Transfer through ETC forms a proton gradient across inner mitochondrial membrane (high outside, low inside). Folding of inner membrane increases surface area.
    - (iii) Protons flow back through ATP synthase by chemiosmosis (oxidative phosphorylation).
    - (iv) Decoupling oxidative phosphorylation from electron transport generates heat (used by endotherms).
- **LO 3.5.B** — Explain how cells obtain energy from biological macromolecules.
  - **EK 3.5.B.1**: Glycolysis releases energy from glucose to form ATP, NADH, and pyruvate.
  - **EK 3.5.B.2**: Pyruvate is transported to mitochondrion for oxidation. Electrons are released during the Krebs cycle, reducing NAD+ to NADH and FAD to FADH2, releasing CO2.
  - **EK 3.5.B.3**: Krebs cycle occurs in mitochondrial matrix. CO2 is released, ATP is synthesized, electrons transferred by NAD+ and FAD.
  - **EK 3.5.B.4**: Electrons from glycolysis and Krebs cycle are transferred by NADH and FADH2 to ETC in inner mitochondrial membrane.
  - **EK 3.5.B.5**: ETC establishes an electrochemical gradient of protons across inner mitochondrial membrane. pH inside matrix is higher than in intermembrane space.
  - **EK 3.5.B.6**: Fermentation allows glycolysis to proceed without oxygen, producing alcohol and lactic acid.
  - **Exclusions**: Memorization of steps in glycolysis and Krebs cycle; names of specific enzymes and electron carriers (except general names).
  - **Key terms**: cellular respiration, mitochondria, glycolysis, pyruvate, Krebs cycle (citric acid cycle), electron transport chain, NADH, FADH2, oxidative phosphorylation, ATP synthase, chemiosmosis, fermentation, lactic acid, alcohol fermentation, aerobic, anaerobic, endotherm
  - **OpenStax reference**: Biology 2e, Chapter 7.1-7.7 (Glycolysis, Oxidation of Pyruvate, Citric Acid Cycle, Oxidative Phosphorylation, Fermentation)

# UNIT 4: Cell Communication and Cell Cycle (10-15% exam weight, ~12-14 class periods)

Big Ideas: ENE, IST

### Topic 4.1: Cell Communication
- **LO 4.1.A** — Describe the ways that cells can communicate with one another.
  - **EK 4.1.A.1**: Cells communicate through direct contact with other cells or from a distance via chemical signaling.

### Topic 4.2: Introduction to Signal Transduction

    - (ii) Positive feedback amplifies responses. Moves the variable further from the initial set point.
  - **Illustrative examples**: Blood sugar regulation (insulin/glucagon), lactation, onset of labor, ripening of fruit

### Topic 4.5: Cell Cycle
- **LO 4.5.A** — Describe the events that occur in the cell cycle.
  - **EK 4.5.A.1**: The cell cycle is a highly regulated series of events.
    - (i) Sequential stages: interphase (G1, S, G2), mitosis, and cytokinesis.
    - (ii) G1 phase: metabolically active, duplicating organelles.
    - (iii) S phase: DNA replicates to form two sister chromatids connected at centromere.
    - (iv) G2 phase: protein synthesis, ATP production, centrosome replication.
    - (v) G0: cell no longer divides but can reenter cell cycle.
    - (vi) Nondividing cells may exit or be held at a stage.
- **LO 4.5.B** — Explain how mitosis results in the transmission of chromosomes from one generation of cells to the next.
  - **EK 4.5.B.1**: Mitosis ensures transfer of a complete genome from parent cell to two genetically identical daughter cells.
    - (i) Mitosis plays a role in growth, tissue repair, and asexual reproduction.
    - (ii) Sequential steps: prophase, metaphase, anaphase, telophase, alternating with interphase.
    - (iii) Prophase: chromatids condense, spindle forms, centrosomes move to poles.
    - (iv) Metaphase: spindle fibers align chromosomes along the equator.
    - (v) Anaphase: sister chromatids separate as spindle fibers pull them toward poles.
    - (vi) Telophase: spindle breaks down, new nuclear envelope develops, cytoplasm divides.
    - (vii) Cytokinesis: cleavage furrow (animal) or cell plate (plant).
  - **Key terms**: cell cycle, interphase, G1, S phase, G2, G0, mitosis, prophase, metaphase, anaphase, telophase, cytokinesis, sister chromatids, centromere, centrosome, mitotic spindle, cleavage furrow, cell plate

# UNIT 5: Heredity (8-11% exam weight, ~8-10 class periods)

Big Ideas: EVO, IST, SYI

### Topic 5.1: Meiosis
- **LO 5.1.A** — Explain how meiosis results in the transmission of chromosomes from one generation to the next.
  - **EK 5.1.A.1**: Meiosis ensures formation of haploid gametes in sexually reproducing diploid organisms.
  - **EK 5.1.A.2**: Meiosis I:
    - (i) Prophase I: homologous chromosomes pair up and condense, synapsis occurs, chiasmata may form, spindle begins forming, nuclear envelope breaks down.
    - (ii) Metaphase I: homologous pairs align along equator.
    - (iii) Anaphase I: homologous chromosomes separate (sister chromatids remain attached).
    - (iv) Telophase I: spindle breaks down, new nuclear envelope, cytokinesis. Two haploid daughter cells formed.
  - **EK 5.1.A.3**: Meiosis II:
    - (i) Prophase II: spindle forms, sister chromatids attach.
    - (ii) Metaphase II: chromosomes align along metaphase plate.
    - (iii) Anaphase II: centromere proteins break down, sister chromatids separated.
    - (iv) Telophase II: spindle breaks down, new envelope, cytokinesis. Four haploid daughter cells formed.
- **LO 5.1.B** — Describe similarities and differences between the phases and outcomes of mitosis and meiosis.
  - **EK 5.1.B.1**: Both use a spindle apparatus but differ in number of cells produced and genetic content.
  - **Key terms**: meiosis, meiosis I, meiosis II, haploid, diploid, gamete, homologous chromosomes, synapsis, chiasmata, crossing over, independent assortment, nondisjunction
  - **OpenStax reference**: Biology 2e, Chapter 11.1-11.2 (The Process of Meiosis, Sexual Reproduction)

### Topic 5.2: Meiosis and Genetic Diversity
- **LO 5.2.A** — Explain how the process of meiosis generates genetic diversity.
  - **EK 5.2.A.1**: Correct separation ensures each gamete receives a haploid set comprising both maternal and paternal chromosomes. Incorrect separation (nondisjunction) produces non-haploid gametes.
  - **EK 5.2.A.2**: During prophase I, non-sister chromatids exchange genetic material via crossing over (recombination), increasing genetic diversity.
  - **EK 5.2.A.3**: Sexual reproduction increases genetic variation through crossing over, random assortment, and fertilization.
  - **Exclusion**: Details of sexual reproduction cycles in various plants and animals.
  - **Key terms**: crossing over, recombination, random assortment, fertilization, nondisjunction, genetic variation
  - **OpenStax reference**: Biology 2e, Chapter 11.1-11.3 (Meiosis, Sexual Reproduction)

### Topic 5.3: Mendelian Genetics
- **LO 5.3.A** — Explain the inheritance of genes and traits as described by Mendel's laws.
  - **EK 5.3.A.1**: Mendel's laws of segregation and independent assortment apply to genes on different chromosomes.
  - **EK 5.3.A.2**: Fertilization involves fusion of two haploid gametes, restoring the diploid number.
    - (i) Rules of probability can be applied to single-gene trait analysis.
    - (ii) Monohybrid, dihybrid, and test crosses determine dominance.
    - (iii) Genotype = set of alleles; can be homozygous or heterozygous.
    - (iv) Phenotype = observable expression.
    - (v) Patterns of inheritance (autosomal, genetically linked, sex-linked) can be predicted from data/pedigrees. Punnett squares predict genotypes and phenotypes.
  - **Relevant equations**: Laws of Probability — P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) if mutually exclusive; P(A and B) = P(A) x P(B) if independent
  - **Key terms**: Mendel's laws, segregation, independent assortment, dominant, recessive, allele, genotype, phenotype, homozygous, heterozygous, Punnett square, monohybrid cross, dihybrid cross, test cross, pedigree
  - **OpenStax reference**: Biology 2e, Chapter 12.1-12.3 (Mendel's Experiments, Laws of Inheritance)

### Topic 5.4: Non-Mendelian Genetics
- **LO 5.4.A** — Explain deviations from Mendel's model of the inheritance of traits.
  - **EK 5.4.A.1**: Patterns not following Mendel's ratios, identifiable by quantitative analysis.
    - (i) Genetically linked genes on same chromosome; map distance/gene mapping calculated from recombination frequency.
    - (ii) Codominance: both alleles expressed in heterozygote.
    - (iii) Incomplete dominance: heterozygote phenotype is a blend.
  - **EK 5.4.A.2**: Sex-linked traits (X- or Y-linked) on sex chromosomes. Inherited at higher rates in XY individuals. Other species use ZW or haplodiploidy.
  - **EK 5.4.A.3**: Pleiotropy: single gene results in multiple traits/effects.
  - **EK 5.4.A.4**: Non-nuclear inheritance.
    - (i) Chloroplasts and mitochondria randomly assorted; don't follow Mendelian rules.
    - (ii) In animals, mitochondria usually transmitted by egg (maternally inherited).
    - (iii) In plants, mitochondria and chloroplasts transmitted in ovule (maternally inherited).
  - **Key terms**: genetic linkage, gene mapping, map distance, codominance, incomplete dominance, sex-linked, X-linked, Y-linked, pleiotropy, non-nuclear inheritance, maternal inheritance, mitochondrial DNA, chi-square test
  - **Relevant equation**: Chi-square = sum of (observed - expected)^2 / expected
  - **OpenStax reference**: Biology 2e, Chapter 12.2-12.3 (Characteristics and Traits, Laws of Inheritance), Chapter 13.1 (Chromosomal Theory and Genetic Linkage)

### Topic 5.5: Environmental Effects on Phenotype
- **LO 5.5.A** — Explain how the same genotype can result in multiple phenotypes under different environmental conditions.
  - **EK 5.5.A.1**: Environmental conditions influence gene expression and can lead to phenotypic plasticity.
  - **Illustrative examples**: Flower color based on soil pH, seasonal fur color in arctic animals, sex determination in reptiles, effect of UV on melanin production, pheromone production in yeast
  - **Key terms**: phenotypic plasticity, environmental influence on gene expression, epigenetics
  - **OpenStax reference**: Biology 2e, Chapter 12.3 (Laws of Inheritance)

# UNIT 6: Gene Expression and Regulation (12-16% exam weight, ~18-20 class periods)

### Topic 6.1: DNA and RNA Structure
- **LO 6.1.A** — Describe the structures involved in passing hereditary information from one generation to the next.
  - **EK 6.1.A.1**: Genetic information stored in and passed through DNA (and sometimes RNA).
    - (i) Prokaryotes typically have circular chromosomes.
    - (ii) Eukaryotes have multiple linear chromosomes comprised of DNA, condensed using histones.
  - **EK 6.1.A.2**: Both prokaryotes and eukaryotes can contain plasmids (extra-chromosomal circular DNA).
- **LO 6.1.B** — Describe the characteristics of DNA that allow it to be used as hereditary material.
  - **EK 6.1.B.1**: Specific nucleotide base pairing is conserved through evolution.
    - (i) Purines (guanine, adenine) have a double ring structure.
    - (ii) Pyrimidines (cytosine, thymine, uracil) have a single ring structure.
    - (iii) Purines pair with pyrimidines: A-T (or A-U in RNA), G-C.
  - **Key terms**: chromosome, linear chromosome, circular chromosome, histone, chromatin, plasmid, purine, pyrimidine, base pairing
  - **OpenStax reference**: Biology 2e, Chapter 14.2 (DNA Structure and Sequencing)

### Topic 6.2: DNA Replication
- **LO 6.2.A** — Describe the mechanisms by which genetic information is copied for transmission between generations.
  - **EK 6.2.A.1**: DNA replication ensures continuity of hereditary information.
    - (i) DNA synthesized 5' to 3' direction.
    - (ii) Semiconservative: one strand serves as template.
    - (iii) Helicase unwinds the strands.
    - (iv) Topoisomerase relaxes supercoiling in front of replication fork.
    - (v) DNA polymerase requires RNA primers to initiate.
    - (vi) DNA polymerase synthesizes continuously on leading strand, discontinuously on lagging strand.
    - (vii) Ligase joins fragments on lagging strand.
  - **Exclusion**: Names of specific enzymes beyond DNA polymerase, ligase, RNA polymerase, helicase, and topoisomerase.
  - **Key terms**: DNA replication, semiconservative, helicase, topoisomerase, DNA polymerase, RNA primer, leading strand, lagging strand, ligase, Okazaki fragments, replication fork
  - **OpenStax reference**: Biology 2e, Chapter 14.4 (DNA Replication in Eukaryotes)

### Topic 6.3: Transcription and RNA Processing
- **LO 6.3.A** — Describe the mechanisms by which genetic information flows from DNA to RNA to protein.
  - **EK 6.3.A.1**: RNA structure determines RNA function.
    - (i) mRNA carries information from DNA to ribosome.
    - (ii) tRNA molecules bind specific amino acids and have anticodon sequences that pair with mRNA codons.
    - (iii) rRNA molecules are building blocks of ribosomes.
  - **EK 6.3.A.2**: RNA polymerase uses a single template strand of DNA (transcription).
  - **EK 6.3.A.3**: RNA polymerase synthesizes mRNA 5' to 3' by reading template 3' to 5'.
  - **EK 6.3.A.4**: In eukaryotes, mRNA undergoes modifications:
    - (i) Poly-A tail makes mRNA more stable.
    - (ii) GTP cap helps with ribosomal recognition.
    - (iii) Excision of introns and splicing of exons (alternative splicing).
  - **Key terms**: transcription, mRNA, tRNA, rRNA, RNA polymerase, template strand, codon, anticodon, poly-A tail, 5' cap (GTP cap), intron, exon, alternative splicing, central dogma
  - **OpenStax reference**: Biology 2e, Chapter 15.1-15.2 (The Genetic Code, Prokaryotic Transcription), Chapter 15.4 (RNA Processing in Eukaryotes)

### Topic 6.4: Translation
- **LO 6.4.A** — Explain how the phenotype of an organism is determined by its genotype.
  - **EK 6.4.A.1**: Translation occurs on ribosomes in cytoplasm (both pro- and eukaryotes) and on rough ER surface in eukaryotes.
  - **EK 6.4.A.2**: In prokaryotes, translation occurs while mRNA is being transcribed.
  - **EK 6.4.A.3**: Translation involves initiation, elongation, and termination.
    - (i) Initiated when rRNA interacts with mRNA at start codon (AUG = methionine).
    - (ii) mRNA read in triplets called codons.
    - (iii) Each codon encodes a specific amino acid (deducible from genetic code chart). Many amino acids encoded by more than one codon.
    - (iv) Nearly all organisms use the same genetic code (evidence for common ancestry).
    - (v) tRNA brings correct amino acid specified by codon.
    - (vi) Amino acid transferred to growing polypeptide chain.
    - (vii) Process continues until stop codon reached.
    - (viii) Translation terminates with release of newly synthesized protein.
  - **EK 6.4.A.4**: In retroviruses, information flows from RNA to DNA via reverse transcriptase, which copies viral RNA into DNA that integrates into host genome.
  - **Key terms**: translation, ribosome, start codon (AUG), stop codon, codon, genetic code, initiation, elongation, termination, polypeptide, reverse transcriptase, retrovirus
  - **OpenStax reference**: Biology 2e, Chapter 15.5 (Ribosomes and Protein Synthesis)

### Topic 6.5: Regulation of Gene Expression
- **LO 6.5.A** — Describe the types of interactions that regulate gene expression.
  - **EK 6.5.A.1**: Regulatory sequences interact with regulatory proteins to control transcription. Some genes are constitutively expressed; others are inducible.
  - **EK 6.5.A.2**: Epigenetic changes can affect gene expression through reversible modifications of DNA or histones.
  - **EK 6.5.A.3**: Phenotype is determined by combination of expressed genes and their expression levels.
    - (i) Observable cell differentiation results from expression of tissue-specific proteins.
    - (ii) Induction of transcription factors during development results in sequential gene expression.
    - (iii) Function and amount of gene products determine phenotype.
- **LO 6.5.B** — Explain how the location of regulatory sequences relates to their function.
  - **EK 6.5.B.1**: Both prokaryotes and eukaryotes have coordinately regulated gene groups.
    - (i) Prokaryotes regulate operons (inducible or repressible).
    - (ii) In eukaryotes, same transcription factors can coordinately regulate groups of genes.
  - **Key terms**: gene regulation, regulatory sequence, transcription factor, constitutive expression, inducible gene, epigenetic, histone modification, DNA methylation, operon, cell differentiation
  - **OpenStax reference**: Biology 2e, Chapter 16.1-16.7 (Regulation of Gene Expression)

### Topic 6.6: Gene Expression and Cell Specialization

  - **EK 6.6.A.1**: RNA polymerase and transcription factors bind to promoter or enhancer sequences to initiate transcription. Can be upstream or downstream of start site.

### Topic 6.7: Mutations
- **LO 6.7.A** — Describe the various types of mutation.
  - **EK 6.7.A.1**: Alterations in DNA can cause changes in protein and consequent phenotype. Mutations can be beneficial, detrimental, or neutral.
    - (i) Point mutations: one nucleotide substituted.
    - (ii) Frameshift mutations: insertion or deletion shifts reading frame.
    - (iii) Nonsense mutations: point mutation causing premature stop.
    - (iv) Silent mutations: no effect on amino acid sequence.
  - **Exclusion**: Knowledge of specific mutations and their effects.
- **LO 6.7.B** — Explain how changes in genotype may result in changes in phenotype.
  - **EK 6.7.B.1**: Errors in DNA replication or repair, and external factors (radiation, reactive chemicals) cause random mutations.
    - (i) Whether beneficial, detrimental, or neutral depends on environmental context.
    - (ii) Mutations are a source of genetic variation.
  - **EK 6.7.B.2**: Errors in mitosis or meiosis can result in phenotype changes.
    - (i) Nondisjunction often results in triploidy (aneuploidy) and new phenotypes.

  - **Illustrative examples**: CFTR gene mutations (cystic fibrosis), MC1R gene (adaptive melanism in pocket mice), sickle cell anemia

    - (ii) Genetic drift: change in allele frequencies in small populations (nonselective).

### Topic 7.5: Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium
- **LO 7.5.A** — Describe the conditions under which allele and genotype frequencies will change in populations.
  - **EK 7.5.A.1**: Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium is a model for non-evolving populations. Conditions:
    - (i) Large population size
    - (ii) No migration
    - (iii) No new mutations
    - (iv) Random mating
    - (v) No natural selection
    - These conditions are never met, but provide a valuable null hypothesis.
  - **EK 7.5.A.2**: Allele frequencies can be calculated from genotype frequencies.
  - **Relevant equations**:
    - Hardy-Weinberg: p^2 + 2pq + q^2 = 1; p + q = 1
    - p = frequency of allele 1; q = frequency of allele 2
  - **Key terms**: Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium, allele frequency, genotype frequency, null hypothesis, p, q, equilibrium conditions
  - **OpenStax reference**: Biology 2e, Chapter 19.1 (Population Evolution)

  - **EK 7.8.A.1**: All species have evolved and continue to evolve. Examples:
    - (i) Genomic changes over time
    - (ii) Continuous change in the fossil record
    - (iii) Evolution of resistance to antibiotics, pesticides, herbicides, or chemotherapy drugs
    - (iv) Pathogens evolving and causing emergent diseases
  - **Key terms**: antibiotic resistance, pesticide resistance, emergent diseases, ongoing evolution
  - **OpenStax reference**: Biology 2e, Chapter 19.3 (Adaptive Evolution)

### Topic 7.9: Phylogeny
- **LO 7.9.A** — Describe the types of evidence that can be used to infer evolutionary relationships.
  - **EK 7.9.A.1**: Phylogenetic trees and cladograms show hypothetical evolutionary relationships that can be tested.
  - **EK 7.9.A.2**: Phylogenetic trees show amount of change over time (calibrated by fossils or molecular clock); cladograms do not.
  - **EK 7.9.A.3**: Traits gained or lost during evolution construct trees/cladograms.
    - (i) Shared derived characters indicate common ancestry.
    - (ii) Molecular data typically more accurate and reliable than morphological traits.
- **LO 7.9.B** — Explain how phylogenetic trees and cladograms can be used to infer evolutionary relatedness.
  - **EK 7.9.B.1**: Nodes represent most recent common ancestor of two groups.
  - **EK 7.9.B.2**: Can be constructed from morphological similarities and from DNA/protein sequence similarities.
  - **EK 7.9.B.3**: Represent hypotheses constantly being revised based on evidence.
  - **Key terms**: phylogenetic tree, cladogram, clade, node, out-group, shared derived character (synapomorphy), molecular clock, common ancestor
  - **OpenStax reference**: Biology 2e, Chapter 20.1-20.2 (Organizing Life on Earth, Determining Evolutionary Relationships)

### Topic 7.10: Speciation
- **LO 7.10.A** — Describe the conditions under which new species may arise.
  - **EK 7.10.A.1**: Speciation occurs when two populations become reproductively isolated.
  - **EK 7.10.A.2**: Biological species concept: species capable of interbreeding and producing viable, fertile offspring.
- **LO 7.10.B** — Describe the rate of evolution and speciation under different ecological conditions.
  - **EK 7.10.B.1**: Punctuated equilibrium: rapid evolution after long stasis. Gradualism: slow evolution over millions of years.
  - **EK 7.10.B.2**: Divergent evolution: adaptation to new habitats results in phenotypic diversification. Adaptive radiation occurs as new habitats become available.
  - **EK 7.10.B.3**: Convergent evolution: similar selective pressures result in similar adaptations in different populations/species.
- **LO 7.10.C** — Explain the processes and mechanisms that drive speciation.
  - **EK 7.10.C.1**: Sympatric speciation (with geographic overlap) and allopatric speciation (geographically isolated).
  - **EK 7.10.C.2**: Various pre-zygotic and post-zygotic mechanisms maintain reproductive isolation and prevent gene flow.
  - **Illustrative examples**: Hawaiian Drosophila, Caribbean Anolis, Apple maggot Rhagoletis
  - **Key terms**: speciation, reproductive isolation, biological species concept, punctuated equilibrium, gradualism, divergent evolution, convergent evolution, adaptive radiation, sympatric speciation, allopatric speciation, pre-zygotic barriers, post-zygotic barriers
  - **OpenStax reference**: Biology 2e, Chapter 18.2-18.3 (Formation of New Species, Reconnection and Speciation Rates)

### Topic 7.11: Variations in Populations
- **LO 7.11.A** — Explain how the genetic diversity of a species or population affects its ability to withstand environmental pressures.
  - **EK 7.11.A.1**: Level of variation affects population dynamics.
    - (i) Genetic diversity influences ability to respond to environmental changes. Low diversity = risk of decline/extinction.
    - (ii) Genetically diverse populations are more resilient.
    - (iii) Alleles adaptive in one environment may be deleterious in another.
  - **Illustrative examples**: California condors, black-footed ferrets, prairie chickens, potato blight, corn rust, antibiotic resistance
  - **Key terms**: genetic diversity, population resilience, extinction risk, environmental pressure
  - **OpenStax reference**: Biology 2e, Chapter 19.2 (Population Genetics)

### Topic 7.12: Origins of Life on Earth
- **LO 7.12.A** — Describe the scientific evidence that supports models of the origin of life on Earth.
  - **EK 7.12.A.1**: Origin of life supported by scientific evidence.
    - (i) Geological evidence reinforces models.
    - (ii) Earth formed ~4.6 bya; environment too hostile until ~3.9 bya; earliest fossil evidence ~3.5 bya.
  - **EK 7.12.A.2**: RNA world hypothesis: RNA could have been earliest genetic material. Three assumptions:
    - (i) At some point, genetic continuity was assured by RNA replication.
    - (ii) Base-pairing is necessary for replication.
    - (iii) Genetically encoded proteins were not involved as catalysts.
  - **Key terms**: origin of life, RNA world hypothesis, abiogenesis, fossil evidence, geological timescale, 4.6 billion years ago, 3.5 billion years ago
  - **OpenStax reference**: Biology 2e, Chapter 22.1 (Prokaryotic Diversity)

# UNIT 8: Ecology (10-15% exam weight, ~19-21 class periods)

Big Ideas: EVO, ENE, IST, SYI (all four!)

### Topic 8.1: Responses to the Environment
- **LO 8.1.A** — Explain how the behavioral and physiological response of an organism is related to changes in internal or external environment.
  - **EK 8.1.A.1**: Organisms respond to changes through behavioral and physiological mechanisms.
  - **EK 8.1.A.2**: Organisms exchange information in response to internal changes and external cues, which can change behavior.
  - **Exclusion**: Knowledge of specific behavioral or physiological mechanisms.
- **LO 8.1.B** — Explain how the behavioral responses of organisms affect their overall fitness and may contribute to population success.
  - **EK 8.1.B.1**: Organisms communicate through various mechanisms (visual, audible, tactile, electrical, chemical).
    - (i) Signaling behaviors produce changes in behavior of others and can result in differential reproductive success.
    - (ii) Animals use signals for dominance, food finding, territory, reproductive success.
  - **EK 8.1.B.2**: Responses to information are vital to natural selection and evolution.
    - (i) Fitness favors innate and learned behaviors that increase survival and reproduction.
    - (ii) Cooperative behavior tends to increase individual fitness and population survival.
  - **Exclusions**: Specific communication mechanisms and community behavioral systems.
  - **Illustrative examples**: Photoperiodism, phototropism, taxis, kinesis, nocturnal/diurnal activity, fight-or-flight, predator warnings, plant responses to herbivory, territorial marking, bird songs, pack behavior, kin selection, courtship, foraging
  - **Key terms**: behavior, innate behavior, learned behavior, photoperiodism, phototropism, taxis, kinesis, fight-or-flight response, cooperative behavior, kin selection, communication (visual, audible, tactile, chemical, electrical)
  - **OpenStax reference**: Biology 2e, Chapter 45.1-45.7 (Animal Behavior)

### Topic 8.2: Energy Flow Through Ecosystems
- **LO 8.2.A** — Describe the strategies organisms use to acquire and use energy.
  - **EK 8.2.A.1**: Organisms use energy to grow, reproduce, and maintain homeostasis.
    - (i) Endotherms use metabolic thermal energy; ectotherms regulate temperature behaviorally.
    - (ii) Net gain in energy results in growth and reproduction.
    - (iii) Net loss results in loss of mass, decreased reproduction, and eventually death.
  - **EK 8.2.A.2**: Organisms use various reproductive strategies in response to energy availability.
- **LO 8.2.B** — Explain how energy flows and matter cycles through trophic levels.
  - **EK 8.2.B.1**: Ecological levels include populations, communities, ecosystems, and biomes.
  - **EK 8.2.B.2**: Energy flows through ecosystems; matter and nutrients cycle via biogeochemical cycles. Conservation of matter. Cycles are interdependent.
  - **EK 8.2.B.3**: Biogeochemical cycles include abiotic and biotic reservoirs and processes cycling matter between them.
  - **EK 8.2.B.4**: Hydrologic (water) cycle: reservoirs (oceans, surface water, atmosphere, organisms); processes (evaporation, condensation, precipitation, transpiration).
  - **EK 8.2.B.5**: Carbon cycle: recycling carbon atoms through biosphere as carbohydrates and back as CO2. Four parts: photosynthesis, cellular respiration, decomposition, combustion.
  - **EK 8.2.B.6**: Nitrogen cycle: nitrogen fixation, assimilation, ammonification, nitrification, denitrification. Largest reservoir is atmosphere. N2 fixed into NH3, ionizes to NH4+.
  - **EK 8.2.B.7**: Phosphorus cycle: weathering releases PO4^3- into soil/groundwater. Producers take in phosphate; consumers eat producers. Returns to soil via decomposition or excretion.
- **LO 8.2.C** — Explain how changes in energy availability affect populations, communities, and ecosystems.
  - **EK 8.2.C.1**: Changes in energy availability can change population size.
  - **EK 8.2.C.2**: Can result in ecosystem disruptions.
    - (i) Change in sunlight can affect number/size of trophic levels (producers, primary/secondary/tertiary/quaternary consumers, decomposers).
    - (ii) Change in biomass/number of producers affects other trophic levels.
- **LO 8.2.D** — Explain how activities of autotrophs and heterotrophs enable energy flow within an ecosystem.
  - **EK 8.2.D.1**: Autotrophs capture energy from physical or chemical sources.
    - (i) Photosynthetic organisms capture sunlight (primary productivity).
    - (ii) Chemosynthetic organisms capture energy from inorganic molecules (can occur without oxygen).
  - **EK 8.2.D.2**: Heterotrophs (carnivores, herbivores, omnivores, decomposers, scavengers) metabolize carbohydrates, lipids, and proteins.
  - **Illustrative examples**: Seasonal reproduction, life-history strategy, food chains/webs, trophic pyramids
  - **Key terms**: endotherm, ectotherm, autotroph, heterotroph, producer, consumer, decomposer, trophic level, food chain, food web, trophic pyramid, primary productivity, biogeochemical cycle, carbon cycle, nitrogen cycle, phosphorus cycle, water cycle, nitrogen fixation, nitrification, denitrification, ammonification, chemosynthesis
  - **OpenStax reference**: Biology 2e, Chapter 46.1-46.3 (Ecology of Ecosystems)

### Topic 8.3: Population Ecology
- **LO 8.3.A** — Describe factors that influence growth dynamics of populations.
  - **EK 8.3.A.1**: Populations comprise individual organisms of the same species interacting with each other and the environment.
  - **EK 8.3.A.2**: Many adaptations are related to obtaining and using energy and matter.
    - (i) Population growth depends on birth rate, death rate, and population size.
  - **Relevant equations**:
    - Population Growth: dN/dt = B - D (B = birth rate, D = death rate, N = population size)
    - Exponential Growth: dN/dt = r_max * N (r_max = maximum per capita growth rate)
  - **Key terms**: population ecology, population size, birth rate, death rate, population growth, exponential growth, per capita growth rate
  - **OpenStax reference**: Biology 2e, Chapter 45.1-45.3 (Population Demography, Life Histories, Environmental Limits to Population Growth)

### Topic 8.4: Effect of Density on Populations
- **LO 8.4.A** — Explain how the density of a population affects and is determined by resource availability.
  - **EK 8.4.A.1**: Carrying capacity is the sustainable abundance of a species supported by available resources.
  - **EK 8.4.A.2**: As density-dependent and density-independent limits are imposed, logistic growth typically ensues.
  - **Relevant equation**:
    - Logistic Growth: dN/dt = r_max * N * ((K - N) / K) where K = carrying capacity
  - **Key terms**: carrying capacity (K), density-dependent factors, density-independent factors, logistic growth, limiting factors
  - **OpenStax reference**: Biology 2e, Chapter 45.3 (Environmental Limits to Population Growth)

### Topic 8.5: Community Ecology
- **LO 8.5.A** — Describe the structure of a community according to its species composition and diversity.
  - **EK 8.5.A.1**: Community structure measured by species composition and species diversity.
  - **Relevant equation**: Simpson's Diversity Index: D = 1 - sum((n/N)^2) where n = total organisms of one species, N = total organisms of all species
- **LO 8.5.B** — Explain how interactions within and among populations influence community structure.
  - **EK 8.5.B.1**: Communities are groups of interacting populations that change over time.
  - **EK 8.5.B.2**: Interactions determine how populations access energy and matter.
  - **EK 8.5.B.3**: Relationships can be characterized by positive and negative effects. Examples: predator/prey, cooperation, trophic cascades, niche partitioning.
  - **EK 8.5.B.4**: Competition, predation, and symbioses (parasitism, mutualism, commensalism) drive population dynamics.
  - **Key terms**: community ecology, species composition, species diversity, Simpson's Diversity Index, predator-prey, competition, symbiosis, parasitism, mutualism, commensalism, niche partitioning, trophic cascade, keystone species
  - **OpenStax reference**: Biology 2e, Chapter 45.4-45.6 (Community Ecology)

### Topic 8.6: Biodiversity
- **LO 8.6.A** — Describe the relationship between ecosystem diversity and its resilience to changes.
  - **EK 8.6.A.1**: Ecosystems with fewer parts and little diversity are often less resilient.
  - **EK 8.6.A.2**: Keystone species, producers, and essential abiotic/biotic factors maintain diversity.
- **LO 8.6.B** — Explain how the addition or removal of any component affects an ecosystem short-term and long-term.
  - **EK 8.6.B.1**: The effects of keystone species are disproportionate to their abundance. Removal often collapses the ecosystem.
  - **Key terms**: biodiversity, ecosystem resilience, keystone species, ecosystem stability
  - **OpenStax reference**: Biology 2e, Chapter 47.1-47.2 (The Biodiversity Crisis, The Importance of Biodiversity)

### Topic 8.7: Disruptions in Ecosystems
- **LO 8.7.A** — Explain the interaction between the environment and random or preexisting variations in populations.
  - **EK 8.7.A.1**: An adaptation is a genetic variation favored by selection, manifesting as a trait providing an advantage in a particular environment.
  - **EK 8.7.A.2**: Heterozygote advantage: heterozygous genotype has higher relative fitness than either homozygous genotype.
  - **EK 8.7.A.3**: Mutations are not directed by specific environmental pressures.
- **LO 8.7.B** — Explain how invasive species affect ecosystem dynamics.
  - **EK 8.7.B.1**: Intentional or unintentional introduction of invasive species can allow exploitation of new niches free of predators/competitors, or outcompeting native species.
- **LO 8.7.C** — Describe human activities that lead to changes in ecosystem structure and dynamics.
  - **EK 8.7.C.1**: Human impact accelerates changes at local and global levels, causing extinctions.
    - (i) Biomagnification
    - (ii) Eutrophication
- **LO 8.7.D** — Explain how geological and meteorological activity leads to changes in ecosystem structure and dynamics.
  - **EK 8.7.D.1**: Geological and meteorological events affect habitat change and ecosystem distribution.
  - **Illustrative examples**: Kudzu, zebra mussels, Dutch elm disease, potato blight, global climate change, logging, urbanization, monocropping, El Nino, continental drift, meteor impact on dinosaurs
  - **Key terms**: adaptation, heterozygote advantage, invasive species, biomagnification, eutrophication, human impact, habitat loss, extinction, climate change, geological events, biogeography
  - **OpenStax reference**: Biology 2e, Chapter 47.1-47.3 (The Biodiversity Crisis, Threats to Biodiversity, Preserving Biodiversity)
