## Topic 1: Appeasement Policy

**Wikipedia URL:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Appeasement *(403 blocked — could not verify directly)*
**Primary sources consulted:**
- https://www.britannica.com/biography/Neville-Chamberlain
- https://www.britannica.com/topic/appeasement-foreign-policy
- https://encyclopedia.ushmm.org/content/en/article/neville-chamberlain
- https://www.iwm.org.uk/history/was-chamberlain-wrong-to-appease-hitler

### Exact Dates
- **1937**: Chamberlain takes office as British Prime Minister
- **September 29–30, 1938**: Munich Agreement signed (culmination of appeasement)
- **March 15–16, 1939**: Hitler seizes rest of Czechoslovakia — appeasement officially abandoned
- **March 31, 1939**: Chamberlain issues guarantee to Poland, definitively repudiating appeasement

### Key People
- **Neville Chamberlain** — British Prime Minister, chief architect of appeasement
- **Édouard Daladier** — French Premier, co-signatory at Munich
- **Winston Churchill** — Leading opponent of appeasement in Parliament
- **Adolf Hitler** — Target of appeasement policy

### Chamberlain's Rationale
Per Britannica: appeasement was driven by multiple factors:
1. **Military unpreparedness** — Britain's military was not ready for another European war; Chamberlain "immediately ordered the acceleration of the British rearmament program" after Munich, suggesting rearmament was still incomplete
2. **Anti-war sentiment** — Strong public revulsion against another war after WWI's catastrophic losses
3. **Belief in negotiation** — Chamberlain genuinely believed Hitler could be reasoned with; he sought to accommodate Italian and German grievances through negotiation
4. **Fear of aerial bombing** — The prospect of cities being bombed into rubble (as Guernica showed) fueled public fear

Per USHMM: "neither France nor Britain felt ready to fight a war with Germany."

### British Public Opinion
- The Munich Agreement "was greeted with jubilation" across Britain (USHMM)
- "Thousands lined the five-mile route from Heston Aerodrome to Buckingham Palace, cheering" when Chamberlain returned from Munich (History.com)
- King George VI made the "unprecedented gesture of allowing Chamberlain alone to receive [the crowd's] applause from the balcony" of Buckingham Palace
- Appeasement was at the time "a popular and seemingly pragmatic policy" (USHMM)
- Specific polling numbers: UNVERIFIED from retrieved sources

### Notable Quotes
- **Chamberlain (September 30, 1938, at 10 Downing Street):** *"My good friends, this is the second time in our history that there has come back from Germany to Downing Street peace with honor. I believe it is peace for our time."* (History.com)
- **Churchill (responding to Munich):** *"You were given the choice between war and dishonour. You chose dishonour and you will have war."* (Britannica)
- **Churchill, alternate formulation of Munich criticism:** *"an unmitigated disaster"* (USHMM)

### Key Numbers
- Czechoslovakia lost **11,000 square miles** of territory and **more than 3 million** of its population via the Munich Agreement (Britannica)

### Verified Facts for Quiz Questions
1. Chamberlain's "peace for our time" speech was delivered on **September 30, 1938**, from a window at **10 Downing Street** (not at the airport, as commonly misremembered)
2. Appeasement policy officially ended when Hitler seized the remainder of Czechoslovakia in **March 1939**, prompting Chamberlain to guarantee Poland's independence on **March 31, 1939**
3. Both the British and French crowds welcomed their leaders home as heroes after Munich — the policy enjoyed mass public support at the time

### Commonly Confused / Surprising
- The "peace for our time" speech was given at Downing Street, not at Heston Aerodrome where Chamberlain landed — the aerodrome speech contained similar but slightly different wording
- Appeasement is now synonymous with failure, but at the time it was mainstream and popular — Churchill was the outlier, not Chamberlain
- Britannica notes that "Chamberlain's appeasement bought the Allies several months to prepare for the coming war" — some historians argue this was a strategic benefit, not pure failure
- The "Italian plan" at Munich had actually been prepared by the German Foreign Office — the agreement was less multilateral than it appeared

## Topic 2: Anschluss (Austrian Annexation)

**Wikipedia URL:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anschluss *(403 blocked)*
**Primary sources consulted:**
- https://www.britannica.com/event/Anschluss
- https://www.history.com/this-day-in-history/march-12/germany-annexes-austria

### Exact Dates
- **February 1938**: Hitler forces Austrian Chancellor Schuschnigg to give Austrian Nazis free rein
- **March 9, 1938**: Schuschnigg calls a plebiscite on Austrian independence
- **March 11, 1938**: Hitler demands plebiscite postponed and Schuschnigg's resignation; Schuschnigg resigns; Nazi Seyss-Inquart appointed Chancellor
- **March 12, 1938**: German troops enter Austria (Anschluss day)
- **March 13, 1938**: Formal proclamation of annexation — Austria declared part of the German Reich
- **April 10, 1938**: Nazi-controlled plebiscite held — **99.7% approval** result announced

### Key People
- **Adolf Hitler** — Ordered and personally accompanied the annexation
- **Kurt von Schuschnigg** — Austrian Chancellor forced to resign; subsequently imprisoned by Nazis until 1945
- **Arthur Seyss-Inquart** — Austrian Nazi appointed Chancellor on March 11; collaborated in proclaiming Anschluss; later executed at Nuremberg as war criminal
- **King Victor Emmanuel III of Italy** — Italy's non-intervention removed the last obstacle to German action (Mussolini had protected Austria in 1934)

### Key Numbers
- **99.7%** — Result of April 10 plebiscite (conducted under Nazi control, not a free vote)
- Austria's population at annexation: UNVERIFIED from retrieved sources (historical figure is approximately 6.7 million — UNVERIFIED)

### Verified Facts for Quiz Questions
1. German troops entered Austria on **March 12, 1938** — Hitler personally crossed the border and received enthusiastic crowds
2. Austrian Chancellor **Kurt von Schuschnigg** was imprisoned by the Nazis after his resignation and not released until 1945
3. The subsequent plebiscite returned **99.7% approval** — widely recognized as manipulated; the real vote Schuschnigg had called was never held

### Commonly Confused / Surprising
- The Anschluss had been discussed/desired by many Austrians since 1919, but "Hitler's rise to power made it less attractive" to many — the 1938 event was coerced, not the organic union many had imagined
- Mussolini had **protected** Austrian independence in 1934 (when he mobilized troops to the Brenner Pass); by 1938 he had abandoned that commitment, removing the last obstacle
- The *Anschluss* happened with virtually no international resistance — Britain and France protested but took no action
- It was "the second time in four years" Austrian Nazis had attempted to seize the government (first attempt: 1934 coup)

## Topic 3: Sudetenland Crisis (1938)

**Wikipedia URL:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sudetenland *(403 blocked)*
**Primary sources consulted:**
- https://www.britannica.com/place/Sudetenland
- https://www.britannica.com/event/Munich-Agreement (overlapping)

### Exact Dates
- **1918–19**: Sudetenland incorporated into new Czechoslovakia (Treaty of Saint-Germain)
- **May 1935**: Henlein's Sudeten German Nazi Party wins nearly two-thirds of the Sudeten German vote in elections
- **April 1938**: Henlein presents the "Karlsbad Programme" — demands for Sudeten autonomy
- **September 15, 1938**: Chamberlain flies to Berchtesgaden to meet Hitler
- **September 22, 1938**: Bad Godesberg meeting — Hitler stiffens demands; wants military occupation by September 28
- **September 29–30, 1938**: Munich Conference settles the crisis; Sudetenland to be occupied by Germany by October 10

### Key People
- **Konrad Henlein** — Leader of the Sudeten German Nazi Party (SdP); received nearly two-thirds of the Sudeten German vote in 1935
- **Adolf Hitler** — Demanded annexation of Sudetenland as condition for "no more territorial demands in Europe"
- **Neville Chamberlain** — Made three flights to Germany to negotiate; accepted German demands
- **Édouard Daladier** — French Premier; co-signed Munich Agreement
- **Edvard Beneš** — Czech President; not invited to Munich conference

### Key Numbers
- **~3,000,000** — German-speaking population in Sudetenland (Britannica)
- **11,000 square miles** — Territory Czechoslovakia lost (Britannica)
- **3+ million** — People lost to Czechoslovakia under the agreement
- **October 10, 1938** — Deadline for German military occupation of Sudetenland

### Verified Facts for Quiz Questions
1. **Czechoslovakia was not consulted** at the Munich conference — Czech diplomats waited in a hotel while four powers decided their country's fate (Britannica)
2. The Sudetenland contained approximately **3 million ethnic Germans** out of Czechoslovakia's total population
3. Henlein's Sudeten German Party had won nearly **two-thirds** of the Sudeten German vote in 1935 elections — political groundwork preceded the 1938 military crisis

### Commonly Confused / Surprising
- The Sudetenland was not one contiguous region — it consisted of sections of northern and western Bohemia and northern Moravia near the Sudeten mountain ranges
- Czechoslovakia was the most democratic and industrially advanced state in Central Europe — it had a genuine military capable of resistance, but was not permitted to fight
- France had a mutual defense treaty with Czechoslovakia but did not honor it — France and Britain together pressured Prague to submit
- Britain and France gave Czechoslovakia an ultimatum: resist alone or submit. They chose to submit.

## Topic 4: Munich Agreement (September 30, 1938)

**Wikipedia URL:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Munich_Agreement *(403 blocked)*
**Primary sources consulted:**
- https://www.britannica.com/event/Munich-Agreement
- https://www.history.com/this-day-in-history/september-30/munich-pact-signed
- https://encyclopedia.ushmm.org/content/en/timeline-event/holocaust/1933-1938/munich-agreement
- https://encyclopedia.ushmm.org/content/en/timeline-event/holocaust/1933-1938/chamberlains-peace-for-our-time-speech

### Exact Dates
- **September 29–30, 1938**: Munich conference and signing
- **September 30, 1938**: Chamberlain delivers "peace for our time" speech at 10 Downing Street
- **October 10, 1938**: Deadline for German military occupation of Sudetenland

### Four Signatories
1. **Adolf Hitler** (Germany)
2. **Neville Chamberlain** (Great Britain)
3. **Édouard Daladier** (France)
4. **Benito Mussolini** (Italy)

*Note: Czechoslovakia was NOT a signatory — Czech diplomats were excluded from the conference room (Britannica)*

### The "Peace for Our Time" Quote
**Exact wording** (History.com / Britannica): *"My good friends, this is the second time in our history that there has come back from Germany to Downing Street peace with honor. I believe it is peace for our time."*

- Delivered: **September 30, 1938**, from a **second-floor window at 10 Downing Street**
- The phrase alludes to Benjamin Disraeli's 1878 declaration after the Congress of Berlin
- Common misquote: "peace in our time" — the actual phrase is **"peace FOR our time"**

### Territory Transferred
- **11,000 square miles** of Sudetenland territory
- More than **3 million** people transferred from Czechoslovakia to Germany
- Czechoslovakia also had to surrender border fortifications (its primary defensive line)

### What Was NOT in the Agreement / Surprising Facts
- The "Italian plan" presented at the conference had actually been drafted by the German Foreign Office (Britannica) — not genuinely Italian
- Czechoslovakia was "informed by Britain and France that it could either resist Germany alone or submit" — there was no real choice offered
- No Czech representative was admitted to the conference room

### Verified Facts for Quiz Questions
1. The Munich Agreement was signed by **four** powers: Germany, Britain, France, and Italy — Czechoslovakia was excluded from negotiations
2. Chamberlain's exact phrase was **"peace for our time"**, not the frequently misquoted "peace in our time"
3. Czechoslovakia lost **11,000 square miles** and over 3 million people, plus its border fortifications

### Commonly Confused / Surprising
- Common misquote: "peace IN our time" (correct: "peace FOR our time")
- Munich is often cited as a two-person deal between Hitler and Chamberlain — it was actually a four-power conference
- The Czech government was not at the table — it was presented with a fait accompli
- Hitler reportedly told aides afterward that he had been "cheated" out of the military victory he wanted; he wanted war, not diplomatic concession

## Topic 5: Czechoslovakia Dismemberment (March 1939)

**Wikipedia URL:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_occupation_of_Czechoslovakia *(403 blocked)*
**Primary sources consulted:**
- https://www.history.com/this-day-in-history/march-15/nazis-take-czechoslovakia
- https://www.britannica.com/place/Czechoslovakia
- https://www.britannica.com/topic/20th-century-international-relations-2085155/The-taking-of-Czechoslovakia

### Exact Dates
- **March 13, 1939**: Slovak leader Monsignor Jozef Tiso taken to Berlin; Hitler demands Slovak independence declaration
- **March 14, 1939**: Slovakia declares "independence" under German pressure
- **March 14–15, 1939 (night)**: Czech President Emil Hacha summoned to Berlin; Hitler threatens bombing of Prague
- **March 15, 1939**: German troops pour into Bohemia and Moravia; Hitler enters Prague in triumph by evening

### Key People
- **Adolf Hitler** — Orchestrated the dismemberment
- **Emil Hacha** — Czech President; coerced into signing away his country; reportedly suffered a heart attack during the Berlin meeting and had to be revived
- **Jozef Tiso** — Slovak leader; declared Slovak "independence" under German coercion; became puppet leader
- **Neville Chamberlain** — Definitively repudiated appeasement after this event

### What Happened to Czech Territory
- **Bohemia and Moravia** → Proclaimed "Protectorate of Bohemia and Moravia" (direct German rule)
- **Slovakia** → Nominally independent state under Tiso, dominated by Germany
- **Sub-Carpathian Ruthenia** → Annexed by Hungary
- Austria (already annexed March 1938)
- Sudetenland (taken September–October 1938)

### Significance for Appeasement
- This was the decisive moment appeasement ended — Hitler had promised at Munich that the Sudetenland was his "last territorial demand in Europe"
- Occupation of ethnic-Czech territory (not German-populated) proved Hitler's expansionism was not about protecting Germans abroad
- Chamberlain "definitively repudiated appeasement" and issued guarantees to Poland, Romania, and Greece within weeks (Britannica)

### Verified Facts for Quiz Questions
1. German troops entered Prague on **March 15, 1939** — less than six months after the Munich Agreement
2. Czech President **Emil Hacha** reportedly suffered a heart attack during the Berlin meeting where Hitler threatened to bomb Prague
3. Czechoslovakia was divided: Bohemia and Moravia became a **German Protectorate**; Slovakia became a nominally independent German puppet state

### Commonly Confused / Surprising
- People often forget Czechoslovakia was broken apart in stages: Sudetenland (Oct 1938) → Slovakia separated + Bohemia/Moravia occupied (March 1939)
- Hacha technically "invited" German troops in writing — Hitler used this as legal cover; it was obtained under extreme duress
- The Munich Agreement had contained a guarantee of the remaining Czech state's borders from Britain and France — this was never honored

## Topic 6: Mussolini's March on Rome (October 1922)

**Wikipedia URL:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/March_on_Rome *(403 blocked)*
**Primary sources consulted:**
- https://www.britannica.com/event/March-on-Rome
- https://www.history.com/articles/mussolini-italy-fascism
- https://www.history.com/news/mussolini-italy-fascism

### Exact Dates
- **October 24, 1922**: Mussolini addresses 40,000 Fascists in Naples; threatens to "march on Rome"
- **October 27–28, 1922**: Fascist columns converge on Rome (the actual march)
- **October 29, 1922**: King Victor Emmanuel III asks Mussolini to form a government
- **October 30, 1922**: Mussolini arrives in Rome by train; fascist forces enter Rome

### Key People
- **Benito Mussolini** — Fascist leader ("Il Duce"); became Prime Minister
- **King Victor Emmanuel III** — Italian king who refused to declare martial law and instead invited Mussolini to govern
- **General Armando Diaz** — Army commander who reportedly told the king the army could not be relied upon to fire on the Fascists (UNVERIFIED — needs additional source)

### Key Numbers
- **40,000**: Fascists gathered in Naples on October 24 (Britannica)
- **~50,000**: Estimated fascist marchers who converged on Rome (search results)
- Mussolini claimed **300,000** — widely considered propaganda (Britannica)

### The Myth vs. Reality
Per Britannica: *"The March on Rome was not the conquest of power that Mussolini later called it but rather a transfer of power within the framework of the constitution, a transfer made possible by the surrender of public authorities in the face of fascist intimidation."*

Key facts:
- The king **refused to sign the state of siege** that would have authorized the army to disperse the marchers
- The army could almost certainly have defeated the lightly-armed fascist columns
- Mussolini himself was **not in Rome during the march** — he was in Milan, ready to flee to Switzerland if it failed
- He arrived in Rome on **October 30 by train**, after the king had already invited him to form a government

### Verified Facts for Quiz Questions
1. Mussolini came to power on **October 29, 1922**, when King Victor Emmanuel III asked him to form a government — he did not seize power by force
2. Mussolini was **not present** during the actual March on Rome — he was in Milan and traveled to Rome by train only after the king's invitation
3. The King's refusal to declare martial law was decisive — the army was capable of stopping the march but was never ordered to do so

### Commonly Confused / Surprising
- The March on Rome is one of history's great myths — Mussolini's Blackshirts didn't "seize" anything; the government handed power over
- Mussolini was appointed Prime Minister through legitimate constitutional procedure — he later dismantled democracy, but his rise to power was technically legal
- The Fascist forces were poorly armed and could likely have been crushed by regular Italian military — the king chose not to resist

## Topic 7: Italy Invades Ethiopia/Abyssinia (1935–36)

**Wikipedia URL:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Italo-Ethiopian_War *(403 blocked)*
**Primary sources consulted:**
- https://www.britannica.com/event/Italo-Ethiopian-War-1935-1936

### Exact Dates
- **December 1934**: Walwal (Wal Wal) border incident between Ethiopia and Italian Somaliland — Mussolini uses as pretext
- **October 3, 1935**: Italy invades Ethiopia
- **October 7, 1935**: League of Nations declares Italy the aggressor
- **April 9, 1936**: Major Italian victory near Lake Ascianghi
- **May 5, 1936**: Italian forces take Addis Ababa, Ethiopian capital
- **May 9, 1936**: Mussolini formally declares Ethiopia part of Italian Empire

### Key People
- **Benito Mussolini** — Ordered the invasion
- **Haile Selassie** — Emperor of Ethiopia; went into exile after fall of Addis Ababa; gave famous League of Nations speech (June 30, 1936)
- **Marshal Pietro Badoglio** — Italian commander; led final advance
- **General Rodolfo Graziani** — Italian commander; notorious for use of chemical weapons against civilians

### Key Numbers
- **~400,000** Italian troops deployed (Britannica)
- Ethiopian casualties: UNVERIFIED from retrieved sources (historical estimates range from 275,000–760,000 dead — UNVERIFIED)
- League of Nations voted economic sanctions but exempted oil (which would have been decisive)

### Chemical Weapons
- Italy used **mustard gas** (yperite) extensively, including against Red Cross hospitals and medical personnel
- Chemical weapons were "employed by Italian forces in Ethiopia (1935–40)" (Britannica)
- This was a major international war crime that the League failed to punish effectively

### League of Nations Response
- League Council declared Italy the **aggressor on October 7, 1935** — within days of the invasion
- Voted economic sanctions but excluded: **oil** (would have grounded the Italian military), **steel**, **coal**
- Britain and France refused to close the **Suez Canal** to Italian shipping (would have been decisive)
- **Hoare-Laval Pact** (December 1935): Secret Anglo-French plan to give Mussolini most of Ethiopia — leaked, caused public outrage, plan abandoned (UNVERIFIED from retrieved sources — needs verification)
- Sanctions lifted in **July 1936** after Ethiopia was conquered

### Verified Facts for Quiz Questions
1. Italy invaded Ethiopia on **October 3, 1935**, using approximately **400,000 troops**
2. Italy used **mustard gas** against Ethiopian forces and civilians — a major war crime that the League of Nations failed to punish
3. The League of Nations declared Italy an aggressor but its sanctions were deliberately weak — the exemption of **oil** from sanctions was the critical failure

### Commonly Confused / Surprising
- This is sometimes confused with the First Italo-Ethiopian War (1895–96) where Ethiopia humiliatingly defeated Italy at Battle of Adwa — Italy's 1935 invasion was partly driven by desire for revenge
- Emperor Haile Selassie gave a personal address to the League of Nations in Geneva (June 1936) that became one of the most famous speeches of the 20th century — the League took no effective action
- The war is seen as a turning point where Mussolini drifted toward Hitler; Italy had previously opposed German expansion (protected Austria in 1934) but League sanctions pushed Italy toward Germany

## Topic 8: Rome-Berlin Axis (October 1936)

**Wikipedia URL:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rome%E2%80%93Berlin_Axis *(403 blocked)*
**Primary sources consulted:**
- https://www.britannica.com/topic/Rome-Berlin-Axis
- https://www.britannica.com/topic/Axis-Powers

### Exact Dates
- **October 24, 1936**: Galeazzo Ciano visits Hitler at Berchtesgaden; informal agreement reached; Germany becomes first power to recognize Italy's annexation of Ethiopia
- **October 25, 1936**: The Rome-Berlin Axis protocol signed (Britannica)
- **November 1, 1936**: Mussolini publicly proclaims the "Rome-Berlin Axis" in a speech in Milan
- **November 25, 1936**: Anti-Comintern Pact — Germany and Japan sign; Italy joins in 1937
- **May 22, 1939**: Pact of Steel signed — Germany and Italy formalize into a full military alliance

### Key People
- **Benito Mussolini** — Italian dictator; coined the term "Axis"
- **Adolf Hitler** — German Führer
- **Galeazzo Ciano** — Mussolini's son-in-law and Foreign Minister; negotiated the agreement at Berchtesgaden

### Nature of the Agreement
- The October 1936 agreement was an **informal political understanding**, NOT a military alliance
- Germany recognized Italy's conquest of Ethiopia — this was the concrete concession
- It was not a binding military commitment — that came later with the Pact of Steel (1939)
- Mussolini used the word "Axis" metaphorically: that Europe would rotate around an "axis" running between Rome and Berlin

### Key Terminology
- **Rome-Berlin Axis** (1936): Political/diplomatic alignment, informal
- **Anti-Comintern Pact** (November 25, 1936, Germany + Japan; Italy joined 1937): Anti-Soviet ideological pact
- **Pact of Steel** (May 22, 1939): Full military alliance between Germany and Italy
- **Tripartite Pact** (September 27, 1940): Germany, Italy, Japan — formal military alliance

### Verified Facts for Quiz Questions
1. The Rome-Berlin Axis was proclaimed by Mussolini on **November 1, 1936**, though the protocol was signed **October 25, 1936**
2. The agreement was negotiated by **Galeazzo Ciano**, Mussolini's son-in-law and Foreign Minister
3. The 1936 Axis was a **political alignment, not a military alliance** — full military commitment came only with the **Pact of Steel in May 1939**

### Commonly Confused / Surprising
- "Axis powers" in WWII refers to the full Tripartite Pact (1940), not just the Rome-Berlin bilateral of 1936
- The 1936 agreement was not a military commitment — Italy was not obligated to fight alongside Germany until 1939
- Germany was the first major power to recognize Italy's annexation of Ethiopia — this was the price Mussolini got for the Axis agreement
- Mussolini had previously opposed Hitler's expansion (protected Austria 1934); the Ethiopia sanctions flipped him toward Germany

## Topic 9: Spanish Civil War (1936–39)

**Wikipedia URL:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spanish_Civil_War *(403 blocked)*
**Primary sources consulted:**
- https://www.britannica.com/event/Spanish-Civil-War

- https://www.britannica.com/topic/International-Brigades
- https://www.history.com/this-day-in-history/spanish-civil-war-breaks-out

### Exact Dates
- **July 17, 1936**: Military uprising begins in Spanish Morocco (start of war)
- **July 18, 1936**: Uprising spreads to mainland Spain
- **October 1, 1936**: Franco named head of state of Nationalist Spain
- **April 26, 1937**: **Guernica bombed** by German Condor Legion (4:30 PM, during market day)
- **Late 1938**: International Brigades formally withdrawn; farewell parade in Barcelona November 15, 1938
- **March 28, 1939**: Nationalist forces enter Madrid — war ends
- **April 1, 1939**: Franco declares victory

### Key People
- **Francisco Franco** — Nationalist general; became head of state October 1, 1936; ruled Spain as dictator until 1975
- **Manuel Azaña** — President of the Spanish Republic
- **Adolf Hitler** — Sent Condor Legion and military aid to Franco
- **Benito Mussolini** — Sent ~70,000 Italian troops ("CTV" — Corpo Truppe Volontarie) to Franco
- **Joseph Stalin** — Provided equipment and advisors to the Republic
- **Pablo Picasso** — Painted *Guernica* (1937) as response to the bombing

### Guernica Bombing (April 26, 1937)
- **Date**: April 26, 1937
- **Time**: 4:30 PM — during the busiest hour of the market day
- **Duration**: ~3 hours of continuous bombing
- **Perpetrators**: German **Condor Legion** (Luftwaffe unit testing tactics in Spain)
- **Authorization**: Approved by General Franco
- **Casualties**: "One-third of Guernica's 5,000 inhabitants were killed or wounded" (History.com); more than 1,000 residents killed
- **Military significance**: Pioneered carpet-bombing and terror-bombing tactics; "by 1942, all major participants in WWII had adopted the bombing innovations developed at Guernica" (History.com)
- **Picasso's painting**: Completed 1937; became one of history's most recognized anti-war artworks; returned to Spain in **September 1981** (previously held at MOMA, New York)

### International Brigades
- **Total volunteers**: ~60,000 over the war's duration (Britannica); never more than 20,000 at one time
- **Active fighters**: ~35,000–40,000 (varying sources; Britannica gives 40,000 who "fought on the Republican side")
- **Countries**: Approximately 50 nations represented
- **Largest contingent**: France — approximately 28,000 volunteers
- **American volunteers**: ~2,800 total; ~900 killed (Abraham Lincoln Battalion)
- **Casualties**: ~5,000–6,000 killed, thousands more missing (search results; slightly different figure from Britannica's 40,000 active fighters)
- **Organization**: Organized by the Communist International (Comintern); many volunteers were Communists or became so
- **Disbanded**: Formally withdrawn late 1938; farewell parade Barcelona, November 15, 1938

### Foreign Military Involvement
| Power | Side | Contribution |
|-------|------|-------------|
| Germany | Nationalist | Condor Legion (~5,000–6,000 personnel); tested Luftwaffe tactics |
| Italy | Nationalist | ~70,000 troops (CTV); aircraft, tanks |
| USSR | Republican | Equipment, advisors, some personnel; political commissars |
| International Brigades | Republican | ~60,000 volunteers from 50 countries |
| Non-Intervention Committee | — | UK/France nominally neutral; effectively blocked Republic from buying weapons |

### Total Casualties
- Britannica: estimates vary widely — Nationalist sources claimed 1,000,000 deaths; modern scholarship estimates ~500,000 or less
- Includes combat deaths, executions, disease, malnutrition

### Verified Facts for Quiz Questions
1. Guernica was bombed on **April 26, 1937** by the **German Condor Legion** — the attack began at **4:30 PM** during market day and lasted ~3 hours
2. The International Brigades drew approximately **60,000 volunteers** total from about **50 countries** — France sent the most (~28,000)
3. Franco was named Nationalist head of state on **October 1, 1936** — less than 3 months after the uprising began

### Commonly Confused / Surprising
- "Guernica" (the Picasso painting) is often more famous than the actual event — the painting was not displayed in Spain until 1981
- The International Brigades were mostly Communists organized by the Comintern — often romanticized as purely idealistic volunteers
- The Non-Intervention Committee (UK, France, Germany, Italy) was supposed to prevent foreign involvement — Germany and Italy joined while continuing to send forces; it was a diplomatic fiction
- Franco survived in power until **1975** — long after his WWII-era allies were defeated

## Topic 10: Kristallnacht (November 9–10, 1938)

**Wikipedia URL:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kristallnacht *(403 blocked)*
**Primary sources consulted:**
- https://encyclopedia.ushmm.org/content/en/article/the-night-of-broken-glass
- https://www.history.com/this-day-in-history/november-9/nazis-launch-kristallnacht
- https://www.britannica.com/event/Kristallnacht

### Exact Dates
- **November 7, 1938**: Herschel Grynszpan shoots Ernst vom Rath in Paris
- **November 9, 1938**: Ernst vom Rath dies from his wounds
- **Night of November 9–10, 1938**: Kristallnacht pogrom (the "Night of Broken Glass")

### The Trigger
- **Herschel Grynszpan** — 17-year-old Polish-born Jewish man living in Paris; his parents had been among 12,000 Polish Jews deported from Germany to the Polish border in late October 1938
- Grynszpan walked into the German Embassy in Paris on November 7 and shot **Ernst vom Rath**, a German diplomat
- vom Rath died on November 9 — his death was the pretext used by Nazi leadership to launch the pogrom
- **Joseph Goebbels** (Nazi Propaganda Minister) delivered a speech to Nazi leaders that evening, signaling them to allow or encourage "spontaneous demonstrations"

### Who Ordered It
- The signal was given by **Joseph Goebbels** at an annual Nazi Party gathering in Munich (USHMM)
- Carried out by **SS**, **SA** (Brownshirts), and **Hitler Youth**
- Local police and fire departments were **ordered not to intervene** or protect Jewish property
- Presented publicly as "spontaneous popular outrage" — actually state-organized

### Key Numbers
- **Synagogues**: More than **1,400** burned (USHMM); search results also cite 267 (lower figure — may refer to completely destroyed vs. damaged — UNVERIFIED which is more accurate)
- **Jewish businesses vandalized**: ~**7,500** (History.com); "thousands" (USHMM)
- **Jewish men arrested**: ~**30,000** (USHMM and History.com)
- **Jews killed**: At least **91** during the riots (History.com); "hundreds" during riots and aftermath (USHMM)
- **Concentration camp destinations**: Dachau, Buchenwald, Sachsenhausen
- **Collective fine**: **1 billion Reichsmarks** (~$400 million in 1938 dollars) imposed on the Jewish community for "provoking" the violence

### Name Meaning
*Kristallnacht* = "Crystal Night" or **"Night of Broken Glass"** — refers to the shattered glass from Jewish shop windows that littered German streets

### What Happened to Arrested Men
- Sent to concentration camps (Dachau, Buchenwald, Sachsenhausen)
- Many released after weeks or months, on condition they agree to emigrate from Germany
- Served as a mechanism to accelerate Jewish emigration

### Verified Facts for Quiz Questions
1. The trigger for Kristallnacht was **Herschel Grynszpan**'s shooting of German diplomat **Ernst vom Rath** in Paris on November 7, 1938
2. Approximately **30,000 Jewish men** were arrested and sent to concentration camps; over **1,400 synagogues** were burned
3. The Nazi government fined the Jewish community **1 billion Reichsmarks** (~$400 million) for "causing" the pogrom

### Commonly Confused / Surprising
- Despite appearing spontaneous, Kristallnacht was **state-organized** — Goebbels gave the signal to party leaders; police were ordered to stand aside
- The fine was imposed on the **victims** — the Jewish community was collectively charged for the damage done to their own property
- "Kristallnacht" is a Nazi-coined euphemism — many historians and Jewish organizations prefer "November Pogrom" or "Reichspogromnacht"
- Grynszpan's parents had been forcibly expelled from Germany to Poland in October 1938 — his shooting was an act of desperation, not a coordinated attack

## Topic 11: Polish-British Mutual Defense Pact (March 1939)

**Wikipedia URL:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anglo-Polish_mutual_assistance *(403 blocked)*
**Primary sources consulted:**
- https://www.britannica.com/event/Anglo-Polish-Pact-of-Mutual-Assistance-1939
- https://www.britannica.com/biography/Neville-Chamberlain
- Search results from Britannica

### Exact Dates
- **March 15, 1939**: Germany occupies Bohemia and Moravia (the trigger)
- **March 31, 1939**: Chamberlain gives **unilateral guarantee** to Poland in the House of Commons — Britain will defend Poland's independence
- **April 6, 1939**: Mutual guarantee extended (Poland reciprocally commits to Britain)
- **August 23, 1939**: Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact signed — Nazi-Soviet non-aggression pact
- **August 25, 1939**: **Formal Anglo-Polish Treaty of Mutual Assistance** signed (the full binding treaty)
- **September 1, 1939**: Germany invades Poland
- **September 3, 1939**: Britain (and France) declare war on Germany

### Key People
- **Neville Chamberlain** — Delivered the March 31 guarantee in Parliament; signed the August treaty
- **Józef Beck** — Polish Foreign Minister who accepted the guarantee and rejected German demands
- **Lord Halifax** — British Foreign Secretary who negotiated the formal treaty

### What It Committed Britain To
- **March 31 guarantee**: Britain would give Poland "all support in its power" if Poland's independence were "clearly threatened" — this was a political promise, not yet a binding treaty
- **August 25 formal treaty**: Full binding mutual defense — each country would come to the other's aid if attacked by a European power (i.e., Germany)

### Two-Stage Process — Commonly Confused
1. **March 31, 1939** — Unilateral British guarantee (political declaration by Chamberlain)
2. **August 25, 1939** — Formal bilateral treaty (legally binding, signed by both sides)

The formal treaty came just **7 days before Germany invaded Poland** and **2 days after the Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact** — Hitler attempted to use the Nazi-Soviet deal to intimidate Britain into abandoning Poland.

### Verified Facts for Quiz Questions
1. Britain's guarantee to Poland was announced on **March 31, 1939** — 16 days after the German occupation of Czechoslovakia
2. The formal **Anglo-Polish Treaty of Mutual Assistance** was signed on **August 25, 1939** — one week before Germany invaded Poland
3. The guarantee was triggered by the dismemberment of Czechoslovakia — it marked the definitive end of appeasement policy

### Commonly Confused / Surprising
- Many people conflate the **March 31 guarantee** (political declaration) with the **August 25 treaty** (formal binding alliance) — they were separate acts
- France gave a parallel guarantee to Poland at the same time as Britain
- The treaty did not prevent Poland from being conquered — Britain and France declared war but could not reach Poland in time to help militarily
- Britain was bound by treaty to go to war — this is why WWII began on September 3, 1939 (not when Germany invaded Poland on September 1)

## Topic 12: Danzig Crisis and Polish Corridor

**Wikipedia URL:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Danzig_crisis *(403 blocked)*
**Primary sources consulted:**
- https://www.britannica.com/place/Polish-Corridor
- https://www.britannica.com/place/Gdansk
- Search results from Britannica

### Exact Dates
- **1919**: Polish Corridor and Free City of Danzig created by Treaty of Versailles
- **1933 and 1935**: Nazi Party wins majority of Danzig Free City assembly seats
- **October 1938**: After Munich, Germany begins pressing for Danzig and Corridor concessions
- **March 1939**: Hitler formally demands cession of Danzig and extraterritorial highway across Corridor
- **March 31, 1939**: Britain and France guarantee Polish independence
- **September 1, 1939**: Germany invades Poland, beginning with attack on Danzig and Corridor

### Key People
- **Adolf Hitler** — Made demands for Danzig in early 1939
- **Józef Beck** — Polish Foreign Minister who rejected German demands
- **Albert Forster** — Nazi Gauleiter of Danzig (UNVERIFIED from retrieved sources)

### The Polish Corridor — Geography
- Strip of land **20 to 70 miles (32–112 km) wide**
- Ran along the lower Vistula River to the Baltic Sea
- Separated **East Prussia** from the rest of Germany
- Created from former German territory (West Prussia, parts of Poznań province)
- Gave Poland its only access to the Baltic Sea — considered vital for Polish economic survival
- Poland built **Gdynia** as a new port rather than relying on Danzig

### Danzig (Gdańsk) — Status
- **Free City of Danzig** — League of Nations protectorate, 1919–1939
- Not part of Poland, not part of Germany — a separate legal entity
- Population: UNVERIFIED from retrieved sources (historical figure ~400,000, predominantly German — UNVERIFIED)
- By **1933 and 1935** elections, Nazi Party held majorities in the Free City's assembly
- Poland had special rights: customs union, postal services, foreign relations management

### Germany's Specific 1939 Demands (March 1939)
Per Britannica:
1. Cession of **Danzig** to Germany
2. Creation of **extraterritorial German highways** across the Polish Corridor to East Prussia

### Poland's Response
- Flat **rejection** of German demands
- Poland accepted British and French guarantees (March 31, 1939)
- Considered corridor and Danzig non-negotiable — loss would leave Poland economically strangled and strategically vulnerable

### Verified Facts for Quiz Questions
1. Germany's 1939 demands were specifically: annexation of **Danzig** and creation of **extraterritorial highways** across the Polish Corridor to East Prussia
2. The Polish Corridor was created by the **Treaty of Versailles** (1919) and measured **20 to 70 miles wide** — it separated East Prussia from the rest of Germany
3. Danzig had been a **League of Nations Free City** since 1919 — it was neither German nor Polish sovereign territory when Hitler demanded it

### Commonly Confused / Surprising
- Danzig was **not part of Poland** — it was a League of Nations Free City; Poland had special rights but not sovereignty
- The Corridor was German territory before WWI — Germany's grievance was that ethnic Germans had been separated from Germany by Versailles
- Despite the corridor being real territory with real Germans, Hitler's goals went far beyond Danzig — the Corridor was a pretext for the conquest of all of Poland
- Poland had refused similar German demands in October 1938 (post-Munich) — the crisis had been building for months before March 1939
