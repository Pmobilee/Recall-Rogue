# Overarching Framework

## 8 Cross-Cutting Themes

These thematic learning objectives run across all 9 periods and are woven into every unit.

1. **NAT — American and National Identity**: How and why definitions of American and national identity have developed and changed; how distinctions have been drawn between different groups of Americans.
2. **WXT — Work, Exchange, and Technology**: How different labor systems, forms of exchange, and technological developments have affected U.S. economic life; how those systems interacted with political, cultural, and social developments.
3. **GEO — Geography and the Environment**: How geographic and environmental factors have shaped human societies and processes; how humans have altered the environment.
4. **MIG — Migration and Settlement**: Why and how people have moved within and to/from the U.S.; how migration has affected U.S. society.
5. **PCE — Politics and Power**: How different groups have competed for power, how governing institutions have evolved, and how political ideas and party systems have changed over time.
6. **WOR — America in the World**: How the U.S. has interacted with other nations and peoples; how foreign policy, trade, and migration have shaped American society.
7. **ARC — American and Regional Culture**: How cultural values, artistic expressions, and intellectual movements have developed in the U.S.; how regional and national cultures have coexisted and interacted.
8. **SOC — Social Structures**: How social categories (race, class, gender, ethnicity, age, sexuality, religion) have developed and changed; how social structures have shaped individual opportunity and group experience.

---

## 6 Historical Thinking Skills

1. **Developments and Processes** — Identify and explain historical developments and processes.
2. **Sourcing and Situation** — Analyze the context in which a historical source was created, including author, audience, purpose, and point of view.
3. **Claims and Evidence in Sources** — Analyze arguments made in historical sources; evaluate the credibility and limitations of sources.
4. **Contextualization** — Explain the broader historical context of a specific development, event, or process.
5. **Making Connections** — Identify and explain patterns across time periods, geographic areas, or groups (comparison); identify and explain causes and effects (causation); identify continuities and changes across time periods (continuity and change over time).
6. **Argumentation** — Develop a claim and support it with evidence from multiple sources; develop a historically defensible thesis.

---

## 3 Reasoning Processes

1. **Comparison** — Describe, compare, and evaluate multiple historical developments within one time period or across multiple time periods; compare different perspectives; compare the historical significance of developments.
2. **Causation** — Describe, explain, and evaluate the causes and effects of historical events and developments; distinguish between immediate and long-term causes and effects.
3. **Continuity and Change Over Time (CCOT)** — Describe, explain, and evaluate historical patterns of continuity and change over time; connect these patterns to larger historical processes or themes.

# UNIT 1: Period 1 — 1491–1607 (4–6% exam weight, ~8 class periods)

Themes: NAT, WXT, GEO, MIG, PCE, WOR, ARC, SOC
Reasoning Process focus: Causation

### Topic 1.1: Contextualizing Period 1
- **Skill focus**: Contextualization — frame the major developments of 1491–1607 within broader world historical context.
- **Content**: Pre-Columbian Americas as part of a world of interconnected societies; Afro-Eurasian trade networks; the demographic and political landscape of the Americas on the eve of European contact.
- **Key terms**: pre-Columbian, Afro-Eurasian trade networks, 1491, contextualization
- **Note**: This is a skill-focused framing topic, not a standalone content topic.

### Topic 1.2: Native American Societies Before European Contact
- **Thematic focus**: GEO, WXT, ARC, SOC
- **Content**:
  - Diverse Native societies adapted to varied geographic environments (Great Plains, Eastern Woodlands, Pacific Northwest, Southwest, Southeast).
  - Complex trade networks, political systems, and cultural practices existed across the Americas.
  - Agricultural societies (e.g., Mississippian, Pueblo/Ancestral Puebloans) and hunter-gatherer societies both flourished.
  - Mesoamerican civilizations (Aztec/Mexica, Maya) and Andean civilizations (Inca) had sophisticated urban centers, agriculture, and governance.
- **Illustrative examples**: Cahokia, Iroquois Confederacy (Haudenosaunee), Pueblo peoples, Aztec (Mexica) at Tenochtitlan, Cherokee, Algonquian-speaking peoples
- **Key terms**: Mississippian culture, Ancestral Puebloans, Iroquois Confederacy, matrilineal, kinship networks, nomadic, sedentary agriculture, maize cultivation, Three Sisters agriculture
- **Source reference**: CED Period 1 Key Concept 1.1

### Topic 1.3: European Exploration in the Americas
- **Thematic focus**: WOR, WXT, MIG, PCE
- **Content**:
  - Portuguese and Spanish exploration driven by desire for trade routes, wealth, and missionary activity.
  - Christopher Columbus's 1492 voyage initiated sustained contact between Europe and the Americas.
  - Treaty of Tordesillas (1494) divided the non-European world between Spain and Portugal.
  - Other European powers (England, France, Netherlands) soon challenged Iberian dominance.
  - Motivations: "God, Gold, and Glory" — religious conversion, economic gain, political prestige.
- **Illustrative examples**: Christopher Columbus (1492), Vasco da Gama, Ferdinand Magellan, Amerigo Vespucci, John Cabot, Jacques Cartier
- **Key terms**: reconquista, Treaty of Tordesillas, Columbian Exchange (introduced), encomienda, conquistador, New World, caravel, compass, astrolabe, mercantilism
- **Source reference**: CED Period 1 Key Concept 1.2

### Topic 1.4: Columbian Exchange, Spanish Exploration, and Conquest
- **Thematic focus**: GEO, WXT, SOC, MIG
- **Content**:
  - The Columbian Exchange transferred plants, animals, diseases, and people between the Eastern and Western hemispheres.
  - European diseases (smallpox, measles) devastated Native populations — estimates suggest up to 90% population decline in some regions.
  - Introduction of horses transformed Plains Indian cultures.
  - New World crops (maize, potatoes, tomatoes, tobacco) transformed European diets and economies.
  - Spanish conquistadors defeated the Aztec (1519–1521) and Inca (1532–1533) empires.
- **Illustrative examples**: Hernán Cortés and the defeat of the Aztec empire (1521), Francisco Pizarro and the Inca (1532), Smallpox epidemic among the Aztec, Introduction of horses to the Great Plains
- **Key terms**: Columbian Exchange, smallpox, demographic collapse, maize, potato, tobacco, conquistador, Hernán Cortés, Moctezuma II, Tenochtitlan, mestizo
- **Source reference**: CED Period 1 Key Concept 1.2

### Topic 1.5: Labor, Slavery, and Caste in the Spanish Colonial System
- **Thematic focus**: WXT, SOC, PCE
- **Content**:
  - Spain established a hierarchical caste system (sistema de castas) in its colonial territories.
  - Encomienda system granted conquistadors labor from Native populations; abuses led to reform debates.
  - Bartolomé de las Casas advocated for Native rights and against encomienda abuses.
  - As Native labor declined due to disease, Spanish colonizers imported enslaved Africans.
  - The transatlantic slave trade began with Spanish and Portuguese colonies in the Americas.
- **Illustrative examples**: Encomienda system, Bartolomé de las Casas's "A Short Account of the Destruction of the Indies," African slavery in Spanish Caribbean, Potosí silver mines (mita labor system)
- **Key terms**: encomienda, mita, sistema de castas, peninsulares, criollos, mestizos, mulatos, Bartolomé de las Casas, transatlantic slave trade, indentured servitude (contrast)
- **Source reference**: CED Period 1 Key Concept 1.2

### Topic 1.6: Cultural Interactions Between Europeans, Native Americans, and Africans
- **Thematic focus**: NAT, SOC, ARC, WOR
- **Content**:
  - Contact between Europeans, Native Americans, and Africans produced both cooperation and conflict.
  - Some Native groups allied with Europeans against rival Native nations.
  - Missionaries sought to convert Native peoples; some Native groups accepted, others resisted.
  - Africans brought distinct cultural practices, knowledge systems, and resistance to some diseases.
  - New syncretic cultures emerged in colonial settings.
- **Illustrative examples**: Franciscan missions in California and New Mexico, Pueblo Revolt of 1680, Malinche (La Malintzin) as cultural intermediary, African knowledge of rice cultivation in Carolina lowcountry
- **Key terms**: syncretism, cultural exchange, missionaries, Franciscans, Jesuits, Pueblo Revolt, resistance, intermediaries, gender roles in Native societies
- **Source reference**: CED Period 1 Key Concept 1.3

### Topic 1.7: Causation in Period 1
- **Skill focus**: Causation — evaluate the causes and effects of European exploration and contact with the Americas.
- **Content**: Practice reasoning about short-term vs. long-term causes; distinguishing intended from unintended consequences; assessing the relative importance of multiple causes.
- **Note**: Skill-practice topic. No new content; applies causation to Period 1 events.

# UNIT 2: Period 2 — 1607–1754 (6–8% exam weight, ~14 class periods)

Themes: NAT, WXT, GEO, MIG, PCE, WOR, ARC, SOC
Reasoning Process focus: Comparison

### Topic 2.1: Contextualizing Period 2
- **Skill focus**: Contextualization — frame the settlement and development of British colonies within the broader Atlantic world.
- **Content**: European competition for colonial possessions; Atlantic trade networks; the diversity of colonial experiments from religious to commercial motives.
- **Key terms**: Atlantic world, British colonization, charter colony, proprietary colony, royal colony
- **Note**: Skill-focused framing topic.

### Topic 2.2: European Colonization
- **Thematic focus**: WOR, MIG, PCE, NAT
- **Content**:
  - Spain, France, Netherlands, and England established colonies with different organizational models and goals.
  - Spanish colonies in the Southwest and Southeast (Florida) emphasized extraction and conversion.
  - French colonies in Canada and the Mississippi Valley focused on the fur trade and allied extensively with Native nations.
  - Dutch New Netherland was primarily commercial; became English New York after 1664.
  - English colonies were the most diverse in motivation: religious refuge, commercial profit, proprietary ventures.
- **Illustrative examples**: New France (Quebec 1608), Dutch New Netherland (1624), Spanish St. Augustine (1565), English Jamestown (1607), Plymouth (1620)
- **Key terms**: joint-stock company, Virginia Company, charter, New France, New Netherland, fur trade, mission system, Huguenots, Dutch patroon system
- **Source reference**: CED Period 2 Key Concept 2.1

### Topic 2.3: The Regions of British Colonies
- **Thematic focus**: WXT, GEO, SOC, ARC
- **Content**:
  - New England colonies: Puritan settlement, town-meeting governance, mixed economy of fishing/trade/small farming, covenant communities.
  - Middle colonies: diverse religious and ethnic populations, fertile farming, commercial centers (Philadelphia, New York).
  - Chesapeake colonies (Virginia, Maryland): tobacco monoculture, indentured servants transitioning to enslaved Africans, House of Burgesses.
  - Southern colonies (Carolinas, Georgia): rice and indigo in lowcountry, reliance on enslaved African labor, plantation system.
- **Illustrative examples**: Massachusetts Bay Colony (John Winthrop, "city upon a hill"), Rhode Island (Roger Williams), Pennsylvania (William Penn), Virginia House of Burgesses, South Carolina rice plantations
- **Key terms**: Puritanism, covenant theology, "city upon a hill," headright system, indentured servitude, tobacco monoculture, rice cultivation, town meeting, House of Burgesses, proprietary colony, Quakers
- **Source reference**: CED Period 2 Key Concept 2.1

### Topic 2.4: Transatlantic Trade
- **Thematic focus**: WXT, WOR, MIG
- **Content**:
  - The British mercantilist system sought to regulate colonial trade for the benefit of the mother country.
  - Navigation Acts (1651–1673) required colonial goods to be shipped on English vessels and through English ports.
  - The triangular trade linked Britain, Africa, and the Americas.
  - The Middle Passage: enslaved Africans transported to the Americas under horrific conditions.
  - Colonial economies increasingly integrated into the Atlantic trade system.
- **Illustrative examples**: Navigation Acts, triangular trade routes, Middle Passage, slave trade statistics (millions transported), tobacco/sugar/rice as export commodities
- **Key terms**: mercantilism, Navigation Acts, triangular trade, Middle Passage, transatlantic slave trade, cash crop, plantation economy, enumerated goods, balance of trade
- **Source reference**: CED Period 2 Key Concept 2.1

### Topic 2.5: Interactions Between American Indians and Europeans
- **Thematic focus**: WOR, GEO, PCE, SOC
- **Content**:
  - European expansion led to sustained conflict over land, trade, and sovereignty with Native nations.
  - Some Native nations allied with European powers against rival groups; others formed pan-Indian resistance movements.
  - King Philip's War (1675–1676) in New England was one of the most devastating conflicts per capita in American history.
  - The Pueblo Revolt (1680) temporarily drove Spanish from New Mexico.
  - Disease continued to reduce Native populations throughout the period.
- **Illustrative examples**: King Philip's War / Metacom's War (1675–1676), Pueblo Revolt (1680), Iroquois Confederacy as diplomatic broker, French-Algonquian alliances in the fur trade, Bacon's Rebellion context
- **Key terms**: Metacom (King Philip), Pueblo Revolt, Pope (Popé), frontier, sovereignty, alliance systems, fur trade diplomacy, middle ground
- **Source reference**: CED Period 2 Key Concept 2.1

### Topic 2.6: Slavery in the British Colonies
- **Thematic focus**: WXT, SOC, PCE, NAT
- **Content**:
  - Slavery evolved from a small-scale institution to a foundational economic and social system in the colonial South.
  - Legal codes defined slavery as hereditary and race-based (Virginia slave codes, 1705).
  - Bacon's Rebellion (1676) highlighted tensions between planters and indentured servants; accelerated shift to enslaved labor.
  - Enslaved Africans maintained cultural practices and formed communities; resistance took many forms (work slowdowns, sabotage, running away, armed rebellion).
  - The Stono Rebellion (1739) in South Carolina was one of the largest slave revolts in colonial America.
- **Illustrative examples**: Virginia Slave Codes (1705), Bacon's Rebellion (1676), Stono Rebellion (1739), plantation system in the Chesapeake and Carolinas, Gullah culture in South Carolina
- **Key terms**: chattel slavery, slave codes, hereditary slavery, racialized slavery, Bacon's Rebellion, Stono Rebellion, Middle Passage, slave resistance, Gullah, African cultural retention
- **Source reference**: CED Period 2 Key Concept 2.2

### Topic 2.7: Colonial Society and Culture
- **Thematic focus**: ARC, NAT, SOC, PCE
- **Content**:
  - Colonial society was hierarchical but more fluid than in Europe; land ownership was more widely attainable.
  - The Great Awakening (1730s–1740s) was a religious revival that challenged established churches and promoted individual religious experience.
  - Enlightenment ideas about reason, natural rights, and government began to circulate among colonial elites.
  - A colonial American identity began to emerge, distinct from British identity.
  - Print culture (newspapers, pamphlets) grew; Benjamin Franklin exemplified colonial intellectual life.
- **Illustrative examples**: George Whitefield and the Great Awakening, Jonathan Edwards "Sinners in the Hands of an Angry God," Benjamin Franklin's "Poor Richard's Almanack," John Peter Zenger trial (1735), colonial newspapers
- **Key terms**: Great Awakening, revivalism, New Light vs. Old Light, Enlightenment, natural rights, social mobility, print culture, colonial assemblies, salutary neglect
- **Source reference**: CED Period 2 Key Concept 2.2

### Topic 2.8: Comparison in Period 2
- **Skill focus**: Comparison — compare different colonial regions, European powers, or social groups in the colonial era.
- **Content**: Practice comparing New England vs. Chesapeake vs. Southern colonies; British vs. French vs. Spanish colonial models; free vs. enslaved labor systems.
- **Note**: Skill-practice topic. No new content; applies comparison to Period 2 themes.

# UNIT 3: Period 3 — 1754–1800 (10–17% exam weight, ~17 class periods)

Themes: NAT, WXT, GEO, MIG, PCE, WOR, ARC, SOC
Reasoning Process focus: Continuity and Change Over Time

### Topic 3.1: Contextualizing Period 3
- **Skill focus**: Contextualization — frame the American Revolution and founding era within global Atlantic context.
- **Content**: Seven Years' War as a global conflict; Enlightenment political philosophy; Atlantic revolutions (American, French, Haitian).
- **Key terms**: Seven Years' War, Atlantic revolutions, Age of Enlightenment, natural rights philosophy
- **Note**: Skill-focused framing topic.

### Topic 3.2: The Seven Years' War (The French and Indian War)
- **Thematic focus**: WOR, PCE, GEO, NAT
- **Content**:
  - The Seven Years' War (1756–1763) / French and Indian War in North America reshaped colonial boundaries.
  - Britain defeated France; gained French Canada and territory east of the Mississippi.
  - British victory removed French threat but increased tensions with Native nations and ran up massive debt.
  - Colonists resented Proclamation of 1763 restricting settlement west of the Appalachians.
  - The war gave colonial militias (including George Washington) military experience.
- **Illustrative examples**: Battle of Fort Necessity (Washington's defeat), Braddock's defeat, Wolfe vs. Montcalm at Quebec (1759), Treaty of Paris (1763), Proclamation of 1763, Pontiac's Rebellion (1763)
- **Key terms**: Seven Years' War, French and Indian War, Treaty of Paris (1763), Proclamation of 1763, Pontiac's Rebellion, British war debt, George Washington's early career
- **Source reference**: CED Period 3 Key Concept 3.1

### Topic 3.3: Taxation Without Representation
- **Thematic focus**: PCE, WOR, NAT, WXT
- **Content**:
  - Britain attempted to raise revenue from colonies through new taxes to pay war debts.
  - Stamp Act (1765), Townshend Acts (1767), and Tea Act (1773) provoked colonial resistance.
  - Colonists argued that Parliament could not tax them without colonial representation ("no taxation without representation").
  - Colonial protests: boycotts, Sons of Liberty, Committees of Correspondence, Continental Congresses.
  - Boston Massacre (1770) and Boston Tea Party (1773) escalated tensions.
- **Illustrative examples**: Stamp Act Congress (1765), Sons of Liberty, Boston Massacre (1770), Boston Tea Party (1773), Intolerable Acts (Coercive Acts) 1774, First Continental Congress (1774)
- **Key terms**: Stamp Act, Townshend Acts, Tea Act, Intolerable Acts, "no taxation without representation," virtual representation, Sons of Liberty, Committees of Correspondence, boycott, colonial assemblies
- **Source reference**: CED Period 3 Key Concept 3.1

### Topic 3.4: Philosophical Foundations of the American Revolution
- **Thematic focus**: NAT, PCE, ARC
- **Content**:
  - Colonial leaders drew on Enlightenment thinkers (Locke, Montesquieu, Rousseau) to justify revolution.
  - John Locke's social contract theory — government derives legitimacy from consent of the governed; natural rights to life, liberty, and property.
  - Thomas Paine's "Common Sense" (1776) made the case for independence in plain language.
  - The Declaration of Independence articulated Enlightenment principles: equality, natural rights, consent of the governed, right to revolution.
  - Republican ideology emphasized civic virtue, corruption in government, and the dangers of tyranny.
- **Illustrative examples**: Thomas Paine's "Common Sense" (1776), Declaration of Independence (1776), John Locke's "Two Treatises of Government," Enlightenment thinkers, republican ideology
- **Key terms**: Enlightenment, social contract, natural rights, consent of the governed, republicanism, civic virtue, Thomas Paine, John Locke, Declaration of Independence, Thomas Jefferson
- **Source reference**: CED Period 3 Key Concept 3.2

### Topic 3.5: The American Revolution
- **Thematic focus**: WOR, PCE, NAT, SOC
- **Content**:
  - The Revolutionary War (1775–1783) involved diverse groups with competing loyalties.
  - Loyalists (Tories) opposed independence; Patriots supported it; many remained neutral.
  - George Washington's Continental Army struggled with supply shortages, desertion, and military defeats.
  - Turning point: Battle of Saratoga (1777) convinced France to ally with the Americans.
  - French alliance provided critical military and financial support.
  - Treaty of Paris (1783) granted American independence and territory east of the Mississippi.
- **Illustrative examples**: Battle of Lexington and Concord (1775), Battle of Bunker Hill, Valley Forge (1777–1778), Battle of Saratoga (1777), Yorktown (1781), Treaty of Paris (1783), role of women (Abigail Adams), enslaved Americans and the Revolution
- **Key terms**: Continental Army, Loyalists, Patriots, Battle of Saratoga, French Alliance, Valley Forge, George Washington, Yorktown, Treaty of Paris (1783), African Americans and the Revolution
- **Source reference**: CED Period 3 Key Concept 3.1

### Topic 3.6: The Influence of Revolutionary Ideals
- **Thematic focus**: NAT, SOC, PCE, WOR
- **Content**:
  - Revolutionary ideals of liberty and equality were applied unevenly across social groups.
  - Abigail Adams and others pushed for women's rights; "Remember the Ladies" letter.
  - Many northern states moved toward gradual emancipation after the Revolution; slavery entrenched in the South.
  - Native Americans largely lost land and sovereignty in the post-Revolution settlement.
  - The Revolution sparked broader Atlantic debates about freedom and equality (Haitian Revolution).
  - Some state constitutions expanded voting rights for white men; property requirements reduced.
- **Illustrative examples**: Abigail Adams's letters, northern gradual emancipation laws (Vermont 1777, Pennsylvania 1780), Haitian Revolution (1791–1804), expansion of white male suffrage in state constitutions
- **Key terms**: gradual emancipation, manumission, coverture, women's republican motherhood, Atlantic revolutions, Haitian Revolution, expansion of suffrage, limits of revolutionary ideals
- **Source reference**: CED Period 3 Key Concept 3.2

### Topic 3.7: The Articles of Confederation
- **Thematic focus**: PCE, WXT, WOR
- **Content**:
  - The Articles of Confederation (1781) created a weak central government with no power to tax or regulate commerce.
  - Northwest Ordinance (1787) established a process for admitting new states and banned slavery north of the Ohio River.
  - Shays' Rebellion (1786–1787) in Massachusetts demonstrated the weakness of the Articles in suppressing domestic unrest.
  - Land Ordinance (1785) created a system for surveying and selling western lands.
  - Problems: no national currency, trade disputes between states, inability to pay war debts.
- **Illustrative examples**: Articles of Confederation, Northwest Ordinance (1787), Land Ordinance (1785), Shays' Rebellion (1786–1787), Daniel Shays
- **Key terms**: Articles of Confederation, confederation, Northwest Ordinance, Land Ordinance, Shays' Rebellion, sovereignty, unicameral legislature, weak central government, national debt
- **Source reference**: CED Period 3 Key Concept 3.2

### Topic 3.8: The Constitutional Convention and Debates over Ratification
- **Thematic focus**: PCE, NAT
- **Content**:
  - Constitutional Convention (Philadelphia, 1787) drafted a new frame of government.
  - Key compromises: Great Compromise (bicameral Congress), Three-Fifths Compromise, Commerce Compromise.
  - Federalists (Hamilton, Madison, Jay) supported ratification; argued in "The Federalist Papers."
  - Anti-Federalists (Patrick Henry, George Mason) feared strong central government; demanded Bill of Rights.
  - The Bill of Rights (first 10 amendments) was added in 1791 to address Anti-Federalist concerns.
- **Illustrative examples**: Constitutional Convention (1787), "The Federalist Papers" (Hamilton, Madison, Jay), Anti-Federalist Papers, Patrick Henry, George Mason, Great Compromise, Three-Fifths Compromise, Bill of Rights (1791)
- **Key terms**: Constitutional Convention, Federalists, Anti-Federalists, Federalist Papers, Great Compromise, Three-Fifths Compromise, separation of powers, checks and balances, Bill of Rights, ratification
- **Source reference**: CED Period 3 Key Concept 3.2

### Topic 3.9: The Constitution
- **Thematic focus**: PCE, NAT
- **Content**:
  - The Constitution established a federal system dividing power between national and state governments.
  - Separation of powers among executive, legislative, and judicial branches with checks and balances.
  - The elastic clause and necessary and proper clause allowed for broad federal interpretation.
  - The Constitution protected slavery (Three-Fifths Compromise, fugitive slave clause, slave trade until 1808).
  - Ongoing debates over strict vs. loose construction of the Constitution.
- **Illustrative examples**: Article I (Congress), Article II (President), Article III (Supreme Court), Supremacy Clause, Necessary and Proper Clause, Amendment process, Marbury v. Madison (1803, anticipate)
- **Key terms**: federalism, separation of powers, checks and balances, bicameral, Electoral College, necessary and proper clause, supremacy clause, judicial review, strict vs. loose construction
- **Source reference**: CED Period 3 Key Concept 3.2

### Topic 3.10: Shaping a New Republic
- **Thematic focus**: PCE, WXT, WOR
- **Content**:
  - Washington's presidency established key precedents: cabinet, two-term tradition, Farewell Address warning against partisan politics and foreign entanglements.
  - Hamilton's financial program: national bank, assumption of state debts, tariffs — promoted commercial/industrial economy.
  - Jefferson and Madison opposed Hamilton's program; states' rights and agrarian vision.
  - Whiskey Rebellion (1794) tested federal authority; Washington led militia response.
  - Jay's Treaty (1794) and Pinckney's Treaty (1795) navigated international relations.
  - XYZ Affair (1797–1798) and Quasi-War with France under Adams.
- **Illustrative examples**: Hamilton's Report on Manufactures, Report on Public Credit, Bank of the United States, Whiskey Rebellion (1794), Washington's Farewell Address (1796), Jay's Treaty, XYZ Affair, Alien and Sedition Acts (1798)
- **Key terms**: Hamilton's financial program, national bank, tariff, assumption of debt, Whiskey Rebellion, Washington's Farewell Address, Jay's Treaty, XYZ Affair, Alien and Sedition Acts, Virginia and Kentucky Resolutions
- **Source reference**: CED Period 3 Key Concept 3.3

### Topic 3.11: Developing an American Identity
- **Thematic focus**: NAT, ARC, SOC
- **Content**:
  - Americans debated what national identity meant in the new republic.
  - Republican motherhood — women's role in educating virtuous citizens.
  - The "cult of domesticity" began to take shape.
  - Debates over the proper role of religion in the new republic.
  - Native American policies under the early republic: treaties and land cessions.
- **Illustrative examples**: Republican motherhood concept, Judith Sargent Murray's writings on women's equality, early national literature (Washington Irving, Noah Webster's dictionary), religious disestablishment in Virginia (Statute for Religious Freedom)
- **Key terms**: republican motherhood, cult of domesticity, civic virtue, national identity, religious freedom, disestablishment, Noah Webster, American cultural independence
- **Source reference**: CED Period 3 Key Concept 3.3

### Topic 3.12: Movement in the Early Republic
- **Thematic focus**: MIG, GEO, WXT
- **Content**:
  - Americans moved westward rapidly after independence; the Appalachian frontier attracted settlers.
  - Land speculation and western settlement created conflicts with Native nations.
  - The Northwest Ordinance (1787) provided a legal framework for western expansion.
  - Post-Revolution migration of Loyalists to Canada and the Caribbean.
- **Illustrative examples**: Daniel Boone and the Wilderness Road, Ohio and Kentucky settlement, Proclamation Line ignored after 1783, Loyalist migration to Canada
- **Key terms**: western expansion, frontier, Northwest Territory, Land Ordinance of 1785, Loyalist emigration, Indian land cessions, Ohio Company
- **Source reference**: CED Period 3 Key Concept 3.3

### Topic 3.13: Continuity and Change in Period 3
- **Skill focus**: Continuity and Change Over Time — evaluate what changed and what persisted from colonial period through the early republic.
- **Content**: Apply CCOT to political systems, social structures, economic patterns, and national identity from 1754–1800.
- **Note**: Skill-practice topic. No new content; applies CCOT to Period 3.

# UNIT 4: Period 4 — 1800–1848 (10–17% exam weight, ~17 class periods)

Themes: NAT, WXT, GEO, MIG, PCE, WOR, ARC, SOC
Reasoning Process focus: Causation

### Topic 4.1: Contextualizing Period 4
- **Skill focus**: Contextualization — frame the early 19th century within broader trends of Atlantic market revolution, democratization, and reform movements.
- **Content**: Global economic integration; Industrial Revolution in Britain influencing America; Age of Democratic Revolutions.
- **Note**: Skill-focused framing topic.

### Topic 4.2: The Rise of Political Parties and the Era of Jefferson
- **Thematic focus**: PCE, NAT, WOR
- **Content**:
  - "Revolution of 1800" — Jefferson's election marked peaceful transfer of power between parties; Federalists to Democratic-Republicans.
  - Jefferson pursued limited government, reduced national debt, and curtailed Federalist programs.
  - Louisiana Purchase (1803) doubled the size of the United States; raised constitutional questions.
  - Lewis and Clark Expedition (1804–1806) explored the new western territories.
  - Judiciary Act of 1801 and Marbury v. Madison (1803) established judicial review.
  - Embargo Act (1807) attempted to use economic pressure on Britain and France; damaged American commerce.
- **Illustrative examples**: Jefferson's inauguration (1801), Louisiana Purchase (1803), Marbury v. Madison (1803), Lewis and Clark (1804–1806), Embargo Act (1807), Aaron Burr
- **Key terms**: "Revolution of 1800," Democratic-Republicans, Federalists, Louisiana Purchase, judicial review, Marbury v. Madison, Embargo Act, strict construction, states' rights, John Marshall
- **Source reference**: CED Period 4 Key Concept 4.1

### Topic 4.3: Politics and Regional Interests
- **Thematic focus**: PCE, WXT, GEO, NAT
- **Content**:
  - Sectionalism grew as regional economic interests diverged: North (manufacturing), South (cotton/slavery), West (land/agriculture).
  - Era of Good Feelings (1815–1825) — one-party rule under James Monroe, but underlying tensions.
  - Missouri Compromise (1820) — Missouri admitted as slave state, Maine as free; 36°30' line.
  - The Corrupt Bargain (1824 election) — John Quincy Adams won over Andrew Jackson; splintered Democratic-Republicans.
  - "Tariff of Abominations" (1828) — high protective tariff angered the South; Nullification Crisis followed.
- **Illustrative examples**: Missouri Compromise (1820), Era of Good Feelings, "Corrupt Bargain" of 1824, Tariff of 1828, South Carolina's nullification doctrine (Calhoun)

- **Illustrative examples**: War of 1812, Battle of New Orleans (Jackson, 1815), Treaty of Ghent, Hartford Convention, Monroe Doctrine (1823), Simon Bolívar and Latin American independence

### Topic 4.5: Market Revolution: Industrialization
- **Thematic focus**: WXT, GEO, MIG

### Topic 4.8: Jackson and Federal Power

# UNIT 5: Period 5 — 1844–1877 (10–17% exam weight, ~17 class periods)

Themes: NAT, WXT, GEO, MIG, PCE, WOR, ARC, SOC
Reasoning Process focus: Comparison

### Topic 5.1: Contextualizing Period 5
- **Skill focus**: Contextualization — frame sectional conflict, Civil War, and Reconstruction within the broader context of 19th-century nationalism and industrialization.
- **Content**: Global context of mid-19th century nationalism; European revolutions of 1848; industrial capitalism's impact on labor and society.
- **Note**: Skill-focused framing topic.

### Topic 5.2: Manifest Destiny
- **Thematic focus**: GEO, MIG, NAT, WOR
- **Content**:
  - Manifest Destiny — belief that U.S. expansion to the Pacific was God-given and inevitable.
  - Oregon Trail and overland migration to the Pacific Northwest; Oregon Treaty with Britain (1846) settled the boundary at 49th parallel.
  - Texas annexation (1845) after independence from Mexico (Texas Revolution 1836).
  - Conflict with Native nations intensified as settlers moved west.
  - Debates over whether new western territories would be slave or free.
- **Illustrative examples**: John O'Sullivan coined "Manifest Destiny" (1845), Oregon Trail, Texas annexation (1845), Oregon Treaty (1846), Battle of the Alamo (1836), Sam Houston
- **Key terms**: Manifest Destiny, Oregon Trail, 49th parallel, Oregon Treaty, Texas annexation, Republic of Texas, Sam Houston, "Remember the Alamo," western migration
- **Source reference**: CED Period 5 Key Concept 5.1

### Topic 5.3: The Mexican–American War
- **Thematic focus**: WOR, GEO, PCE, NAT
- **Content**:
  - President Polk provoked war with Mexico in 1846 by sending troops to disputed territory between the Rio Grande and Nueces River.
  - U.S. won decisively; Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo (1848) gave U.S. Mexican Cession (California, New Mexico, Arizona, Nevada, Utah, parts of Colorado).
  - War intensified debate over slavery's expansion into new territories.
  - Wilmot Proviso (1846) proposed banning slavery in territory gained from Mexico; passed House, failed Senate.
  - Critics (Abraham Lincoln, Henry David Thoreau) opposed the war as unjust aggression.
- **Illustrative examples**: Polk's provocation, Battle of Buena Vista (Zachary Taylor), General Winfield Scott's capture of Mexico City, Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo (1848), Wilmot Proviso (1846), Mexican Cession
- **Key terms**: Mexican-American War, Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo, Mexican Cession, Wilmot Proviso, popular sovereignty, manifest destiny, Abraham Lincoln's "spot resolutions"
- **Source reference**: CED Period 5 Key Concept 5.1

### Topic 5.4: The Compromise of 1850
- **Thematic focus**: PCE, NAT, SOC
- **Content**:
  - California Gold Rush (1849) brought rapid population growth; California sought statehood as a free state.
  - Compromise of 1850 (Henry Clay, Stephen Douglas): California admitted free; New Mexico and Utah territories organized with popular sovereignty; Fugitive Slave Act strengthened; slave trade banned in Washington D.C.
  - Fugitive Slave Act outraged northerners; "Uncle Tom's Cabin" (Harriet Beecher Stowe, 1852) inflamed northern opinion.
  - Compromise temporarily eased tensions but deepened fundamental disagreements.
- **Illustrative examples**: California Gold Rush (1849), Compromise of 1850, Henry Clay's last major compromise, Fugitive Slave Act (1850), "Uncle Tom's Cabin" (1852), Harriet Beecher Stowe
- **Key terms**: Compromise of 1850, California statehood, popular sovereignty, Fugitive Slave Act, "Uncle Tom's Cabin," Harriet Beecher Stowe, Henry Clay, Daniel Webster, John C. Calhoun
- **Source reference**: CED Period 5 Key Concept 5.2

### Topic 5.5: Sectional Conflict: Regional Differences
- **Thematic focus**: WXT, SOC, NAT, GEO
- **Content**:
  - The North and South had increasingly divergent economic systems, social structures, and political cultures.
  - North: industrializing, wage labor, growing urban population, anti-slavery sentiment grew.
  - South: plantation agriculture, enslaved labor, rural, committed to slavery as social and economic system.
  - West: small farmers seeking free land without competition from slave labor.
  - Free Soil Party (1848) opposed slavery's expansion; represented northern small farmers and workers.
- **Illustrative examples**: Free Soil Party, Harriet Beecher Stowe's "Uncle Tom's Cabin," economic statistics on North vs. South, Impending Crisis of the South (Hinton Rowan Helper, 1857)
- **Key terms**: sectionalism, free labor ideology, free soil movement, slave power conspiracy, "Slave Power," wage labor vs. slave labor, industrial capitalism
- **Source reference**: CED Period 5 Key Concept 5.2

### Topic 5.6: Failure of Compromise
- **Thematic focus**: PCE, NAT, SOC
- **Content**:
  - Kansas-Nebraska Act (1854) repealed Missouri Compromise; allowed popular sovereignty in Kansas and Nebraska.
  - "Bleeding Kansas" — pro-slavery and anti-slavery settlers flooded Kansas; violence erupted.
  - Republican Party formed (1854) in direct opposition to Kansas-Nebraska Act; anti-slavery expansion platform.
  - Caning of Senator Charles Sumner (1856) by Rep. Preston Brooks — symbolized breakdown of civility.
  - Dred Scott v. Sandford (1857) — Supreme Court ruled enslaved people were not citizens; Congress could not ban slavery in territories (invalidated Missouri Compromise).
  - John Brown's raid on Harpers Ferry (1859) — attempted to spark slave rebellion; inflamed South.
- **Illustrative examples**: Kansas-Nebraska Act (1854), "Bleeding Kansas," Republican Party formation (1854), Caning of Sumner (1856), Dred Scott v. Sandford (1857), John Brown, Harpers Ferry (1859), Lincoln-Douglas debates (1858)
- **Key terms**: Kansas-Nebraska Act, popular sovereignty, Bleeding Kansas, Republican Party, Dred Scott, Chief Justice Taney, John Brown, Harpers Ferry, Lincoln-Douglas debates, Stephen Douglas, Abraham Lincoln
- **Source reference**: CED Period 5 Key Concept 5.2

### Topic 5.7: Election of 1860 and Secession
- **Thematic focus**: PCE, NAT
- **Content**:
  - Election of 1860: Lincoln (Republican) won without a single southern electoral vote; Democratic Party split between Douglas (North) and Breckinridge (South).
  - South Carolina seceded in December 1860; six more deep South states followed by February 1861.
  - Confederate States of America formed; Jefferson Davis elected president.
  - Lincoln's election seen in the South as existential threat to slavery.
  - Crittenden Compromise failed as last attempt to avert war.
- **Illustrative examples**: Election of 1860 (four-way race), South Carolina secession (December 1860), Confederate States of America, Jefferson Davis, Crittenden Compromise, Fort Sumter (April 1861)
- **Key terms**: Election of 1860, secession, Confederate States of America, Jefferson Davis, Abraham Lincoln, Fort Sumter, Crittenden Compromise, states' rights, slavery as the cause of secession
- **Source reference**: CED Period 5 Key Concept 5.2

### Topic 5.8: Military Conflict in the Civil War
- **Thematic focus**: PCE, WOR, NAT
- **Content**:
  - Civil War (1861–1865): North vs. South; Union vs. Confederacy.
  - Major turning points: Battle of Antietam (1862) — bloodiest single day; Battle of Gettysburg (1863) — Confederate high tide; Sherman's March to the Sea (1864).
  - North's advantages: larger population, industrial base, railroads, navy blockade.
  - South's advantages: defensive posture, motivated soldiers, early strong military leadership (Lee).
  - African American soldiers: USCT (United States Colored Troops) — over 180,000 served; 54th Massachusetts Infantry.
- **Illustrative examples**: Battle of Antietam (1862), Battle of Gettysburg (1863), Grant's Overland Campaign, Sherman's March (1864–1865), 54th Massachusetts Infantry, Ulysses S. Grant, Robert E. Lee, William Tecumseh Sherman
- **Key terms**: Union, Confederacy, Battle of Antietam, Battle of Gettysburg, total war, blockade, USCT (U.S. Colored Troops), Anaconda Plan, 54th Massachusetts Infantry, Appomattox (April 1865)
- **Source reference**: CED Period 5 Key Concept 5.3

### Topic 5.9: Government Policies During the Civil War
- **Thematic focus**: PCE, WXT, SOC, NAT
- **Content**:
  - Emancipation Proclamation (January 1, 1863) — freed enslaved people in Confederate states; transformed war aims; allowed Black enlistment.
  - 13th Amendment (1865) — abolished slavery throughout the United States.
  - Morrill Tariff Act, Homestead Act (1862), Pacific Railroad Act (1862), Land-Grant College Act (Morrill Act, 1862) — transformative wartime legislation.
  - Confederate policies: conscription, impressment, states' rights tensions within the Confederacy.
  - Copperheads (Peace Democrats) opposed the war in the North.
- **Illustrative examples**: Emancipation Proclamation (1863), 13th Amendment (1865), Homestead Act (1862), Morrill Land-Grant Act, habeas corpus suspension, draft riots (New York, 1863), Copperheads
- **Key terms**: Emancipation Proclamation, 13th Amendment, Homestead Act, Morrill Act, habeas corpus, Copperheads, contraband camps, Confederate home front, Union conscription, New York Draft Riots
- **Source reference**: CED Period 5 Key Concept 5.3

### Topic 5.10: Reconstruction
- **Thematic focus**: PCE, SOC, NAT, WXT
- **Content**:
  - Presidential Reconstruction (Lincoln/Johnson): lenient terms; Black Codes in southern states restricted freedpeople's rights.
  - Congressional (Radical) Reconstruction: 14th Amendment (1868, citizenship and equal protection), 15th Amendment (1870, Black male suffrage), Freedmen's Bureau.
  - Reconstruction governments in southern states: biracial coalitions; public schools established; some Black elected officials.
  - Freedpeople sought land, education, family reunification, and political participation.
  - Northern Republican commitment to Reconstruction waned by the early 1870s.
- **Illustrative examples**: Black Codes, Freedmen's Bureau, 14th Amendment (1868), 15th Amendment (1870), Reconstruction governments, Hiram Revels (first Black senator), Joseph Rainey, carpetbaggers, scalawags
- **Key terms**: Reconstruction, Presidential Reconstruction, Radical Reconstruction, 14th Amendment, 15th Amendment, Freedmen's Bureau, Black Codes, freedpeople, carpetbaggers, scalawags, biracial democracy
- **Source reference**: CED Period 5 Key Concept 5.3

### Topic 5.11: Failure of Reconstruction
- **Thematic focus**: PCE, SOC, NAT
- **Content**:
  - White supremacist violence: Ku Klux Klan, White League, Red Shirts used terror to suppress Black political participation.
  - Enforcement Acts (1870–1871) targeted KKK; temporarily effective.
  - "Redeemer" Democrats regained control of southern state governments.
  - Compromise of 1877 — Hayes-Tilden dispute resolved by withdrawing federal troops from the South; effectively ended Reconstruction.
  - Sharecropping and convict leasing emerged as neo-slave labor systems.
  - "Solid South" — Democratic control of the South for the next century.
- **Illustrative examples**: Ku Klux Klan, Enforcement Acts, Slaughterhouse Cases (1873), Civil Rights Cases (1883, anticipate), Compromise of 1877, Rutherford B. Hayes, Samuel Tilden, sharecropping, convict leasing
- **Key terms**: Ku Klux Klan, Redeemers, Compromise of 1877, sharecropping, convict leasing, "Solid South," failure of Reconstruction, white supremacy, voter suppression, lost cause mythology
- **Source reference**: CED Period 5 Key Concept 5.3

### Topic 5.12: Comparison in Period 5
- **Skill focus**: Comparison — compare northern and southern war aims, wartime experiences, Reconstruction policies and outcomes.
- **Content**: Apply comparison to perspectives on slavery, secession, wartime policies, and Reconstruction visions.

# UNIT 6: Period 6 — 1865–1898 (10–17% exam weight, ~18 class periods)

### Topic 6.2: Westward Expansion: Economic Development
- **Thematic focus**: WXT, GEO, MIG, WOR
- **Content**:
  - Transcontinental railroad (completed 1869, Promontory Summit, Utah) accelerated western settlement and economic integration.
  - Cattle industry: long drives from Texas to railheads (Abilene, Dodge City); open range era.
  - Mining booms (Comstock Lode, Black Hills gold) attracted fortune-seekers.
  - Homestead Act (1862) attracted small farmers to the Great Plains; sod houses and dry farming.
  - Commercial farming on the Great Plains; mechanization (McCormick reaper).

### Topic 6.3: Westward Expansion: Social and Cultural Development
- **Thematic focus**: SOC, GEO, NAT, MIG
- **Content**:
  - Plains Indians (Sioux, Cheyenne, Comanche) resisted displacement; series of Indian Wars.
  - Buffalo extermination was deliberate federal policy to undermine Plains Indian subsistence.
  - Dawes Severalty Act (1887) — allotted tribal land to individual Indians; aimed at assimilation; destroyed tribal land bases.
  - Battle of Little Bighorn (1876) — Custer's defeat; temporary Native victory.
  - Wounded Knee Massacre (1890) — U.S. Army killed ~250 Sioux; symbolized end of armed resistance.
  - Helen Hunt Jackson's "A Century of Dishonor" (1881) criticized federal Indian policy.
- **Illustrative examples**: Little Bighorn (1876), Wounded Knee (1890), Dawes Act (1887), Sitting Bull, Crazy Horse, Ghost Dance movement, Helen Hunt Jackson, boarding schools (Carlisle)
- **Key terms**: Plains Indians, Indian Wars, Buffalo extermination, Dawes Severalty Act, assimilation policy, Little Bighorn, Wounded Knee, Sitting Bull, Crazy Horse, reservation system, boarding schools
- **Source reference**: CED Period 6 Key Concept 6.1

### Topic 6.4: The "New South"
- **Thematic focus**: WXT, SOC, PCE, NAT
- **Content**:
  - Proponents of a "New South" (Henry Grady) promoted industrial development in the post-Reconstruction South.
  - Limited industrialization: textile mills, tobacco processing, iron/steel (Birmingham, AL).
  - Jim Crow laws: legal segregation enacted across the South; separate but unequal.
  - Plessy v. Ferguson (1896) upheld "separate but equal" doctrine.
  - Convict leasing, sharecropping, debt peonage bound Black southerners in near-slavery.
  - Lynching as racial terror: thousands of African Americans lynched 1877–1950.
- **Illustrative examples**: Henry Grady's "New South" speech, Birmingham steel, Plessy v. Ferguson (1896), convict leasing, sharecropping, lynching statistics, Ida B. Wells's anti-lynching campaign
- **Key terms**: New South, Jim Crow, Plessy v. Ferguson, separate but equal, convict leasing, sharecropping, debt peonage, lynching, Ida B. Wells, disfranchisement (poll tax, literacy tests, grandfather clause)
- **Source reference**: CED Period 6 Key Concept 6.1

### Topic 6.5: Technological Innovation
- **Thematic focus**: WXT, ARC
- **Content**:
  - Second Industrial Revolution driven by new technologies: steel, electricity, petroleum, chemicals.
  - Thomas Edison's lab at Menlo Park; electric light bulb (1879), phonograph.
  - Alexander Graham Bell's telephone (1876).
  - Steel industry: Bessemer process made cheap steel possible; Andrew Carnegie's vertical integration.
  - Railroads as the first modern corporations; standard gauge; time zones (1883).
  - Mass production techniques beginning to emerge.
- **Illustrative examples**: Thomas Edison (light bulb 1879, phonograph), Alexander Graham Bell (telephone 1876), Bessemer steel process, Carnegie Steel, railroad corporations, Standard Oil (petroleum)
- **Key terms**: Second Industrial Revolution, Bessemer process, vertical integration, horizontal integration, trusts, monopoly, electricity, petroleum, telephone, mass production
- **Source reference**: CED Period 6 Key Concept 6.2

### Topic 6.6: The Rise of Industrial Capitalism
- **Thematic focus**: WXT, PCE
- **Content**:
  - A small number of industrialists (Robber Barons / Captains of Industry) accumulated enormous wealth.
  - Andrew Carnegie (steel) used vertical integration; John D. Rockefeller (Standard Oil) used horizontal integration and trusts.
  - J.P. Morgan used finance capital to dominate industries.
  - Social Darwinism: Herbert Spencer's ideas applied to business — "survival of the fittest" justified wealth inequality.
  - Gospel of Wealth (Carnegie): wealthy had obligation to give back philanthropically.
  - Antitrust: Sherman Antitrust Act (1890) — first federal attempt to regulate monopolies; weakly enforced initially.
- **Illustrative examples**: Andrew Carnegie, John D. Rockefeller, J.P. Morgan, Standard Oil trust, Carnegie Steel, Social Darwinism, "Gospel of Wealth" essay, Sherman Antitrust Act (1890)
- **Key terms**: robber barons, captains of industry, vertical integration, horizontal integration, trust, monopoly, Social Darwinism, Gospel of Wealth, Sherman Antitrust Act, laissez-faire capitalism
- **Source reference**: CED Period 6 Key Concept 6.2

### Topic 6.7: Labor in the Gilded Age
- **Thematic focus**: WXT, SOC, PCE
- **Content**:
  - Industrial workers faced dangerous conditions, long hours, low wages, and child labor.
  - Labor unions sought to improve conditions: Knights of Labor (1869) — open to all workers; American Federation of Labor (AFL, 1886) — skilled craft unions.
  - Major strikes: Great Railroad Strike (1877), Haymarket Affair (1886), Homestead Strike (1892), Pullman Strike (1894).
  - Government and business used courts, militia, and federal troops to suppress labor actions.
  - Women and children comprised large portions of industrial workforce.
- **Illustrative examples**: Knights of Labor (Terence Powderly), AFL (Samuel Gompers), Great Railroad Strike (1877), Haymarket Affair (1886), Homestead Strike (1892), Pullman Strike (1894), Pinkerton Agency
- **Key terms**: Knights of Labor, American Federation of Labor, Samuel Gompers, strike, collective bargaining, Haymarket Affair, Homestead Strike, Pullman Strike, injunction, yellow-dog contract, child labor
- **Source reference**: CED Period 6 Key Concept 6.2

### Topic 6.8: Immigration and Migration in the Gilded Age
- **Thematic focus**: MIG, SOC, NAT, GEO
- **Content**:
  - "New Immigration" (1880s–1910s): Southern and Eastern Europeans (Italians, Poles, Russians, Jews, Greeks) replaced earlier Northern European immigrants.
  - Chinese immigration to the West; Chinese Exclusion Act (1882) — first major federal restriction on immigration.
  - Internal migration: African Americans began moving to northern cities; Great Migration accelerated later.
  - Immigrants settled in ethnic enclaves in industrial cities; maintained cultural traditions.
  - Ellis Island (opened 1892) as gateway for European immigrants.
- **Illustrative examples**: Ellis Island, Chinese Exclusion Act (1882), steerage conditions, ethnic neighborhoods (Little Italy, Jewish Lower East Side), Jacob Riis's "How the Other Half Lives" (1890)
- **Key terms**: New Immigration, push/pull factors, Ellis Island, Chinese Exclusion Act, ethnic enclave, nativism, assimilation, Jacob Riis, tenement housing, Angel Island

### Topic 6.9: Responses to Immigration in the Gilded Age
- **Thematic focus**: NAT, SOC, PCE
- **Content**:

### Topic 6.10: Development of the Middle Class

### Topic 6.11: Reform in the Gilded Age
- **Thematic focus**: PCE, SOC
- **Content**:

  - Omaha Platform (1892) articulated Populist demands.
  - Election of 1896: William Jennings Bryan (Democrat-Populist) vs. William McKinley (Republican); Bryan's "Cross of Gold" speech; McKinley won; Populism collapsed.
  - Mugwumps and civil service reform: Pendleton Civil Service Act (1883) replaced spoils system with merit system.
- **Illustrative examples**: Farmers' Alliance, Populist Party, Omaha Platform (1892), William Jennings Bryan, "Cross of Gold" speech (1896), Election of 1896, Pendleton Civil Service Act (1883)

### Topic 6.12: Controversies over the Role of Government in the Gilded Age
- **Thematic focus**: PCE, WXT
- **Content**:
  - Laissez-faire ideology dominated business thinking; government should not interfere in the economy.
  - Early regulation: Interstate Commerce Act (1887) — regulated railroads; created ICC.
  - Sherman Antitrust Act (1890) — broadly worded but weakly enforced against business; used against labor unions.
  - Tariff debates: Republicans supported high protective tariffs; Democrats opposed.
  - Supreme Court narrowly interpreted federal power to regulate commerce in this era.
- **Illustrative examples**: Interstate Commerce Act (1887), ICC, Sherman Antitrust Act (1890), high protective tariffs, Wabash v. Illinois (1886), Social Darwinism as policy ideology
- **Key terms**: laissez-faire, Interstate Commerce Act, Interstate Commerce Commission, Sherman Antitrust Act, protective tariff, court injunctions against labor, limited government
- **Source reference**: CED Period 6 Key Concept 6.3

### Topic 6.13: Politics in the Gilded Age
- **Thematic focus**: PCE, SOC
- **Content**:
  - Gilded Age politics marked by high voter turnout, fierce party competition, and widespread corruption.

- **Illustrative examples**: Tammany Hall, Boss Tweed, Roscoe Conkling, James Garfield assassination (1881), Chester Arthur's civil service reform (Pendleton Act), Mugwumps supporting Grover Cleveland (1884)
- **Key terms**: spoils system, patronage, machine politics, Tammany Hall, Boss Tweed, Mugwumps, Stalwarts, Half-Breeds, civil service reform, Pendleton Act, Gilded Age corruption

### Topic 7.3: The Spanish–American War
- **Thematic focus**: WOR, PCE, NAT
- **Content**:

  - Roosevelt Corollary (1904) to Monroe Doctrine: U.S. as "policeman" of Western Hemisphere.
- **Illustrative examples**: USS Maine, yellow journalism, Rough Riders, Theodore Roosevelt at San Juan Hill, Treaty of Paris (1898), Philippine-American War, Platt Amendment, Roosevelt Corollary (1904)

### Topic 7.4: The Progressives
- **Thematic focus**: PCE, SOC, WXT, ARC
- **Content**:
  - Progressivism (1890s–1920s): middle-class reformers sought to address corruption, poverty, and inequality created by industrialization.
  - Muckrakers exposed corruption and social problems: Ida Tarbell (Standard Oil), Upton Sinclair ("The Jungle"), Lincoln Steffens ("The Shame of the Cities").
  - Political reforms: direct democracy (initiative, referendum, recall), direct primary, 17th Amendment (direct election of senators, 1913).
  - Social reforms: settlement houses, child labor laws, women's suffrage (19th Amendment, 1920).
  - Constitutional amendments: 16th (income tax), 17th (direct election of senators), 18th (Prohibition), 19th (women's suffrage).
  - TR's "Square Deal," Taft's trust-busting, Wilson's "New Freedom."
- **Illustrative examples**: Ida Tarbell, Upton Sinclair's "The Jungle," muckrakers, 16th Amendment (1913), 17th Amendment (1913), 18th Amendment (1919), 19th Amendment (1920), Pure Food and Drug Act (1906), NAACP (1909)
- **Key terms**: Progressivism, muckrakers, direct democracy, initiative/referendum/recall, 16th-19th Amendments, Pure Food and Drug Act, NAACP, women's suffrage, Ida Tarbell, Upton Sinclair, Jane Addams

### Topic 7.5: World War I: Military and Diplomacy
- **Thematic focus**: WOR, PCE, NAT
- **Content**:
  - U.S. initially neutral (1914–1917); Wilson's policy of neutrality challenged by German submarine warfare.
  - Lusitania sinking (1915), Sussex Pledge, Zimmermann Telegram (1917), resumption of unrestricted submarine warfare pushed U.S. into war.
  - U.S. declared war April 1917; AEF (American Expeditionary Forces) under General Pershing.
  - Wilson's Fourteen Points (1918) outlined liberal internationalist peace program.
  - Paris Peace Conference: Treaty of Versailles (1919); harsh terms on Germany; Wilson's League of Nations rejected by Senate.
  - Senate rejection of the League (Henry Cabot Lodge) marked return to isolationism.
- **Illustrative examples**: Lusitania (1915), Zimmermann Telegram (1917), General Pershing, Fourteen Points, Treaty of Versailles, League of Nations, Henry Cabot Lodge, Senate rejection of the League
- **Key terms**: neutrality, submarine warfare, Lusitania, Zimmermann Telegram, AEF, Fourteen Points, self-determination, League of Nations, Treaty of Versailles, Senate reservationists, isolationism
- **Source reference**: CED Period 7 Key Concept 7.3

### Topic 7.6: World War I: Home Front
- **Thematic focus**: WXT, SOC, NAT, PCE
- **Content**:
  - War mobilization transformed the American economy and government's role.
  - War Industries Board, Food Administration (Hoover), Committee on Public Information (Creel).
  - Great Migration: hundreds of thousands of Black southerners moved to northern cities for war industry jobs.
  - Civil liberties curtailed: Espionage Act (1917), Sedition Act (1918) — Schenck v. U.S. (1919, "clear and present danger").
  - Women filled industrial jobs; strengthened suffrage movement.
  - Anti-German sentiment and nativism; anti-immigrant hostility.
- **Illustrative examples**: War Industries Board, Food Administration, Committee on Public Information, Great Migration (World War I phase), Schenck v. U.S. (1919), Espionage Act, Eugene Debs imprisoned
- **Key terms**: War Industries Board, Great Migration, Committee on Public Information, Espionage Act, Sedition Act, Schenck v. U.S., civil liberties, home front mobilization, women in war industries
- **Source reference**: CED Period 7 Key Concept 7.2

### Topic 7.7: 1920s: Immigration
- **Thematic focus**: MIG, SOC, NAT
- **Content**:

  - Continued migration within the U.S. (Great Migration ongoing; Hispanic migration to Southwest).
- **Illustrative examples**: Emergency Quota Act (1921), National Origins Act / Johnson-Reed Act (1924), Palmer Raids, Red Scare (1919–1920), Sacco and Vanzetti, J. Edgar Hoover

### Topic 7.8: 1920s: Cultural and Political Controversies
- **Thematic focus**: ARC, SOC, NAT, PCE
- **Content**:

  - "Return to normalcy" — Harding, Coolidge, and Republican laissez-faire politics.

- **Key terms**: Roaring Twenties, consumer culture, Jazz Age, Harlem Renaissance, Prohibition, Scopes Trial, fundamentalism, modernism, KKK revival, mass media, "return to normalcy"
- **Source reference**: CED Period 7 Key Concept 7.2

### Topic 7.9: The Great Depression
- **Thematic focus**: WXT, SOC, PCE, GEO
- **Content**:

  - Hoover's limited response: Reconstruction Finance Corporation; opposed direct federal relief.
  - Bonus Army (1932) — WWI veterans marched on Washington; driven out by Army.
  - Dust Bowl: drought + poor land practices devastated Great Plains; Okies migrated to California.
- **Illustrative examples**: Black Tuesday (October 29, 1929), Smoot-Hawley Tariff (1930), Hoovervilles, Bonus Army (1932), Dust Bowl, Okies, Dorothea Lange's photography, Reconstruction Finance Corporation

  - Civil Rights Act (1964) and Voting Rights Act (1965) were legislative culminations.
- **Illustrative examples**: Greensboro sit-ins (1960), Freedom Rides, Birmingham (Bull Connor, fire hoses), "Letter from Birmingham Jail," March on Washington and "I Have a Dream" speech (1963), Freedom Summer, Bloody Sunday (Selma, 1965)
- **Key terms**: nonviolent direct action, sit-ins, Freedom Rides, SNCC, SCLC, CORE, Birmingham campaign, March on Washington, "I Have a Dream," Freedom Summer, Selma, Bloody Sunday, Civil Rights Act (1964), Voting Rights Act (1965)
- **Source reference**: CED Period 8 Key Concept 8.2

### Topic 8.11: The Civil Rights Movement Expands
- **Thematic focus**: SOC, PCE, NAT
- **Content**:
  - Black Power movement (mid-1960s): Stokely Carmichael, Black Panther Party — emphasized Black self-determination and armed self-defense.
  - Urban rebellions: Watts (1965), Newark, Detroit (1967) — reflected northern Black frustration with poverty and police violence.
  - Other civil rights movements inspired by African American struggle: Chicano movement (La Raza), American Indian Movement (AIM), Asian American movement, LGBTQ+ movement (Stonewall, 1969).
  - Women's liberation movement: NOW (1966, Betty Friedan), second-wave feminism, ERA debate.
  - Disability rights movement.
- **Illustrative examples**: Black Power (Stokely Carmichael), Black Panther Party (Bobby Seale, Huey Newton), Watts Rebellion (1965), Chicano movement / United Farm Workers (Cesar Chavez, Dolores Huerta), AIM (Wounded Knee 1973), Stonewall (1969), NOW, ERA
- **Key terms**: Black Power, Black Panther Party, urban rebellions, Chicano movement, La Raza, AIM, LGBTQ+ rights, Stonewall, second-wave feminism, NOW, ERA, disability rights, intersectionality
- **Source reference**: CED Period 8 Key Concept 8.2

### Topic 8.12: Youth Culture of the 1960s
- **Thematic focus**: ARC, SOC, NAT
- **Content**:
  - Baby boom generation came of age; college enrollment surged.
  - Counterculture: hippies, drug experimentation, Woodstock (1969), sexual revolution.
  - New Left: SDS (Students for a Democratic Society), Port Huron Statement (1962).
  - Student antiwar protests: Berkeley Free Speech Movement (1964), campus demonstrations.
  - Cultural revolution in music (Bob Dylan, Beatles, Jimi Hendrix), film, and art.
- **Illustrative examples**: SDS and Port Huron Statement (1962), Berkeley Free Speech Movement, Woodstock (1969), Bob Dylan, counterculture, Tom Hayden, hippie culture, sexual revolution, Kent State (1970)
- **Key terms**: counterculture, New Left, SDS, Port Huron Statement, Free Speech Movement, hippies, Woodstock, sexual revolution, generation gap, antiwar movement
- **Source reference**: CED Period 8 Key Concept 8.3

### Topic 8.13: The Environment and Natural Resources from 1968 to 1980
- **Thematic focus**: GEO, PCE, WXT
- **Content**:
  - Environmental movement grew from 1960s counterculture and public health concerns.
  - Rachel Carson's "Silent Spring" (1962) exposed dangers of pesticides; sparked modern environmentalism.
  - Earth Day (April 22, 1970) mobilized millions.
  - Environmental legislation: Clean Air Act (1970), Clean Water Act (1972), EPA created (1970), Endangered Species Act (1973).
  - Nixon signed major environmental laws despite being a Republican.
  - Energy crisis (1973 OPEC oil embargo) — fuel shortages, stagflation.
- **Illustrative examples**: Rachel Carson's "Silent Spring," Earth Day (1970), EPA creation (1970), Clean Air Act, Clean Water Act, Love Canal, OPEC oil embargo (1973)
- **Key terms**: environmentalism, Rachel Carson, "Silent Spring," Earth Day, EPA, Clean Air Act, Clean Water Act, Endangered Species Act, OPEC, energy crisis, stagflation
- **Source reference**: CED Period 8 Key Concept 8.3

### Topic 8.14: Society in Transition
- **Thematic focus**: PCE, NAT, SOC, WXT
- **Content**:

  - Stagflation: combination of inflation and unemployment; challenged Keynesian economics.

  - Rise of the New Right: Moral Majority, anti-ERA, anti-abortion, white evangelical Christianity in politics.
- **Illustrative examples**: Nixon's "Southern Strategy," Nixon's China visit (1972), Watergate (1972–1974), Nixon's resignation, Gerald Ford's pardon, stagflation, Camp David Accords (1978), Iran hostage crisis (1979), Moral Majority (Jerry Falwell)
- **Key terms**: Nixon, détente, Southern Strategy, Watergate, Saturday Night Massacre, articles of impeachment, stagflation, Camp David Accords, Iran hostage crisis, New Right, Moral Majority
- **Source reference**: CED Period 8 Key Concept 8.3

# UNIT 9: Period 9 — 1980–Present (4–6% exam weight, ~8 class periods)

Themes: NAT, WXT, GEO, MIG, PCE, WOR, ARC, SOC
Reasoning Process focus: Causation

### Topic 9.1: Contextualizing Period 9
- **Skill focus**: Contextualization — frame Reagan-era conservatism and post-Cold War America within global context of neoliberalism, globalization, and shifting power.
- **Content**: Global spread of market economies; collapse of the Soviet Union; rise of global terrorism; digital revolution.
- **Note**: Skill-focused framing topic.

### Topic 9.2: Reagan and Conservatism
- **Thematic focus**: PCE, WXT, NAT, SOC
- **Content**:
  - Ronald Reagan's election (1980) marked triumph of New Right conservatism.
  - Reaganomics: supply-side economics ("trickle-down"), tax cuts (Economic Recovery Tax Act, 1981), deregulation, reduced domestic spending.
  - Reagan's foreign policy: defense buildup, SDI ("Star Wars"), support for anti-communist movements (Reagan Doctrine), Iran-Contra scandal.
  - Social conservatism: opposition to abortion (Roe v. Wade rollback campaign), school prayer, gay rights.
  - Conservative coalition: evangelical Christians, business interests, anti-government libertarians, suburban whites.
  - Crack cocaine epidemic and War on Drugs; AIDS crisis and government response.
- **Illustrative examples**: Economic Recovery Tax Act (1981), Reaganomics, SDI, Iran-Contra Affair, Reagan Doctrine, Moral Majority, war on drugs, AIDS crisis and Ryan White, Sandra Day O'Connor (first female Supreme Court justice)
- **Key terms**: Reaganomics, supply-side economics, deregulation, Reagan Doctrine, Iran-Contra, SDI, New Right, evangelical political mobilization, War on Drugs, AIDS crisis
- **Source reference**: CED Period 9 Key Concept 9.2

### Topic 9.3: The End of the Cold War
- **Thematic focus**: WOR, PCE, NAT
- **Content**:
  - Gorbachev's reforms (glasnost, perestroika) weakened the Soviet Union.
  - Fall of the Berlin Wall (November 9, 1989) — symbolized Cold War's end.
  - Soviet Union dissolved (December 1991).
  - George H.W. Bush navigated the end of the Cold War and German reunification.
  - Gulf War (1991): U.S.-led coalition expelled Iraq from Kuwait; demonstration of U.S. military power.
  - "New World Order" — U.S. as sole superpower; debates over future of U.S. foreign policy.
- **Illustrative examples**: Gorbachev, glasnost, perestroika, fall of the Berlin Wall (1989), dissolution of the USSR (1991), Gulf War / Operation Desert Storm (1991), George H.W. Bush
- **Key terms**: Cold War end, glasnost, perestroika, Berlin Wall, dissolution of the Soviet Union, New World Order, Gulf War, Operation Desert Storm, U.S. as sole superpower
- **Source reference**: CED Period 9 Key Concept 9.3

### Topic 9.4: A Changing Economy
- **Thematic focus**: WXT, SOC, GEO, MIG
- **Content**:
  - Deindustrialization: manufacturing jobs moved overseas; rise of the service economy.
  - Globalization: NAFTA (1994) and WTO (1995) expanded free trade.
  - Technology revolution: personal computers (Apple, IBM), internet (World Wide Web, 1991), dot-com boom and bust.
  - Income inequality grew; middle class squeezed; labor union decline.
  - Growing economic role of women and minorities in the workforce.
  - Immigration: Hispanic and Asian immigration surged after 1965 Immigration Act changes.
- **Illustrative examples**: NAFTA (1994), WTO, personal computer revolution, internet boom, decline of manufacturing in the Rust Belt, income inequality data (Gini coefficient), dot-com bubble (late 1990s)
- **Key terms**: deindustrialization, globalization, NAFTA, WTO, internet revolution, service economy, income inequality, outsourcing, dot-com, free trade, labor union decline
- **Source reference**: CED Period 9 Key Concept 9.1

### Topic 9.5: Migration and Immigration in the 1990s and 2000s
- **Thematic focus**: MIG, SOC, NAT, PCE
- **Content**:
  - Immigration continued at high levels; shift to Latino and Asian immigration.
  - Debates over undocumented immigration; border enforcement; DREAM Act.
  - Immigration Reform and Control Act (IRCA, 1986) — amnesty for some undocumented immigrants.
  - Anti-immigration sentiment: Proposition 187 (California, 1994).
  - Refugees from conflicts: Vietnam (earlier), Central America, Kosovo, Iraq, Afghanistan.
  - Internal migration: Sunbelt growth continued; suburbanization; gentrification in cities.
- **Illustrative examples**: IRCA (1986), Proposition 187 (California, 1994), DREAM Act debates, border militarization, Latino population growth, demographic change
- **Key terms**: undocumented immigration, IRCA, DREAM Act, border enforcement, Proposition 187, nativism, demographic change, refugee policy, Sunbelt growth
- **Source reference**: CED Period 9 Key Concept 9.1

### Topic 9.6: Challenges of the 21st Century
- **Thematic focus**: WOR, PCE, SOC, WXT, NAT
- **Content**:
  - September 11, 2001 terrorist attacks: Al-Qaeda attacks killed ~3,000; transformed U.S. policy.
  - War on Terror: Afghanistan War (2001–present), Iraq War (2003–2011); USA PATRIOT Act; Department of Homeland Security; Abu Ghraib and debates over torture.
  - Great Recession (2008): financial crisis; bank bailouts (TARP); Obama stimulus.
  - Obama presidency (2009–2017): first African American president; Affordable Care Act (2010); election significance.
  - Deepening political polarization; Tea Party movement; social media's impact on politics.
  - Black Lives Matter movement and police violence debates.
  - Climate change as a policy challenge; debates over government's role.
- **Illustrative examples**: September 11, 2001, USA PATRIOT Act, Iraq War (WMD debate), Abu Ghraib, Hurricane Katrina (2005), Great Recession (2008), TARP, Barack Obama's election (2008), Affordable Care Act (2010), Tea Party, Black Lives Matter
- **Key terms**: September 11, War on Terror, USA PATRIOT Act, Afghanistan War, Iraq War, WMDs, Great Recession, TARP, Barack Obama, Affordable Care Act, Tea Party, Black Lives Matter, climate change, political polarization
- **Source reference**: CED Period 9 Key Concept 9.3

### Topic 9.7: Causation in Period 9
- **Skill focus**: Causation — evaluate the causes and effects of the conservative resurgence, end of the Cold War, and challenges of the early 21st century.

# KEY NOTES FOR DECK GENERATION

## Scope and Coverage
- **Total topics**: 9 units, ~89 topics (including skill-focused contextualizing and reasoning topics)
- **Testable content topics**: approximately 72 (excluding contextualizing and reasoning practice topics)
- **Exam weight distribution**: Units 3–8 each carry 10–17%; Units 1, 2, and 9 carry lower weight (4–8%)
- **Overlapping periods**: Intentional — Period 4 ends 1848, Period 5 begins 1844; Period 6 ends 1898, Period 7 begins 1890. Facts in the overlap zones belong to the unit that best contextualizes them.

## On Skill Topics (X.1 Contextualizing, X.7/X.8/X.12/X.13/X.14/X.15 Reasoning)
- These are framework/skill topics — they do not generate standalone content facts
- They may be referenced in chain themes (e.g., "Causation in Period 1" as a chain that links events from that unit)
- Do not generate facts with quizQuestion about "what is causation" — that is meta-curriculum, not history content

## CED Philosophy: No Prescribed Specifics for Most Topics
- The CED intentionally avoids prescribing specific people, events, or dates for most topics
- Our deck must fill in the canonical examples that are universally taught and appear on actual exams
- The "Illustrative examples" and "Key terms" fields above represent that judgment, grounded in standard APUSH pedagogy
- Every fact must still be sourced from verified references — the CED topic structure is the scope scaffold, not a fact source

## Deck Architecture Recommendations
- **9 unit sub-decks** mapping to Periods 1–9
- **Chain themes** should include: era-specific political leaders, key legislation/documents, turning-point events, social movements, economic systems, foreign policy events
- **Minimum chain themes per unit**: 8+ for larger units (3–8), 4+ for smaller units (1, 2, 9)
- **Fact types to include**: people/roles, events/dates, documents/legislation, cause-effect, significance/impact, vocabulary/terms
- **Answer pool homogeneity**: Keep pools domain-specific (e.g., separate pools for legislation, battles, political figures)
- **Exam weighting**: Units 3–8 should have proportionally more facts given their 10–17% exam weight
