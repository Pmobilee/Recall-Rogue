# Recall Rogue: comprehensive game design research

**The most successful mobile roguelites share a single design truth: every second must deliver either a reward, a meaningful decision, or visible progress toward both.** Recall Rogue's fusion of card roguelite progression and disguised spaced repetition is an ambitious design that has no direct precedent — but each individual component has been validated by games generating billions in revenue and millions of hours of play. This report distills actionable findings across seven research areas, grounded in specific data, developer interviews, academic research, and community sentiment. The core challenge isn't making the education work — it's making the game so addictive that players don't notice they're learning.

---

## 1. What makes mobile roguelites impossible to put down

The replayability of top mobile roguelites stems from **combinatorial variety**, not content volume. Slay the Spire's 4 characters each have ~75 cards plus 150+ relics, creating a design space so large that virtually no two runs are identical. Vampire Survivors' 37+ weapon evolution combinations (specific weapon + passive item = evolved form) turn every run into a discovery experiment. Dead Cells' "widening pool" mechanic — spending Cells between runs to permanently unlock weapons that then appear randomly in future runs — means the game literally becomes more varied the more you play it.

**Session length is the make-or-break factor for mobile.** Average mobile game sessions run ~3.5 minutes on iOS, but mid-core players will sustain 10–20 minutes. Vampire Survivors brilliantly hard-caps runs at 30 minutes (Death spawns to end the run), giving players a predictable maximum time commitment. Early deaths naturally create 5–10 minute "quick" sessions, while skilled play extends to the full 30. Archero chapters take 5–15 minutes. Downwell runs last 2–5 minutes for beginners, up to 10 for experts. **The 8-minute quick / 20-minute deep target for Recall Rogue aligns perfectly with the proven sweet spot.** Implement this through difficulty-as-filter (early runs end in death quickly, extending as players improve) combined with a hard session cap.

Reward pacing demands **layered frequency across multiple timescales**. Vampire Survivors delivers XP gems from every single enemy kill (micro-reward every 1–2 seconds), level-ups every 30–90 seconds with a meaningful weapon/item choice, and weapon evolution discoveries every few minutes. Archero layers room-clear progress indicators, level-up ability choices every 30–45 seconds, and angel/devil encounters at variable intervals. The research consensus is that **no more than 15–20 seconds should pass without some form of feedback** (visual, audio, progress indicator, or reward). The most successful casual games average 5–10 seconds between meaningful events. For Recall Rogue's target of "something interesting every 10–15 seconds," layer micro-rewards (particle effects from card plays, damage numbers, combo flashes) with mid-frequency rewards (card rewards, relics, floor progression) and macro-rewards (rare relic discoveries, boss encounters, act completions).

### The "one more run" formula has five specific ingredients

The compulsion to restart isn't mystical — it's engineered from five concrete mechanics. **First, meta-progression that makes every run productive**: Hades' Mirror of Night (12+ upgrade categories persisting through death), Rogue Legacy's castle upgrades, and Dead Cells' permanent weapon unlocks ensure that even a 2-minute death generates tangible progress. **Second, near-miss frequency around 30%**: research shows a near-miss rate of approximately 30% maximizes desire to continue playing, and roguelites naturally produce this through procedural difficulty variation. **Third, build theorycrafting**: players who die thinking "what if I'd taken that poison card instead?" are already planning their next run. **Fourth, near-zero restart friction**: Vampire Survivors' instant restart is critical — the formula is `restart_friction < perceived_reward_potential`. **Fifth, an expanding discovery horizon**: the Binding of Isaac starts with half its item pool locked, so the more you play, the more there is left to find.

The descent decision — push deeper vs. play it safe — works through **visible escalation indicators combined with time pressure**. Risk of Rain's difficulty scales continuously with time (the formula compounds exponentially: `playerFactor × stagesCompleted^1.15 × 0.0506 × difficultyValue × timeInMinutes`), forcing players to balance thorough exploration against the ticking clock. Downwell's combo system (8/16/24 kill chains without touching ground) creates self-imposed risk for skilled players. Spelunky's ghost timer (invincible ghost spawns after 2.5 minutes per level) physically prevents overly cautious play. Recall Rogue's dungeon descent creates this same tension — push deeper for better relics and card rewards, but earlier floors are safer. Enemy escalation at each act mirrors the "difficulty increases faster than reward" curve.

## 2. Dopamine architecture that keeps players tapping

The most effective dopamine loops in 2023–2026 mobile hits operate on **simultaneous multi-track reward systems**. Monopoly GO (~$2.4B+ lifetime revenue) runs 2–5 events concurrently, each with its own reward track, ensuring something is always about to trigger. Royal Match's "Butler's Gift" streak system awards escalating free boosters for consecutive wins (up to Rocket + Propeller + TNT + Light Ball), resetting on failure — creating win-streak dopamine that boosted revenue by **17%** when cooperative events were added. The core principle remains B.F. Skinner's variable ratio reinforcement: rewards after unpredictable intervals produce the highest response rates and the most extinction-resistant behavior.

### Vampire Survivors' creator weaponized slot machine psychology

Luca Galante spent most of his pre-VS career making mobile slot machine games. He's stated: *"Slot games are very simple. All the player has to do is press one button, and the game designers have to find a way to push the player to press that button."* His key creative takeaway was specifically **chest-opening animations** — one slot game had "a really good jingle when a treasure chest opened." VS's chest ceremony uses a slot-reel animation where potential rewards cycle past like a slot machine, a gold counter that ticks upward building anticipation, audio surges timed to the reveal, and variable reward tiers (standard = 1 item; rare = 3; ultra-rare "jackpot" = 5 with intensified effects). Players routinely refuse to skip the animation. Every run, regardless of outcome, yields gold for permanent upgrades — the 30-minute time limit means players frequently die in the 25–29 minute range, creating intense near-miss motivation.

**The optimal loot reveal ceremony follows a 4-phase arc.** Phase 1: Build-up (1–3 seconds) — glowing cracks, rarity-indicating aura, rumble/shake. Phase 2: Reveal moment (0.5–2 seconds) — explosive light burst, camera zoom, distinct audio sting. Phase 3: Display/celebration (2–5 seconds) — item showcase, rarity-appropriate effects, stats fade-in. Phase 4: Reward integration (1–2 seconds) — item flies into inventory, counter animates upward. Genshin Impact's wish animation color-codes the shooting star (purple = 4★, gold = 5★) before the reveal, building tension during the animation itself. For Recall Rogue's relic and card reward reveals, target **3–8 seconds for standard reveals, 8–15 seconds for rare/legendary**, with rarity-differentiated visual language at every tier.

### Card combat satisfaction requires synchronized multi-sensory feedback

Drawing from Steve Swink's "Game Feel" framework and Vlambeer's "Art of Screenshake" methodology, satisfying card combat requires layering **visual** (damage numbers arcing to enemy, brief enemy knockback, screen flash on correct answer, particle bursts from card plays), **audio** (crisp impact sound on correct answer, soft tone on wrong, chain connection sounds, combo escalation audio), and **haptic** feedback (iOS UIImpactFeedbackGenerator.heavy on correct, .light on card tap, synchronized within 20–75ms of visual/audio per CHI 2023 research). The critical rule: feedback intensity must be **proportional to action significance** — Quick Play ≠ Charged play ≠ perfect 5-chain turn.

Preventing dead zones (30+ seconds with no reward) requires running 3–5 simultaneous reward tracks: per-card rewards (damage numbers, enemy react), streak rewards (combo counter escalating with each correct answer), turn-structure rewards (Surge turn announcement every 3rd turn), floor progress (visible HP bar depletion, depth counter), and meta-rewards (deck synergies clicking into place). **Always show the next goal** — progress bars, enemy HP, combo counter, and floor depth should be perpetually visible. Near-misses should be made explicit: show enemy HP at low percentages explicitly, show combo at 4/5 with clear "one more" visual.

## 3. Making education invisible inside addictive gameplay

The most powerful finding in learning game research is that **the games that teach best never market as educational**. Kerbal Space Program teaches orbital mechanics through gameplay that punishes failure with education — you *must* understand thrust-to-weight ratios and orbital maneuvers to succeed, but the physics is experienced, not explained. NASA officially endorses it. Assassin's Creed Discovery Tour tested within **7% of lecture-taught students** on history content. Civilization teaches historiography — how history works through procedural consequences — not just facts. The common thread: **learning IS the core mechanic, not a layer on top of gameplay.** The term for this is "stealth learning" (Sharp, 2012), and research shows players learn more from games not labeled "educational" because they avoid a biased mindset.

### Duolingo's retention mechanics provide the blueprint for habit formation

Duolingo's 128M+ monthly active users and $500M+ annual revenue rest on a few specific mechanics with measured effects. **Streaks** are the most powerful: users maintaining a 7-day streak are **3.6× more likely** to stay engaged long-term. Streak Freeze reduced churn by **21%** for at-risk users. The iOS widget displaying streaks increased user commitment by **60%**. **Leaderboards** drive users to complete **40% more lessons per week**, and introducing leagues increased lesson completion by **25%**. The genius of the core action is that it takes **under 3 minutes** — low enough friction that even low-motivation days produce engagement. Critically, Duolingo pushes sign-up *until after* a test lesson — this single change produced a **20% jump** in next-day retention.

Duolingo developed **Half-Life Regression (HLR)**, a proprietary spaced repetition model trained on 13 million learning traces that cut prediction error to nearly half of the standard Leitner system's error rate. A/B tests showed HLR produced a **9.5% increase** in retention for practice sessions and a **12% increase** in overall activity. For Recall Rogue, the key insight is that skill "strength meters" that decay over time visually prompt practice at optimal intervals — **the algorithm is invisible; players just see "this skill needs practice."**

### Disguising SM-2 as a game mechanic requires careful design

Academic research by Schimanke et al. (2013, 2014) identified a critical tension: **when games are too engaging, players play multiple rounds in a session, which compromises spaced repetition scheduling** (intervals become too short). Their solution was an auxiliary algorithm that mimics SM-2's ranking concept in a round-based manner without corrupting the spacing schedule. For Recall Rogue, this means using different question content across sessions within the same play period, or switching to different game modes for extra rounds.

Practical disguise strategies that work: frame review content as revisiting familiar encounters with known enemies, make boss fights require knowledge from earlier content, use rest site and shop quizzes to review earlier material for rewards, and make card mastery tiers visible as tangible power upgrades that "need charging" through correct answers. Research from eLearning Industry (50,000+ learners) found combining spaced repetition with gamification produces **3× better retention** than either alone, with completion rates jumping from 18% to 72%. The critical design: make delayed rewards **3–5× larger** than immediate ones, so the biggest point bonuses come not at initial learning but at 7-day and 30-day reviews.

### The 85% accuracy rule governs optimal difficulty

Wilson et al. (2019) in *Nature Communications* mathematically derived that the **optimal error rate for learning is ~15.87%** (approximately 85% accuracy). This applies to humans, animals, and artificial neural networks. When tasks are too easy (100% accuracy), there's nothing left to learn. When too hard (~50% accuracy), learning stalls. Duolingo's default difficulty is actually calibrated too high (~95%+ accuracy) — users who enable hard mode learn faster. For Recall Rogue, target **80–85% correct answer rate** through adaptive difficulty, and resist the temptation to make questions too easy. Robert and Elizabeth Bjork's "desirable difficulties" framework (1994–2020) shows that conditions that slow the rate of apparent learning actually optimize long-term retention — the best learning *feels* harder during practice.

**Quiz frequency research** from task interruption studies (Adamczyk & Bailey, 2004) found that interruptions at **subtask boundaries** (low mental workload moments) cause significantly less annoyance than mid-task interruptions. For Recall Rogue, this means quiz questions must appear at natural transition points: at the player's deliberate Charge action, during boss quiz phases, at rest sites — never as forced interruptions mid-turn. Keep questions under 15 seconds interaction time, make content contextually relevant to current gameplay, and give players agency (discoverable knowledge triggers vs. forced interruptions).

## 4. Monetization that respects players and still generates revenue

Every beloved mobile roguelite — Vampire Survivors (free, ad-supported only), Slay the Spire ($10 premium), Dead Cells ($8.99 premium) — chose non-predatory approaches. None use energy systems, loot boxes, or progression-gating. However, **Survivor.io (a Vampire Survivors clone with traditional F2P monetization) made $170M+ in IAP revenue** with 55M downloads — dramatically more than VS's ad-only approach. This illustrates the fundamental trade-off: aggressive monetization vastly outperforms ethical models in raw revenue, but at the cost of player goodwill and brand integrity.

### Energy systems are tolerated only under very specific conditions

Player sentiment on energy systems is overwhelmingly negative in Western markets and in action/roguelite genres specifically. Reddit communities consistently describe them as "the worst part of freemium design" with forum posts titled "Warning! Game has a stamina system like mobile games!" However, energy systems generate approximately **one-third of total bookings** in casual F2P games — Candy Crush's extra lives for $0.99 is reportedly its #1 conversion driver. The "first session always free" model is the **most palatable version** of energy systems because it guarantees a complete gameplay experience on every return. Critical implementation details: frame any gate as narrative (a "life support budget"), not gamification (an energy timer); allow the resource to be earned through good play, not just time or payment; ensure casual players rarely hit the wall (3–4 sessions per day at minimum via regeneration); and always offer an ad-based alternative. World of Warcraft's famous reframe — switching "XP penalty" to "rested XP bonus" (identical mechanic, different framing) — turned player hatred into acceptance.

### The $4.99/month subscription model is well-validated

Mobile game subscriptions are growing rapidly: App Store subscriptions rose 73% from 2019–2021, Google Play up 152%. Clash of Clans' Gold Pass at $4.99/month saw revenue climb **145% in launch week** and became the game's 4th best-selling offer. Hay Day's Farm Pass increased player spending by **62% month-over-month**. Sensor Tower reports that **time-saving subscriptions have higher retention and lower churn** than content-locked subscriptions. The $4.99 price point hits the sweet spot of perceived value. **29% of mobile game IAP spenders would willingly pay for an ad-free experience alone.** Bundle ad removal, bonus cosmetics, and exclusive visual effects into a subscription pass — convenience and aesthetics, never content gating.

**Cosmetic-only revenue is insufficient as the sole model for a single-player indie roguelite.** Research shows 47% of players will buy cosmetics only after spending 30+ hours in a game, and cosmetics require multiplayer/social visibility to drive purchase motivation. The recommended monetization stack: free base game (essential for mobile discoverability), subscription pass at $4.99/month (primary revenue driver), cosmetic purchases ($1–$5 each), optional rewarded ads (VS model — watch ad for bonus at run end), and a one-time premium unlock ($5–$10) for players who prefer paying once. Battle passes drive **1–40% of total revenue** depending on integration depth, with over 60% of top Western mobile games now using them. Optimal duration for casual games is 2–4 weeks.

## 5. Auto-battle that feels like playing, not watching

The engagement spectrum from fully manual (Soul Knight) to mostly auto (AFK Arena) reveals that **the sweet spot for mobile is "punctuated interactivity"**: weapons fire automatically, but movement and key decisions remain player-controlled. Archero pioneered this — the character auto-attacks when stationary, with the player controlling only movement, creating an elegant tension between dodging and dealing damage. This one-thumb control attracted both casual and core players, generating **$35M+ in IAP revenue in its first 3 months** from an 11-person team.

**The key to engaging auto-battle is that preparation matters more than execution.** AFK Arena ($1B+ lifetime revenue) makes team composition, formation, positioning, and one manual ultimate ability activation per fight the player's primary inputs — the auto-combat simply tests whether your strategic setup works. The Game Developer article on autobattlers found that "it is the risk of failure and defeat that makes the players' choices meaningful" — auto-battle games that maintain genuine fail states stay engaging, while those where spending money guarantees victory become boring.

For quiz/decision popups mid-combat, **pause on reward moments, not arbitrary points**. Vampire Survivors pauses completely on level-up, presenting 3–4 choices with brief post-selection invulnerability. Archero layers level-up ability choices (pick 1 of 3), angel stages (heal vs. power), and devil encounters (powerful ability at 20% max HP cost). Slay the Spire's post-combat card reward (choose 1 of 3 or skip) proves that the option to decline is itself a powerful decision. No major games have successfully embedded trivia questions into real-time action combat — **this is an underexplored design space.** The recommendation: frame knowledge-testing as strategic choices organic to the game world (Charging a card IS the decision — the quiz is inseparable from the combat action) rather than disconnected trivia popups.

**The 30–60 second encounter target is ideal for mobile.** Archero rooms take 15–45 seconds, AFK Arena battles 30–90 seconds, and mobile session data shows total sessions of 4–5 minutes. With 30–60 second encounters plus 5–10 second decision popups between them, a session looks like: Fight (45s) → Choose (8s) → Fight (45s) → Choose (8s) → Fight (45s) → Boss (60s) = ~3.5 minutes. This fits mobile perfectly.

For connecting knowledge and combat, the Charge mechanic achieves the ideal template: Quick Play is always safe but weaker, Charging costs AP but multiplies damage — making knowledge mastery directly translate to combat power. The "risk vs. reward" tension is built into every card play, creating engagement hooks on every single turn. Recall Rogue should ensure **knowledge-power dependency**: correct answers generate more damage, relic synergies reward strategic thinking, and pure combat skill without knowledge will ceiling out at deeper floors.

## 6. What communities actually say about mobile roguelites

Reddit communities reveal a strikingly consistent set of priorities. **Short, digestible runs are non-negotiable**: "One of the greatest strengths behind roguelikes is their digestibility... you can pick it up real quick, do a run or two, then put it back down." Every top-recommended mobile roguelite on r/AndroidGaming and roguelite-focused sites (Dead Cells, Slay the Spire, Downwell, Balatro) is a **premium purchase, not F2P with energy systems.** This is a clear market signal.

**Meaningful meta-progression is the #1 requested feature**: "I love roguelites because even if I die I still made progress. I just don't have the free time anymore to play a game over and over with no progress." However, mandatory early-game grind gates are despised: "Nearly impossible to beat game that you have to dive in, feel bad, die, die, die again, literally doing runs not to beat the game but FARM to get unlocks." Dead Cells' approach (easy at beginning, escalating difficulty through Boss Cell modes) is consistently praised over Hades' approach (mandatory early deaths to unlock basic tools).

**Educational games face a near-universal perception problem.** Players describe them as "animated versions of homework" and "a thinly veiled reskin of curriculum." The analysis from MediaResonance is blunt: "Many educational games are bad because they are not games at all. They are gamification of school." The "educational" label itself is toxic — NPR reported that players learned more from Assassin's Creed (not marketed as educational) than from explicitly educational games. **Recall Rogue must never market as educational.** The learning should be invisible in marketing and near-invisible in gameplay.

### Retention benchmarks define success thresholds

Industry data from GameAnalytics (2025, 10,000+ projects, 2.7B MAU) establishes these benchmarks:

- **D1 retention**: Median 22.91%, top 25% at 26–28%, 90th percentile by revenue at **35.4%**
- **D7 retention**: Median 3.42–3.94%, 90th percentile by revenue at **14.3%**
- **D28 retention**: Median 0.85%, 90th percentile by revenue at **6.1%**

Aspirational targets for a well-performing indie game: **D1 >30%, D7 >10%, D30 >5%**. The D1→D7 cliff is where most games die. Key insight: "Usually somewhere in the mid D20s a game's retention curve becomes flat — D30 retention usually looks a lot like D365 retention," making D30 the key predictor of long-term health. iOS consistently outperforms Android on retention by 4–6 percentage points.

**The biggest indie mobile game mistakes** identified across developer communities: neglecting marketing until launch ("Marketing your game is your job... it's every bit as hard as programming"), scope creep destroying core loop clarity ("What began as a three-month project becomes an eighteen-month nightmare"), no analytics integration (one developer: "We didn't have a notification system or progress reminder to bring players back. Once they stopped, they were gone for good"), and building for the wrong platform/genre fit.

## 7. Research answers to specific quiz design questions

### Research says 3 answer choices, but 4 matches player expectations

Rodriguez's 2005 meta-analysis of **80 years of multiple-choice research** found definitively that **3 options are optimal**: most 4th and 5th distractors are "nonfunctional" (selected by <5% of test-takers), 3-option items maintain equivalent discrimination and reliability, and students answer 3-option questions **~5 seconds faster** (36 vs. 41 seconds). However, every major mobile quiz game (Trivia Crack, QuizUp, Who Wants to Be a Millionaire) uses 4 options, matching player expectations.

**Recommendation**: Use **3 options by default** for speed and flow (each question should resolve in under 10 seconds), with an optional "hard mode" lifeline mechanic that adds a 4th option for bonus rewards. This gets the best of both worlds: research-optimal learning speed in normal play, with the familiar 4-option format available as a rewarding challenge variant.

### Showing the correct answer on wrong guess is research-validated

Multiple meta-analyses support this approach. Bangert-Drowns et al. (1991) found immediate feedback with correct answer display is "generally more effective than delayed feedback for complex learning." A 2021 *Frontiers in Psychology* study on mobile quiz apps confirmed that immediate feedback had "similar positive effects on quiz performance and response certainty in the short and long term." Duolingo uses exactly this pattern: red highlight on wrong answer, correct answer displayed, "Continue" button (no auto-advance).

**Recommendation**: Keep the current approach with refinements. Show correct answer highlighted in **green** with wrong answer in **red**. Enforce a **minimum 1-second display** before allowing the tap-continue (prevents accidental skip-through). Keep text to "Correct: [answer]" only — save detailed explanations for an optional post-run review screen. Track wrong answers for spaced repetition re-quizzing. Don't over-penalize wrong answers in gameplay; the learning moment IS the consequence.

### Run failure should feel productive, not punishing

Most roguelites are binary: complete the run (keep everything) or die (lose everything). **Kahneman and Tversky's Prospect Theory** (1979) shows losses are psychologically ~2× as painful as equivalent gains. A loss framed as "lost X%" feels worse than the equivalent framed as "kept Y%." A CHI PLAY 2020 paper confirmed loss aversion exists in game contexts.

**Recommendation**: Ensure meta-progression makes every run feel productive even on failure. SM-2 updates persist across runs (learning is never lost). Gold earned carries over. Relics and cards do not persist between runs (standard roguelite expectation — this is correct). Frame the run-end screen in positive terms: facts mastered, accuracy achieved, floors reached — not "you died at floor 3." For comparison: Hades retains 0% of run loot but meta-currencies persist, making every death feel productive. Dead Cells' Cells system gives meaningful permanent progress even from early deaths. Recall Rogue's strongest retention mechanic is SM-2 itself — players literally get better at the content through repeated runs, which is a more meaningful progression than any meta-currency.

## Conclusion: the surprising insights that should shape Recall Rogue

Three counterintuitive findings stand out across this research. **First, the best learning feels worse during practice.** Bjork's desirable difficulties research proves that conditions slowing apparent learning actually optimize long-term retention — Recall Rogue should resist making questions easy to preserve player comfort. Target 85% accuracy, not 95%. **Second, energy systems are more dangerous in roguelites than in any other genre.** Every beloved mobile roguelite uses premium or ad-only monetization. The roguelite community specifically expects unlimited runs. Any energy/gate system risks alienating the core audience — if implemented, do so with extreme generosity. **Third, Schimanke's research reveals that making a learning game too fun actually breaks the spaced repetition algorithm** by compressing intervals. Recall Rogue needs session-boundary awareness: serve different question content if players chain multiple runs, or use varied game modes for extended sessions.

The path forward is clear: build the most addictive card roguelite possible first, test and validate its retention without any educational content, then layer in the spaced repetition system so seamlessly that players experience quiz moments as a natural part of combat strategy — the Charge mechanic achieves exactly this. The education must be earned by the gameplay, never the reverse.
